# arXiv:2603.08940

## The Origin of the Red Sequence: Environmental Quenching vs. Mass Quenching

**Authors:** D. J. Wilman, A. M. Hopkins, P. J. Francis, J. S. B. Wyithe, A. J. Bunker, A. J. Connolly, P. C. Hewett, S. M. Croom, M. A. Ferreras, B. P. Venemans, et al.

**Published:** 2026-03-11

**Categories:** astro-ph.GA

**DOI:** 10.48550/arXiv.2603.08940

---

## Abstract

We disentangle the roles of environment and mass in galaxy quenching using a sample of 50,000 galaxies from the GAMA survey. We find that both mass and environment contribute to quenching, but their relative importance varies with redshift. At z~0.1, mass quenching dominates for M* > 10^10.5 M☉, while environmental quenching becomes important at lower masses and in dense environments. The fraction of quenched galaxies in clusters is 40% higher than in the field at fixed mass, indicating that environment provides an additional quenching channel beyond mass-driven processes.

---

## Key Findings

- **50,000 galaxies** from GAMA survey
- **Mass quenching**: dominates for M* > 10^10.5 M☉ at z~0.1
- **Environmental quenching**: important at lower masses and in dense environments
- **Cluster vs. field**: 40% higher quenched fraction in clusters at fixed mass
- **Environment as additional channel**: beyond mass-driven processes

## Methods

- GAMA survey spectroscopy and photometry
- Environment classification (field/group/cluster)
- Quenched/star-forming classification
- Mass and environment dependence analysis
- Redshift evolution (limited to z~0.1)

## Relevance to Galaxy Evolution

Understanding the origin of the red sequence requires disentangling mass and environmental effects. This work shows both contribute, with environment providing an additional quenching mechanism.
