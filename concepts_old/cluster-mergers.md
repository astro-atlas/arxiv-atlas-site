Concept: Cluster mergers

Summary:
Major mergers of galaxy clusters are collisions between massive dark-matter halos that bring together intracluster gas, galaxies, and dark matter at relative velocities of 1000s km/s. These events reshape cluster morphology, drive shocks and cold fronts in the X-ray-emitting intracluster medium (ICM), and can produce spatial offsets between collisional gas and collisionless components (galaxies, dark matter). Observationally notable examples include the Bullet cluster (1E 0657-56) and El Gordo (ACT-CL J0102-4915).

Why it matters:
- Testbed for dark matter properties: offsets between gas and galaxies/dark matter constrain self-interaction cross-sections.
- Galaxy evolution in dense environments: mergers can trigger/shut down star formation, drive morphological transformations via ram pressure, tidal interactions.
- Cluster mass assembly and cosmology: frequency of very massive mergers probes structure formation and extreme tail of mass function.

Key observables and techniques:
- X-ray imaging and spectroscopy (Chandra, XMM-Newton, eROSITA): detect shocks, cold fronts, centroid offsets, temperature structure.
- Optical imaging and spectroscopy: galaxy spatial distribution, redshift/velocity structure, lensing mass maps.
- Weak/strong gravitational lensing: map total mass and compare with baryonic tracers.
- Radio observations: detect radio relics and halos associated with merger shocks and turbulence.

Important diagnostics:
- Line-of-sight velocity distribution of member galaxies — substructure tests (Dressler–Schectman, KMM, KDE) identify multiple components.
- Velocity dispersion → dynamical mass estimates (with caution in non-virialized systems).
- X-ray — galaxy centroid offsets quantify collisional vs collisionless response.
- Shock Mach number and temperature jumps constrain merger speed and geometry.

Representative papers and resources:
- 2603.10849 (Peanut cluster) — spectroscopic redshifts and evidence for near-pericenter massive merger.
- Randall et al. (2008) — Bullet cluster constraints on dark matter.
- Menanteau et al. (2012), Jee et al. (2014) — analyses of El Gordo.

Open questions:
- How common are near-pericenter massive mergers at z~0.4–0.5?
- What are robust methods to measure merger geometry (impact parameter, line-of-sight component) from combined X-ray, optical, and lensing data?
- Quantitative limits on dark matter self-interaction from systems with ambiguous substructure signatures.
