High-velocity clouds (HVCs)

Definition:
- HVCs are neutral hydrogen (HI) structures observed in 21 cm emission with line-of-sight velocities (v_lsr) that deviate strongly from Galactic rotation. They are commonly defined by |v_lsr| > 90 km/s; very high-velocity clouds (VHVCs) exceed |v_lsr| > 170 km/s.

Context and importance:
- HVCs populate the circumgalactic environment of the Milky Way and are candidate channels for metal-poor gas accretion that can sustain star formation in galactic disks.
- Their distances, metallicities, and origins have been uncertain because stellar tracers are rarely found within them.

Insights from He et al. (arXiv:2603.10607):
- Direct stellar detection in Complex H: discovery of two young open clusters (Emei-1 and Emei-2) physically associated with Southern Complex H (SCH), providing a stellar distance anchor at 13.8 ± 0.6 kpc.
- Measured metallicities for cluster member stars are subsolar (Z ≈ 0.04–0.06 Z_sun), consistent with low-metallicity HVC gas seen in absorption studies.
- Cold HI cores (e.g., C1) show narrow velocity dispersions (~2 km/s) and compact sizes; such cores may harbor shielded molecular gas enabling star formation despite low overall metallicity.
- HVC dynamics can include internal cloud-cloud collisions that trigger star formation; interactions with the Galactic disk produce characteristic velocity gradients ("slow-fast-slow" patterns) as outer layers decelerate and merge with disk gas.

Implications for observation and theory:
- Stellar detections in HVCs are rare because young stars escape their natal gas quickly (<~20 Myr); surveys should target very young populations and cold HI cores.
- Low metallicity reduces CO emissivity; searching for dense HI and alternative tracers (e.g., dust continuum, other molecules, deep targeted CO) may be more effective.
- HVC-born stars and clusters could contribute to halo populations and to some hypervelocity objects.
