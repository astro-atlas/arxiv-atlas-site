Dust-obscured Type-1 AGNs

Overview
- Type-1 AGNs are classically defined by the presence of broad emission lines in their optical spectra. A subset of these, however, show significant dust extinction from host-galaxy-scale dust (not only the torus), producing large Balmer decrements or suppressed UV/optical continua while still showing broad lines at longer wavelengths.

Why they matter
- They can hide significant accretion power from optical surveys, biasing luminosity functions, BH mass distributions, and Eddington ratio estimates.
- Understanding this population is important for galaxy–AGN co-evolution studies, especially when linking AGN activity to host properties like star formation and morphology.

Identification
- Large Balmer decrements (e.g., Hα/Hβ), red optical–MIR colors, or SED fits showing a reddened AGN continuum with MIR excess.
- Cross-matching MIR-selected samples with optical spectroscopy (e.g., NEP-Wide, WISE surveys) helps find them.

Correcting estimates
- Use extinction-insensitive tracers (MIR luminosities, Paschen/Brackett lines in IR) or SED-based extinction corrections to recover intrinsic Lbol and MBH.
