<p>
  <strong>Cosmic dawn</strong> refers to the epoch at z &asymp; 10&ndash;20 (cosmic ages ~150&ndash;500 Myr after the Big Bang) when the first galaxies assembled, produced the first heavy elements and <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a>, and began ionizing the neutral intergalactic medium. This period marks the transition from a dark, neutral universe to one illuminated by starlight and structured into the first gravitationally bound systems. JWST has opened a direct observational window into this epoch, revealing a surprisingly rich population of <a href="/pages/high-redshift-galaxies" class="wikilink">luminous galaxies</a><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="physical-conditions">Physical Conditions</h2>
<p>
  At cosmic dawn, the Universe is less than ~500 Myr old. The available time for stellar mass assembly, chemical enrichment, and dust production is severely limited. Standard cosmological parameters<a href="#ref3">[3]</a> give the age of the Universe as ~400 Myr at z ~ 11.5 and ~300 Myr at z ~ 14. Despite these short timescales, JWST observations reveal galaxies with stellar masses up to log(M<sub>*</sub>/M<sub>&odot;</sub>) ~ 9&ndash;10 and significant dust content, implying extremely efficient early assembly<a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="galaxy-assembly">Galaxy Assembly and Feedback</h2>
<p>
  Several theoretical frameworks compete to explain the rapid buildup of galaxies at cosmic dawn:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Radiative-feedback model (Ferrara+2024):</strong> Galaxies undergo alternating &ldquo;<a href="/pages/red-monsters" class="wikilink">red monster</a>&rdquo; (dust-obscured, rapid assembly) and &ldquo;<a href="/pages/blue-monsters" class="wikilink">blue monster</a>&rdquo; (UV-bright, dust-free) phases. During the red phase (~20% of lifetime), ~70% of stellar mass forms in a dust-enshrouded environment before radiation-driven outflows clear the dust<a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Feedback-free model (Dekel+2023):</strong> Early galaxies grow rapidly via cold gas inflows without significant feedback regulation, explaining high UV luminosities and stellar masses<a href="#ref7">[7]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Density-regulated SF (Somerville+2025):</strong> Star formation efficiency depends on local gas density, with semi-analytic models reproducing observed galaxy properties<a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="dust-and-metals">Dust and Metals at z &gt; 10</h2>
<p>
  The discovery of dust-reddened galaxies at cosmic dawn challenges models predicting slow <a href="/pages/grain-growth" class="wikilink">grain growth</a> through ISM accretion<a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. EGS-z11-R0 at z = 11.45 exhibits A<sub>V</sub> ~ 1 mag and detection of [Fe V] &lambda;4227, providing direct evidence that iron-peak elements &mdash; key constituents of <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust grains</a> &mdash; were already present in the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">ISM</a> at this epoch<a href="#ref5">[5]</a>. Iron enrichment at z &gt; 11 is consistent with prompt release from massive stars within ~10 Myr of formation<a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. These observations imply that dust production through stellar sources (supernova ejecta, AGB star winds) and rapid <a href="/pages/grain-growth" class="wikilink">grain growth</a> in dense environments may dominate over slow ISM accretion at the earliest times.
</p>
<h2 id="ionizing-sources">Ionizing Sources and AGN</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">Emission-line diagnostics</a> at cosmic dawn reveal hard ionizing radiation fields, but separating AGN from extreme stellar ionization remains challenging. UV diagnostic diagrams (C IV/C III] vs. C III]/He II ratios) consistently place z &gt; 10 galaxies like GN-z11<a href="#ref11">[11]</a>, GHZ9<a href="#ref12">[12]</a>, and EGS-z11-R0<a href="#ref5">[5]</a> in composite regions. The possibility of early black-hole growth through massive seed channels (direct-collapse black holes, primordial black holes) is consistent with the inferred AGN contributions and black-hole masses<a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>.
</p>
