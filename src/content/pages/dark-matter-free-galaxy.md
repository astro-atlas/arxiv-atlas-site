<h2 id="definition">Definition</h2>
<p>A <strong>dark-matter-free galaxy</strong> is a galaxy whose total dynamical mass &mdash; inferred from the gravitational motion of its stars or star clusters &mdash; is consistent with its stellar (and gas) mass alone, requiring no dark matter component <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. These galaxies are extreme outliers from the <a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">stellar mass&ndash;halo mass relation</a>, which predicts dark matter halos &gt;100&times; more massive than their stellar content for dwarf galaxies <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="observational-evidence">Observational Evidence</h2>

<h3>Foundational: Why galaxies should have dark matter</h3>
<p>In the standard &Lambda;CDM paradigm, galaxies form within dark matter halos through gas cooling and subsequent <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Low-mass dwarf galaxies are expected to be the most dark-matter-dominated systems, with halo masses exceeding stellar masses by factors of 100 or more <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. This makes the existence of dark-matter-free dwarfs deeply puzzling.</p>

<h3>Intermediate: The NGC 1052 system</h3>
<p>All three confirmed dark-matter-free galaxies reside in the NGC 1052 field and are <a href="/pages/ultra-diffuse-galaxy" class="wikilink">ultra-diffuse galaxies</a>:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>NGC 1052-DF2:</strong> &sigma; = 6.1<sup>+3.7</sup><sub>&minus;3.1</sub> km/s after stellar corrections; dynamical mass consistent with stellar mass <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></li>
  <li><strong>NGC 1052-DF4:</strong> &sigma; = 7.7<sup>+2.0</sup><sub>&minus;2.4</sub> km/s; similarly dark-matter-free <a href="#ref2">[2]</a></li>
  <li><strong>NGC 1052-DF9:</strong> &sigma; = 6.4<sup>+4.0</sup><sub>&minus;4.3</sub> km/s; third confirmation, validating the <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf prediction</a> <a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>All three fall below the universal baryon fraction (f<sub>b</sub> = &Omega;<sub>b</sub>/&Omega;<sub>m</sub> &asymp; 0.157), meaning any residual dark matter halo would require an unphysically high baryon fraction <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</p>

<h3>Specialist: Shared unusual properties</h3>
<p>Beyond dark matter deficiency, these galaxies share several anomalous properties: extremely luminous globular clusters <a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>, large effective radii for their luminosities, and nearly monochromatic globular cluster colors <a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. All are members of a linear trail of ~12 galaxies with kinematically connected velocities <a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="formation">Formation Mechanisms</h2>
<p>The leading explanation is the <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf scenario</a>: a high-speed collision between gas-rich galaxies that physically separates baryons from dark matter <a href="#ref14">[14]</a><a href="#ref15">[15]</a>. Alternative proposals include tidal dwarf formation <a href="#ref16">[16]</a>, quasar-driven gas expulsion <a href="#ref17">[17]</a>, and tidal stripping of infalling streams <a href="#ref18">[18]</a>, though none explain the full combination of observed properties <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</p>

<div class="dispute">
  <h4>Dispute: Survivability under tides</h4>
  <p><strong>Ogiya (2022)</strong> <a href="#ref19">[19]</a> argued that dark-matter-free galaxies would be tidally disrupted on short timescales in the NGC 1052 group environment.</p>
  <p><strong>Lee et al. (2024)</strong> <a href="#ref20">[20]</a> showed via dedicated simulations that trail galaxies can survive under the actual tidal field. The continued existence of DF9 with properties consistent with DF2/DF4 provides empirical support for survivability.</p>
</div>

<h2 id="implications">Implications for Dark Matter Physics</h2>
<p>Paradoxically, galaxies <em>lacking</em> dark matter provide strong evidence <em>for</em> dark matter as a distinct physical component. If dark matter were merely an artifact of modified gravity, it could not be separated from baryonic matter <a href="#ref21">[21]</a>. The physical separation observed in these systems &mdash; analogous to the Bullet Cluster at galaxy cluster scales <a href="#ref21">[21]</a> &mdash; demonstrates that dark matter behaves as a collisionless substance distinct from baryonic matter.</p>
