<p>
  <strong>Magnetic fields</strong> play a central role in regulating the <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> process, from controlling the fragmentation of <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a> to launching protostellar outflows. The key question is whether magnetic fields are strong enough to significantly delay or regulate <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravitational collapse</a>, or whether they are subdominant to <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> and gravity.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">B-Field Orientation Relative to Filaments</h2>
<p>
  Planck and Herschel observations reveal a systematic relationship between B-field orientation and gas density in <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filamentary clouds</a>: magnetic fields are predominantly <strong>perpendicular</strong> to high-<a href="/pages/column-density" class="wikilink">column-density</a> filaments (N(H₂) > 5×10²¹ cm⁻²) but <strong>parallel</strong> to low-density striations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>. This transition is reproduced in MHD simulations and reflects the dynamical importance of fields: in the strong-field regime, matter flows preferentially along field lines, forming filaments perpendicular to the field<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Mass-to-Flux Ratio and Magnetic Criticality</h2>
<p>
  The <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a> λ quantifies whether a cloud is magnetically subcritical (λ &lt; 1, B-field prevents collapse), supercritical (λ &gt; 1, gravity dominates), or transcritical (λ ~ 1). In the IRDC G351.77-0.53, λ ~ 0.9 ± 0.6 on cloud scales transitions to λ > 1 near the densest clump c1, consistent with magnetically regulated collapse<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Hourglass Morphology as Collapse Signature</h2>
<p>
  When a magnetized cloud core contracts under gravity, initially uniform field lines are dragged inward, producing a characteristic <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass-shaped B-field</a> morphology<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. This has been observed in several star-forming regions including OMC-1 (Orion)<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>, DR21<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>, and G351.77-0.53<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. The hourglass is a smoking-gun signature of magnetically regulated gravitational collapse.
</p>
<h2 id="section-3">Energy Budget</h2>
<p>
  In star-forming clouds, the relative contributions of magnetic (E_B = B²V/8π), gravitational (E_G = GM²/L), and kinetic (E_K = Mσ_V²) energies determine the dynamical state. In G351.77-0.53, all three energies are comparable (~10⁴⁷ ergs), confirming that B-fields are dynamically important<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-4">B-Field Studies in IRDCs</h2>
<p>
  Magnetic field observations in <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">IRDCs</a> — the earliest stages of massive star formation — remain relatively rare but are growing. Key results include:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>G11.11-0.12</strong> — SOFIA/HAWC+ reveals B-field structure influencing star formation<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G14.225-0.506</strong> — magnetically dominated with parallel inter-filament fields<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G28.37+0.07</strong> — complex B-field controlling fragmentation<sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G351.77-0.53</strong> — hourglass morphology and transcritical magnetization<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
