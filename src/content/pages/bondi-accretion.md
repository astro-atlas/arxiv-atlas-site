<p>
  <strong>Bondi accretion</strong> describes the spherically symmetric accretion of gas onto a compact object from a uniform medium at infinity. The <strong>Bondi radius</strong> r<sub>B</sub> = GM/c<sub>s</sub><sup>2</sup> defines the <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravitational</a> sphere of influence, within which gas is gravitationally captured and falls approximately in free fall <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. In AGN, the Bondi radius sets the outer boundary of the circumnuclear accretion environment and is a key scale for understanding the transition from galaxy-scale gas to accretion-flow-scale physics <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="theory">Theory</h2>
<p>
  The Bondi (1952) solution assumes a non-rotating, steady-state, spherically symmetric gas flow with adiabatic equation of state <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The accretion rate is:
</p>
<p>
  &#7744;<sub>B</sub> = 4&pi;&lambda; (GM)<sup>2</sup> &rho;<sub>&infin;</sub> / c<sub>s,&infin;</sub><sup>3</sup>
</p>
<p>
  where &lambda; is a dimensionless parameter depending on the adiabatic index, M is the central mass, &rho;<sub>&infin;</sub> and c<sub>s,&infin;</sub> are the density and sound speed at infinity <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Within the Bondi radius, gas falls at the free-fall velocity v &prop; r<sup>&minus;0.5</sup>, and for adiabatic accretion the electron density follows n<sub>e</sub> &prop; r<sup>&minus;1.5</sup> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="ngc1275">Application to NGC 1275</h2>
<p>
  For NGC 1275 (the host galaxy of <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84</a>), the adopted black hole mass M<sub>BH</sub> = 1.1&times;10<sup>9</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub> <a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a> and an ambient sound speed inferred from X-ray observations yield a Bondi radius of ~11.8 pc <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. Fujita &amp; Nagai (2016) used this framework to model the electron density and predict <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a> from the infalling gas <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Park et al. (2026) showed that the RM profile of 3C 84 from 1–15 pc follows RM &prop; r<sup>&minus;2.7</sup>, naturally explained by a circumnuclear medium filling the Bondi sphere with n<sub>e</sub> &prop; r<sup>&minus;1.5 to &minus;2</sup> and B &prop; r<sup>&minus;1.7 to &minus;2.2</sup> <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. This represents the first observational application of Bondi-sphere gas properties via spatially resolved RM.
</p>
<h2 id="limitations">Limitations of the Bondi Model</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Spherical symmetry:</strong> Real accretion involves rotation, jets, and outflows that break spherical symmetry <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Steady-state assumption:</strong> AGN feedback, jet-driven cavities, and cold gas precipitation can modulate the accretion rate on short timescales <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Multiphase medium:</strong> Cold gas filaments, <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a>, and hot ICM coexist within the Bondi sphere in Perseus <a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>, complicating the single-phase assumption</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">Magnetic fields</a>:</strong> Not included in the original Bondi solution but may affect the accretion rate and flow geometry <a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
</ul>
