<p>
  <strong>Shock waves</strong> are abrupt transitions in a fluid where pressure, density, temperature, and velocity change discontinuously across a thin boundary. In astrophysics, shocks are fundamental to energy dissipation, particle acceleration, and the dynamics of jets, supernova remnants, and large-scale structure. The connection between shocks and non-thermal particle populations was established through foundational theoretical work <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a> and has been central to understanding cosmic ray origins ever since <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="rankine-hugoniot">Rankine-Hugoniot Jump Conditions</h2>
<p>
  Shocks are governed by conservation of mass, momentum, and energy across the shock front. The <strong>Rankine-Hugoniot jump conditions</strong> relate upstream (pre-shock) and downstream (post-shock) quantities as a function of the upstream Mach number and the fluid's adiabatic index:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Compression ratio:</strong> The density jump ρ₂/ρ₁ depends on Mach number; for strong shocks with adiabatic index γ = 5/3, the maximum compression ratio is 4</li>
  <li><strong>Pressure jump:</strong> Post-shock pressure increases strongly with Mach number</li>
  <li><strong>Temperature jump:</strong> Post-shock gas is heated, with most kinetic energy converted to thermal energy</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="oblique-shocks">Oblique Shocks</h2>
<p>
  When the shock front is inclined to the upstream flow, the jump conditions apply only to the velocity component normal to the shock surface. This produces <strong>oblique shocks</strong> with reduced effective compression compared to head-on (perpendicular) shocks. Oblique shocks are ubiquitous in astrophysical jets, where <a href="/pages/recollimation-shocks" class="wikilink">recollimation shocks</a> form at high inclination to the jet axis <a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The physics of particle acceleration at oblique shocks differs from the perpendicular case: Kirk &amp; Heavens (1989) showed that the accelerated spectrum depends on the angle between the upstream <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> and shock normal <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Gieseler et al. (1999) incorporated cross-field diffusion effects <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. Bell (2011) discussed how guide-field geometry modifies acceleration efficiency <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>, and Kirk et al. (2023) and Reville et al. (2025) provided further theoretical developments <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="dsa">Diffusive Shock Acceleration</h2>
<p>
  Charged particles gain energy at shocks through <a href="/pages/diffusive-shock-acceleration" class="wikilink">diffusive shock acceleration (DSA)</a>: repeated cycles of scattering across the shock front via magnetic <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>, gaining energy at each crossing <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. The resulting energy spectrum is a power law whose index depends on the compression ratio <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The maximum energy achievable depends on the shock size, magnetic field, and <a href="/pages/turbulence-and-diffusion" class="wikilink">diffusion regime</a>. Hillas (1984) provided an order-of-magnitude confinement criterion: E_max scales with the product of acceleration region size and magnetic field strength <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. The diffusion coefficient — governed by magnetic turbulence properties — determines how efficiently particles are confined and accelerated <a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. Three commonly used regimes are Bohm (most efficient), Kraichnan (intermediate), and Kolmogorov (least efficient) <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="shocks-in-jets">Shocks in Astrophysical Jets</h2>
<p>
  Jets host a variety of shock types. Early theoretical work by Blandford &amp; Rees (1974) and Scheuer (1974) linked shocks to radio emission in extragalactic sources <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. Subsequent work identified specific shock classes:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Propagating (internal) shocks:</strong> Studied by Marscher &amp; Gear (1985) and Daly &amp; Marscher (1988) as drivers of radio variability <a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>. Gómez et al. (1995, 1997, 2000) modeled superluminal motion and standing-shock patterns <a href="#ref14">[14]</a><a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Standing shocks:</strong> Komissarov &amp; Falle (1997) distinguished standing from propagating shocks in relativistic flows <a href="#ref17">[17]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/recollimation-shocks" class="wikilink">Recollimation shocks</a>:</strong> Oblique standing shocks formed by cocoon confinement <a href="#ref18">[18]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Termination shocks:</strong> Where the jet decelerates at its working surface; related transport methods were developed by Morlino et al. (2021) for wind termination shocks <a href="#ref20">[20]</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  Kaiser &amp; Alexander (1997), Ouyed &amp; Pudritz (1999), and Micono et al. (1999) provided analytic treatments and simulations constraining jet stability and electron acceleration at non-relativistic shocks <a href="#ref21">[21]</a><a href="#ref22">[22]</a><a href="#ref23">[23]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="observational-signatures">Observational Signatures</h2>
<p>
  Particle acceleration at shocks produces non-thermal radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Radio synchrotron:</strong> From relativistic electrons in magnetic fields; observed in jets of all classes <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></li>
  <li><strong>X-ray emission:</strong> From inverse Compton scattering or thermal plasma heating at shock fronts</li>
  <li><strong>Gamma-ray emission:</strong> Recent detections by HAWC <a href="#ref24">[24]</a>, H.E.S.S. <a href="#ref25">[25]</a>, and LHAASO <a href="#ref26">[26]</a> have revealed TeV/PeV sources consistent with shock acceleration in microquasar jets</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="open-questions">Open Questions</h2>
<ul>
  <li>What determines the injection efficiency — the fraction of thermal particles that enter the acceleration process? <a href="#ref2">[2]</a></li>
  <li>How do magnetic fields self-organize at shocks, and what turbulence spectrum results? <a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></li>
  <li>How do guide fields and shock obliquity jointly affect acceleration efficiency? <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
  <li>Can alternative diffusion mechanisms (e.g., mirror diffusion; Lazarian 2021, Barreto-Mota 2025) significantly change maximum energies? <a href="#ref27">[27]</a><a href="#ref28">[28]</a></li>
</ul>
