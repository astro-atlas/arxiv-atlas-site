<p>
  <strong>Interstellar dust</strong> consists of tiny solid particles — silicate minerals, carbonaceous grains, and ice-coated composites — dispersed throughout the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a>. Though comprising only ~1% of the ISM by mass, dust profoundly influences astrophysics: it absorbs and scatters starlight (extinction), re-emits absorbed energy as thermal infrared radiation, catalyzes molecule formation (including H₂), and — when aligned with <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> — produces polarized emission that reveals B-field structure.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Properties and Composition</h2>
<p>
  Dust grains range in size from ~5 nm (large molecules/PAHs) to ~0.25 μm. Their composition includes amorphous silicates (Mg, Fe-bearing), carbonaceous materials (graphite, amorphous carbon, PAHs), and in cold dense environments, ice mantles of H₂O, CO, CO₂. The grain size distribution follows approximately a power law dn/da ∝ a⁻³·⁵ (the MRN distribution).
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Dust as a Tracer</h2>
<p>Dust thermal emission is one of the most powerful tracers in astronomy:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Far-infrared/submm continuum</strong> — optically thin dust emission at λ > 100 μm traces total gas <a href="/pages/column-density" class="wikilink">column density</a> N(H₂), used by surveys like ATLASGAL (870 μm)<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup> and Herschel</li>
  <li><strong>Mid-infrared absorption</strong> — <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">IRDCs</a> are identified as dark silhouettes against the MIR Galactic background<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">Polarized emission</a></strong> — non-spherical dust grains align with local B-fields via radiative torques; their polarized thermal emission maps the plane-of-sky magnetic field<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-2">Dust and Star Formation</h2>
<p>
  In <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a>, dust shields molecules from UV photodissociation, enabling the chemistry that produces species like C¹⁸O, N₂H⁺, and NH₃ used to study cloud kinematics and <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>. Dust grains also provide surfaces for H₂ formation and mediate the thermal balance of dense gas through far-infrared cooling.
</p>
<p>
  The gas-to-dust mass ratio (~100 in the Milky Way) links dust continuum observations to total gas mass estimates, though this ratio introduces systematic uncertainty of order 50% in mass calculations<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
