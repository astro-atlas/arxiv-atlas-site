<p>
  <strong>Radiatively inefficient accretion flows</strong> (RIAFs), also termed advection-dominated accretion flows (ADAFs), are hot, geometrically thick, optically thin accretion solutions that arise when the mass accretion rate is well below the Eddington rate <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. In this regime, viscous heating of the gas cannot be efficiently radiated away; instead, the thermal energy is advected inward with the flow or carried outward in winds and jets <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. RIAFs are the standard accretion model for low-luminosity AGN, including the supermassive black holes in M87 and <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84 (NGC 1275)</a> <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="theory">Hot Accretion Flow Theory</h2>
<p>
  Narayan &amp; Yi (1994) derived self-similar ADAF solutions showing that when the accretion rate ṁ &lt;&lt; &alpha;<sup>2</sup> ṁ<sub>Edd</sub> (where &alpha; is the viscosity parameter), the ion temperature approaches the virial value T<sub>i</sub> ~ 10<sup>12</sup> K while the electron temperature remains much lower (T<sub>e</sub> ~ 10<sup>9</sup>&ndash;10<sup>11</sup> K) due to inefficient Coulomb coupling <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This two-temperature structure is fundamental: ions carry most of the thermal energy but radiate inefficiently, while electrons produce the observed emission primarily through synchrotron, bremsstrahlung, and inverse Compton processes <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Narayan &amp; Yi (1995) extended the theory to include a Bernoulli parameter analysis, showing that ADAF solutions naturally have positive Bernoulli parameters, implying that outflows and winds are energetically favored <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. This prediction was confirmed by subsequent MHD simulations and is now considered an integral feature of hot accretion flows <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Yuan &amp; Narayan (2014) provided a comprehensive review consolidating two decades of theoretical and observational progress on hot accretion flows, establishing the modern framework that includes winds, jets, convection, and <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> effects <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="thin-disk-comparison">Comparison with Standard Thin Disk</h2>
<p>
  The Shakura &amp; Sunyaev (1973) standard thin disk model applies when the accretion rate is a significant fraction of the Eddington rate <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. In contrast to RIAFs <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Geometry:</strong> Thin disks are geometrically thin (H/R &lt;&lt; 1) and optically thick; RIAFs are geometrically thick (H/R ~ 1) and optically thin <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Radiative efficiency:</strong> Thin disks radiate ~10% of the rest-mass energy; RIAFs radiate orders of magnitude less, with most energy advected or ejected <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Temperature:</strong> Thin disks have T ~ 10<sup>5</sup>&ndash;10<sup>7</sup> K with single-temperature gas; RIAFs are two-temperature with T<sub>i</sub> &gt;&gt; T<sub>e</sub> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Spectral signatures:</strong> Thin disks produce a "big blue bump" thermal UV/optical spectrum; RIAFs produce hard X-ray spectra with weak thermal components <a href="#ref3">[3]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Transition:</strong> The critical accretion rate separating regimes is ṁ<sub>crit</sub> ~ &alpha;<sup>2</sup> ṁ<sub>Edd</sub> ~ 10<sup>&minus;2</sup> ṁ<sub>Edd</sub> for typical &alpha; ~ 0.1 <a href="#ref3">[3]</a></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="low-luminosity-agn">Application to Low-Luminosity AGN</h2>
<p>
  Most supermassive black holes in the local universe accrete far below the Eddington rate and are classified as low-luminosity AGN (LLAGN) <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. RIAF models successfully explain the spectral energy distributions of the prototypical LLAGN Sgr A* (the Galactic Center black hole) and M87 <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>. The Event Horizon Telescope imaging of M87* and Sgr A* provided direct constraints on the accretion flow geometry, confirming expectations from RIAF theory <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">NGC 1275 (3C 84)</a>, the central galaxy of the <a href="/pages/perseus-cluster" class="wikilink">Perseus cluster</a>, hosts a LLAGN with a powerful radio jet <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Its accretion flow is modeled as a RIAF, with <a href="/pages/bondi-accretion" class="wikilink">Bondi accretion</a> from the hot cluster atmosphere feeding the hot flow at large radii <a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="faraday-screen">Role as Faraday Screen</h2>
<p>
  The hot, magnetized gas in a RIAF provides a dense Faraday-rotating medium surrounding the jet base <a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. Park et al. (2026) measured <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation measures</a> (RM) in <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84</a> using <a href="/pages/vlbi-polarimetry" class="wikilink">VLBI polarimetry</a> at 43&ndash;141 GHz and found RM gradients consistent with the jet passing through a magnetized accretion flow <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. The observed RM values (~10<sup>4</sup>&ndash;10<sup>5</sup> rad m<sup>&minus;2</sup>) constrain the electron density and magnetic field strength in the circumnuclear medium <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  In M87, RIAF models predict RM values of ~10<sup>5</sup> rad m<sup>&minus;2</sup> near the jet base, consistent with EHT polarimetric measurements <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>. The RIAF thus serves as both the fuel reservoir for jet launching and a diagnostic screen whose Faraday rotation properties encode the accretion flow structure <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="winds-jets">Winds, Jets, and Feedback</h2>
<p>
  RIAFs naturally produce outflows: the positive Bernoulli parameter of the hot gas drives thermal winds from the accretion flow surface <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>. MHD simulations show that these winds can carry away a substantial fraction of the inflowing mass, reducing the actual accretion rate reaching the black hole to ṁ(r) &prop; r<sup>s</sup> with s ~ 0.5&ndash;1 <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Magnetically arrested disk (MAD) states in RIAFs efficiently launch relativistic jets via the Blandford-Znajek mechanism <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. The jet power can exceed the radiative luminosity by orders of magnitude in LLAGN, making mechanical <a href="/pages/agn-feedback" class="wikilink">AGN feedback</a> dominant over radiative feedback in these systems <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. In the <a href="/pages/perseus-cluster" class="wikilink">Perseus cluster</a>, the mechanical power of the 3C 84 jets inflates X-ray cavities and drives <a href="/pages/shock-waves" class="wikilink">weak shocks</a> that heat the intracluster medium <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="simulations">GRMHD Simulations</h2>
<p>
  General-relativistic MHD simulations have become the primary tool for studying RIAF dynamics beyond the self-similar approximation <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. Tchekhovskoy et al. (2011) showed that magnetically arrested disks can extract up to ~300% of the accretion power through the Blandford-Znajek mechanism for rapidly spinning black holes <a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. These simulations confirm the two-temperature structure, wind production, and jet launching predicted by analytic RIAF theory <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.
</p>
