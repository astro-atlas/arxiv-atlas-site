<p>
  The <strong>Butcher-Oemler effect</strong> is the observation that galaxy clusters at intermediate redshifts (z ~ 0.3–0.5) contain a significantly higher fraction of blue, star-forming galaxies than nearby clusters <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This provided early evidence that galaxy populations in clusters evolve with cosmic time, with environmental processes progressively quenching <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="connection">Connection to Modern Understanding</h2>
<p>
  The Butcher-Oemler effect is now understood within the framework of environmental quenching: infalling <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">blue cloud</a> galaxies are transformed by mechanisms including <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a>, strangulation, and harassment. The population-dependent <a href="/pages/orbital-anisotropy-in-galaxy-clusters" class="wikilink">orbital anisotropy</a> seen in the <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">Coma Cluster</a> <a href="#ref2">[2]</a> represents the z ≈ 0 end-point of this evolutionary process.
</p>
