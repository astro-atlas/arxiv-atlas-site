<h2 id="definition">Definition</h2>
<p>The <strong>bullet dwarf scenario</strong> proposes that <a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">dark-matter-free galaxies</a> form when two gas-rich dwarf galaxies collide at high speed (~300 km/s), physically separating their baryonic gas from dark matter halos <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The gas, stripped from its dark matter, collapses to form new galaxies that contain stars but little or no dark matter &mdash; a dwarf-scale analog of the Bullet Cluster <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="physics">Physical Mechanism</h2>

<h3>Foundational: Collisionless vs. collisional matter</h3>
<p>Dark matter is collisionless: dark matter particles pass through each other during a collision. Gas, however, is collisional: it shocks, compresses, and can be separated from dark matter during high-speed encounters <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. This fundamental difference enables the physical separation of baryonic and dark components during galaxy-galaxy collisions.</p>

<h3>Intermediate: Collision dynamics</h3>
<p>In the bullet dwarf model, two gas-rich galaxies pass through each other at high velocity. The dark matter halos pass through unimpeded, while the gas from both galaxies shocks, decelerates, and is left behind along the collision axis <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. This gas then fragments and forms new galaxies &mdash; producing a linear trail of objects along the collision trajectory <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.</p>

<h3>Specialist: Predictions and confirmations</h3>
<p>The scenario makes several specific, testable predictions:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Linear alignment:</strong> Newly-formed galaxies should be arranged in a line along the collision axis <a href="#ref3">[3]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em> by the discovery of the NGC 1052 trail <a href="#ref4">[4]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Kinematic connection:</strong> Trail members should have velocities linearly increasing with position <a href="#ref3">[3]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em> by Keim et al. (2025) <a href="#ref5">[5]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Dark matter deficiency:</strong> Trail galaxies should lack dark matter <a href="#ref1">[1]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em> for DF2 <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>, DF4 <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>, and DF9 <a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Luminous globular clusters:</strong> High-pressure gas conditions during collision should produce unusually massive star clusters <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em> <a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Large sizes:</strong> Galaxies formed from expanding gas should be unusually diffuse <a href="#ref12">[12]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em></li>
  <li><strong>Monochromatic GC colors:</strong> Single-burst star formation should produce uniform cluster colors <a href="#ref13">[13]</a> &mdash; <em>confirmed</em></li>
</ul>

<h2 id="simulations">Simulations</h2>
<p>Shin et al. (2020) <a href="#ref3">[3]</a> produced the first hydrodynamical simulations of the bullet dwarf scenario, demonstrating that a high-speed collision can produce a trail of dark-matter-free objects. Lee et al. (2021) <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> showed the scenario naturally produces luminous globular clusters. Lee et al. (2024) <a href="#ref14">[14]</a> performed simulations specific to the NGC 1052 system, demonstrating that trail galaxies can survive tidal disruption in the group environment.</p>

<div class="dispute">
  <h4>Dispute: Survivability of trail galaxies</h4>
  <p><strong>Ogiya (2022)</strong> <a href="#ref15">[15]</a> argued that dark-matter-free galaxies would be quickly disrupted by tidal forces in the NGC 1052 group.</p>
  <p><strong>Lee et al. (2024)</strong> <a href="#ref14">[14]</a> showed survivability is possible under realistic tidal field conditions. The detection of DF9 with properties consistent with DF2/DF4 provides empirical evidence <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</p>
</div>

<h2 id="alternatives">Alternative Formation Scenarios</h2>
<p>Several alternatives have been proposed for dark-matter-free galaxy formation:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Tidal dwarf galaxies:</strong> Formed from debris of massive galaxy mergers <a href="#ref16">[16]</a>; cannot explain the trail&rsquo;s kinematic structure or luminous GCs</li>
  <li><strong>Quasar-driven outflows:</strong> Gas expelled by AGN activity <a href="#ref17">[17]</a>; does not predict the observed linear alignment</li>
  <li><strong>Tidal stripping of streams:</strong> Linear substructures from infalling material <a href="#ref18">[18]</a>; does not explain GC properties</li>
</ul>
<p>Only the bullet dwarf scenario explains the full combination of trail alignment, dark matter deficiency, luminous GCs, large sizes, and monochromatic cluster colors <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</p>
