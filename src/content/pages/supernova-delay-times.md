<p>
  The <strong>delay time</strong> of a supernova is the interval between the formation of its progenitor star(s) and the eventual explosion. For <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernovae</a>, the delay time distribution (DTD) is a critical diagnostic of the progenitor channel and a key input for chemical evolution models<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Delay Time Distributions</h2>
<p>
  Observational measurements indicate that the Type Ia DTD follows approximately a t<sup>&minus;1</sup> power law, with significant SN Ia production extending from ~0.5 Gyr to beyond 10 Gyr after star formation<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. The minimum delay time of ~0.5 Gyr reflects the evolutionary timescale of the least massive white dwarf progenitors that can contribute to the SN Ia rate. The "prompt" component (delay times ~0.5&ndash;1 Gyr) contributes a fraction of events in star-forming galaxies, while the "delayed" or "tardy" component (&gt;1 Gyr) dominates in early-type hosts<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Implications for SN Ia Environments</h2>
<p>
  The long delay times of most Type Ia progenitors mean these SNe preferentially trace old stellar populations<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>. This has a critical consequence for <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">merging galaxies</a>: if the merger event is &le;0.5 Gyr old, any Type Ia SN found in the merger remnant or <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring</a> must have originated from a pre-existing old stellar population rather than from stars formed during the merger<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>. This is the central argument for <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> of the SN 2025adpq progenitor: the ring is only ~70&ndash;200 Myr old, far younger than the &ge;0.5 Gyr minimum delay time<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Core-Collapse Comparison</h2>
<p>
  Core-collapse supernovae (Types II, Ib, Ic) have much shorter delay times (&le;50 Myr), set by the main-sequence lifetimes of massive stars (&ge;8 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>)<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>. Consequently, core-collapse SNe are efficiently produced during merger-triggered starbursts, while Type Ia SNe are underrepresented in actively merging systems unless pre-existing old progenitors are present<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  Delay time distributions constrain both the progenitor physics of <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SNe</a> and the chemical enrichment history of galaxies. The strong constraint provided by the mismatch between CRG ring ages and SN Ia delay times offers a new and independent test of DTD models<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>. Future studies of SN rates in dynamically aged structures (like collisional rings, where ages can be derived from expansion velocities) could tighten DTD constraints<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>.
</p>
