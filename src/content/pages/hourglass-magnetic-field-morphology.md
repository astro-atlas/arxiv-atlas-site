<p>
  The <strong>hourglass magnetic field morphology</strong> is a distinctive pinched, waist-like pattern in <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> lines observed toward collapsing cloud cores and clumps. It arises when <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravitational contraction</a> drags initially ordered (often perpendicular-to-filament) field lines inward, concentrating them toward the densest region. The hourglass shape is one of the strongest observational signatures that magnetic fields are dynamically important and that collapse is magnetically regulated<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Physical Origin</h2>
<h3>Gravitational Pinching</h3>
<p>
  In the classic picture, a cloud with initially uniform B-field perpendicular to a <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filament</a> undergoes gravitational contraction. Because field lines are frozen into the (partially) ionized gas, the collapsing material drags field lines inward, creating a pinched hourglass geometry centered on the densest clump<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. MHD simulations by Nakamura & Li (2008) show this arises naturally when <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> cannot support the cloud against settling along field lines, and ambipolar diffusion allows dense cores to become self-gravitating<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h3>Converging Sub-filaments</h3>
<p>
  An alternative mechanism produces hourglass-like morphology through converging sub-filaments aligned with the B-field. As material flows along multiple sub-filaments toward a central hub, the field lines — parallel to the low-density striations but perpendicular to the dense spine — naturally produce a pinched pattern<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. Both mechanisms may act simultaneously.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Observational Examples</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>G351.77-0.53</strong> — SOFIA/HAWC+ 214 μm <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">polarimetry</a> reveals a clear hourglass toward clump c1, with B_POS reaching ~0.8 mG. The cloud is <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">magnetically transcritical</a> (λ ~ 0.9) on large scales, transitioning to supercritical near c1<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>OMC-1 (Orion A)</strong> — JCMT BISTRO and SOFIA/HAWC+ show hourglass morphology with magnetic energy dominating the energy budget<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>DR21</strong> — JCMT BISTRO-2 shows perpendicular B-fields at pc-scales with hourglass toward the southern massive core<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Serpens South</strong> — B-field lines follow inflowing filaments to the main hub<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-2">Diagnostic Power</h2>
<p>
  The hourglass morphology directly constrains the <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a> and collapse geometry. Combined with the <a href="/pages/davis-chandrasekhar-fermi-method" class="wikilink">DCF method</a>, it allows estimation of field strength at different scales. The transition from transcritical (cloud envelope) to supercritical (core) mass-to-flux ratios is a prediction of magnetically regulated collapse models that can be tested in hourglass sources<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  However, distinguishing gravitational pinching from sub-filament convergence requires high-resolution polarimetry combined with kinematic data to identify infall signatures<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
