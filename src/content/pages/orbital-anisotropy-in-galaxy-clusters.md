<p>
  <strong>Orbital anisotropy</strong> β(r) quantifies whether galaxy orbits in a cluster are predominantly radial, tangential, or isotropic <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. It is a key diagnostic of cluster assembly history and environmental processing: infalling galaxies tend to have radial orbits, while virialised populations are more isotropic <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="radial-trends">Radial Trends</h2>
<p>
  Both observations and simulations consistently show anisotropy growing with radius — from near-isotropy at the cluster centre to predominantly radial orbits in the outskirts <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. This reflects the transition from a virialised core to an infall-dominated periphery <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="population-dependence">Population Dependence</h2>
<p>
  The key finding of Pedratti et al. (2026) is that anisotropy profiles differ by galaxy colour <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">Blue cloud</a> galaxies:</strong> More radial orbits at all radii, consistent with recent infall <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">Red sequence</a> galaxies:</strong> More isotropic, consistent with virialisation <a href="#ref7">[7]</a></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">Green valley</a> galaxies:</strong> Intermediate behaviour <a href="#ref7">[7]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  This pattern is consistent with <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a> along radial orbits quenching blue infalling galaxies into the green valley and eventually the red sequence <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. Mamon et al. (2019) found similar trends in stacked WINGS clusters <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>, and Biviano et al. (2025) confirmed this in CLASH-VLT clusters <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. However, Hwang &amp; Lee (2008) reported no significant differences <a href="#ref11">[11]</a>, suggesting this may depend on sample size or cluster dynamical state.
</p>
<h2 id="parametric-models">Parametric Models</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Osipkov-Merritt:</strong> β(r) = r²/(r² + r<sub>β</sub>²), widely used for its simplicity <a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Tiret (generalised):</strong> Captures transitions between central isotropy and outer radial orbits with more flexibility <a href="#ref14">[14]</a></li>
</ul>
