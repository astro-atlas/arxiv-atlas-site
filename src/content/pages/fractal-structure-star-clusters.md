<p>
  <strong>Fractal structure</strong> in young star clusters refers to the self-similar, hierarchically clumped spatial distribution of stars that characterizes newly formed stellar populations. Rather than being smoothly distributed, young clusters show subgroups within subgroups — a pattern inherited from the turbulent, fractal density field of their parent <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular cloud</a>.<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref2">2</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="origin">Origin: Inheritance from Molecular Clouds</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">Giant molecular clouds</a> are themselves fractal structures, shaped by <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">supersonic turbulence</a> and <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a>. When a cloud undergoes gravoturbulent collapse<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref4">4</a></sup>, it fragments into dense clumps and filaments that serve as nurseries for stellar subclusters.<sup><a href="#ref5">5</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref6">6</a></sup> The resulting young cluster naturally inherits the spatial hierarchy of its birth environment.
</p>
<p>
  Observations from the Herschel Space Observatory confirm that filamentary molecular cloud networks give rise to chains of dense cores and proto-clusters<sup><a href="#ref7">7</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref8">8</a></sup>, while X-ray and near-infrared surveys of young stellar clusters (e.g., with Chandra) reveal rich subcluster populations within OB associations and star-forming regions.<sup><a href="#ref9">9</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="erasure">Dynamical Erasure</h2>
<p>
  Fractal substructure is not permanent. As clusters evolve dynamically, gravitational interactions smooth out the initial clumpiness — a process called <a href="/pages/dynamical-relaxation-clusters" class="wikilink">dynamical relaxation</a>. The timescale for fractal erasure has been quantified in multiple simulations:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>Laverde-Villarreal et al. (2025)<sup><a href="#ref10">10</a></sup> and Akhmetali et al. (2026)<sup><a href="#ref11">11</a></sup> both find fractal erasure at approximately 2.5 free-fall times (t<sub>ff</sub>), across cloud masses ranging from ~2500 M<sub>☉</sub> to ~3 &times; 10<sup>5</sup> M<sub>☉</sub>.</li>
  <li>Earlier work by Maschberger et al. (2010)<sup><a href="#ref12">12</a></sup> found low initial Q values rising to non-fractal values within a couple of free-fall times in gravitationally bound clusters.</li>
  <li>Ballone et al. (2020)<sup><a href="#ref13">13</a></sup> found that very massive clusters (10<sup>4</sup>–10<sup>5</sup> M<sub>☉</sub>) remained fractal after 2 t<sub>ff</sub>, possibly due to different initial density profiles.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="feedback">Role of Stellar Feedback</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/stellar-feedback" class="wikilink">Stellar feedback</a> from massive stars can both accelerate and delay fractal erasure. Photoionization and stellar winds can disperse surrounding gas, triggering secondary <a href="/pages/subcluster-formation" class="wikilink">subcluster formation</a> in compressed shells — prolonging fractal structure beyond 4 t<sub>ff</sub> in some simulations.<sup><a href="#ref11">11</a></sup> However, this effect is stochastic, depending critically on when and where a massive star forms.<sup><a href="#ref11">11</a></sup> In other simulations, massive stars form without measurably enhancing fractality.
</p>
<h2 id="measurement">Measuring Fractal Structure</h2>
<p>
  Three main diagnostics are used:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/q-parameter-fractality" class="wikilink">Q parameter</a></strong> (Cartwright &amp; Whitworth 2004<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup>): ratio of normalized MST edge length to mean interparticle separation. Q &lt; 0.8 (2D) or &lt; 0.7 (3D) indicates fractal structure.</li>
  <li><strong>Box-counting dimension</strong>: slope of ln N(L) vs. −ln L; global measure of spatial extent.<sup><a href="#ref14">14</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Correlation dimension</strong>: slope of the correlation integral C(r) vs. ln r; sensitive to local clustering.<sup><a href="#ref15">15</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref16">16</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>
  The Q parameter and correlation dimension are more sensitive to local substructure, while box-counting dimension captures global spatial coverage. The correlation dimension shows a moderate positive correlation with Q, while box-counting shows only a weak correlation.<sup><a href="#ref11">11</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="open-questions">Open Questions</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Does the ~2.5 t<sub>ff</sub> erasure timescale hold for very different initial density profiles (e.g., flat or power-law)?</li>
  <li>What is the 3D fractal structure of real young clusters, as opposed to projected 2D observations?</li>
  <li>How does the fractal structure of a cluster at birth affect its long-term survival and dissolution into the field?</li>
</ul>
