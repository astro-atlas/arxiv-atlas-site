<p>
  <strong>Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia)</strong> are thermonuclear explosions of carbon-oxygen white dwarfs in binary systems. They are defined spectroscopically by the absence of hydrogen lines and the presence of a strong Si II absorption feature near 6150 &Aring;<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Their relatively uniform peak luminosities, correctable via the width-luminosity relation, make them indispensable as cosmological distance indicators<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Progenitor Channels</h2>
<p>
  Two main progenitor scenarios are debated<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Single-degenerate (SD):</strong> A white dwarf accretes material from a non-degenerate companion until approaching the Chandrasekhar mass (~1.4 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>), triggering thermonuclear runaway.</li>
  <li><strong>Double-degenerate (DD):</strong> Two white dwarfs merge following orbital decay via gravitational wave emission, leading to detonation.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  Both channels predict a range of <a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay times</a> from star formation to explosion, typically &ge;0.5 Gyr<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. This long delay means SNe Ia preferentially trace older stellar populations<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Host Galaxy Environments</h2>
<p>
  SNe Ia occur across all galaxy types but their rates and properties correlate with host galaxy properties<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>. More luminous (slower-declining) SNe Ia are preferentially found in star-forming galaxies, while fainter (faster-declining) events occur more frequently in early-type hosts<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>. In <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">merging systems</a>, pre-existing old stellar populations can be <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamically displaced</a> to large offsets, producing SNe Ia in unexpected environments such as <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional rings</a><sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Large-Offset and Hostless Events</h2>
<p>
  Some SNe Ia are found at large projected offsets from the nearest visible galaxy or with no detectable host at all<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>. Proposed explanations include explosions in the intracluster stellar population<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>, ultra-faint dwarf galaxies below detection limits<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a></sup>, hypervelocity progenitor systems, and <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> during <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy mergers</a><sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>. See also <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless supernovae</a> and <a href="/pages/large-offset-supernovae" class="wikilink">large-offset supernovae</a>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  Type Ia supernovae are central to modern cosmology as standardizable candles that led to the discovery of the accelerating expansion of the universe. Understanding their progenitor systems and environments is critical for reducing systematic uncertainties in distance measurements. The discovery of SNe Ia in <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional rings</a> (e.g., SN 2025adpq<sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>) reveals new channels of progenitor displacement that must be accounted for in host-galaxy corrections used in cosmological analyses.
</p>
