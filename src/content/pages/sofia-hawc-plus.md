<p>
  <strong>HAWC+</strong> (High-resolution Airborne Wideband Camera-plus) was the far-infrared camera and <a href="/pages/polarimetry" class="wikilink">polarimeter</a> aboard the <strong>Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy (SOFIA)</strong>, a modified Boeing 747SP carrying a 2.5-meter telescope to altitudes of 12–14 km, above ~99% of atmospheric water vapor<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. HAWC+ was the primary facility for far-infrared <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">dust polarimetry</a> at wavelengths inaccessible from the ground.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Instrument Capabilities</h2>
<p>HAWC+ operated in five bands:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Band A (53 μm)</strong> — 4.85″ resolution, 2.55″ pixel scale</li>
  <li><strong>Band C (89 μm)</strong> — 7.80″ resolution</li>
  <li><strong>Band D (154 μm)</strong> — 13.6″ resolution</li>
  <li><strong>Band E (214 μm)</strong> — 18.2″ resolution, 4.55″ pixel scale; the most commonly used band for cloud-scale B-field mapping</li>
</ul>
<p>
  HAWC+ measured Stokes I, Q, and U simultaneously, providing polarization fraction and angle maps. Standard data products (Level 4) include debiased polarization vectors with quality flags<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Key Science Results</h2>
<p>HAWC+ produced transformative <a href="/pages/magnetic-fields-in-star-formation" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> maps of star-forming regions:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>G351.77-0.53</strong> — revealed <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass B-field morphology</a> in an <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">IRDC</a> (Band E, 214 μm)<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>OMC-1 (Orion)</strong> — multiwavelength polarimetry showing field morphology across density scales<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G11.11-0.12</strong> — B-field structure in the "Snake" IRDC<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Milky Way Bone filaments</strong> — systematic B-field survey of Galactic Bones<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-2">Legacy</h2>
<p>
  SOFIA ceased operations in September 2022. HAWC+ data remain the only far-infrared polarimetric observations of many star-forming regions, as no current or near-future facility covers the 50–200 μm polarimetric window. The data are archived and continue to yield new results including the G351 study<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
