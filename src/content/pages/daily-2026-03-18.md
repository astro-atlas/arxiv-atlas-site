<h1 class="page-title">astro-ph.GA Daily Summary — March 18, 2026</h1>

<p class="daily-intro">Eight papers spanning cosmic dawn to the local universe, from dust-reddened galaxies at z = 11 to the kinematics of the Coma Cluster. Today's highlights: a "red monster" confirmed at record redshift, a Type Ia supernova displaced by a collisional ring, and the first spatially resolved magnetic field measurement inside an elliptical galaxy's Bondi sphere.</p>

<h2>🌌 Galaxy Environments &amp; Evolution</h2>

<p>Three papers paint a consistent picture of how environment reshapes galaxies at every scale. In the <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">Coma Cluster</a>, new DESI kinematics reveal the orbital geometry of <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a> in real time — blue galaxies fall in on radial orbits and get stripped into the red sequence. At the group scale, the M51 system shows that even low-mass <a href="/pages/galaxy-groups" class="wikilink">groups</a> can erase the radial gradients we associate with undisturbed disk growth, challenging the assumption that environmental processing requires cluster-scale halos. And in the most dramatic case, a head-on <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy collision</a> physically displaces old stellar populations into expanding rings — creating <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernovae</a> that appear hostless at cosmological distances. Together, these results remind us that environment acts through multiple channels simultaneously: stripping gas, flattening gradients, and literally ejecting stars from their birthplaces.</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-16706" class="wikilink">Pedratti et al.</a> — DESI kinematics of 1651 Coma galaxies show blue cloud members plunging through the cluster on radial orbits, providing direct kinematic evidence for <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a> as the driver of the blue → green → red evolutionary sequence.</li>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-15869" class="wikilink">Thainá-Batista et al.</a> — J-PLUS resolved maps of the M51 group reveal flattened age and <a href="/pages/metallicity-gradients" class="wikilink">metallicity gradients</a> compared to the pristine M101 group, with M63 showing outside-in <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a> from a past encounter.</li>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-15899" class="wikilink">O'Connor et al.</a> — A Type Ia supernova in a <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring</a> 11.4 kpc from its host shows that galaxy collisions can <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamically displace</a> old progenitors, identifying ring galaxies as "supernova factories" with implications for the <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless SN</a> problem.</li>
</ul>

<h2>🔴 The High-Redshift Frontier</h2>

<p>The most distant spectroscopically confirmed dust-reddened galaxy pushes the timeline for <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> enrichment to just 400 Myr after the Big Bang. For galaxy evolution, this matters because it means current rest-frame UV surveys may be systematically missing a significant fraction of early <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> happening behind dust — with direct consequences for cosmic SFR density estimates, stellar mass functions at z &gt; 10, and reionization models. If the "<a href="/pages/red-monsters" class="wikilink">red monster</a>" phase accounts for ~20% of early galaxy lifetimes as predicted, the true diversity of the first galaxies is far richer than UV-selected samples suggest.</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-15841" class="wikilink">Rodighiero et al.</a> — Spectroscopic confirmation of EGS-z11-R0 at z = 11.45 with red UV slope (β ≈ −1.0) and tentative [Fe V] detection, making it the highest-redshift "<a href="/pages/red-monsters" class="wikilink">red monster</a>" — a dust-enshrouded galaxy assembling its stellar mass before radiation clears the dust.</li>
</ul>

<h2>⭐ Star Formation &amp; ISM Physics</h2>

<p>Two papers address the initial conditions of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> — the physics that ultimately sets the stellar mass function and feedback budget in galaxies. The fractal structure inherited by young clusters from their parent clouds gets erased on a universal timescale (~2.5 free-fall times), suggesting a fundamental gravitational clock governs cluster assembly regardless of environment. Meanwhile, new polarimetry of an <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">infrared dark cloud</a> catches a massive filament at the transcritical boundary where magnetic, gravitational, and kinetic energies are all comparable — the tipping point that determines whether and how massive stars form. These results constrain the sub-galactic physics that feeds back into galaxy-scale evolution.</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-16183" class="wikilink">Akhmetali et al.</a> — Simulations show young <a href="/pages/star-cluster-formation" class="wikilink">star clusters</a> erase their inherited <a href="/pages/fractal-structure-star-clusters" class="wikilink">fractal substructure</a> by ~2.5 free-fall times through dynamical relaxation — a timescale that appears universal across cloud masses.</li>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-16425" class="wikilink">Jadhav et al.</a> — SOFIA/HAWC+ reveals an <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass magnetic field</a> in IRDC G351.77−0.53 with <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a> λ ≈ 0.9, placing this massive filament right at the transcritical boundary between magnetic support and gravitational collapse.</li>
</ul>

<h2>🚀 AGN Jets &amp; Non-Thermal Physics</h2>

<p>Two papers reshape our understanding of how AGN jets interact with their surroundings — a key ingredient in the feedback cycle that regulates star formation in massive galaxies. A new theoretical framework shows that <a href="/pages/recollimation-shocks" class="wikilink">recollimation shocks</a> in sub-relativistic jets can accelerate particles to PeV–EeV energies, meaning even modest Seyfert jets could inject significant non-thermal energy into the circumgalactic medium. Complementing this, multi-decade VLBI polarimetry of <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84</a> reveals that the <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a> screen isn't the accretion flow but the ambient circumnuclear medium — giving us the first spatially resolved <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> profile inside the <a href="/pages/bondi-accretion" class="wikilink">Bondi sphere</a> of an elliptical galaxy. Together, these constrain both the energy input from jets and the magnetized environment they propagate through.</p>

<ul>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-16647" class="wikilink">Peretti et al.</a> — Semi-analytic modeling promotes <a href="/pages/recollimation-shocks" class="wikilink">recollimation shocks</a> to dominant particle accelerators across jet classes, predicting PeV–EeV proton energies in Seyfert jets with hadronic signatures matching recent HAWC and LHAASO detections.</li>
  <li><a href="/pages/2603-16796" class="wikilink">Park et al.</a> — Nine years of multi-frequency VLBI show all rotation measures in 3C 84 follow RM ∝ r<sup>−2.7</sup> from 1–15 pc, tracing the ambient medium within the Bondi sphere rather than a RIAF — yielding B ∝ r<sup>−1.7 to −2.2</sup>, far steeper than M87.</li>
</ul>
