  <div class="key-takeaways-title">Key Takeaways</div>
  <h4>Main Results</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>DF9's stellar velocity dispersion (&sigma; = 6.4<sup>+4.0</sup><sub>&minus;4.3</sub> km/s) confirms it lacks dark matter, consistent with the 8.3 km/s expected from its 1.4 &times; 10<sup>8</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub> stellar mass alone and far below the 27 &plusmn; 3 km/s expected from a standard <a href="/pages/nfw-profile" class="wikilink">NFW</a> dark matter halo <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.</li>
    <li>Three galaxies on the NGC 1052 trail are now confirmed <a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">dark-matter-free</a> (DF2, DF4, DF9), all falling below the universal baryon fraction line &mdash; ruling out normal scatter in the <a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">stellar mass&ndash;halo mass relation</a> <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.</li>
    <li>The <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf collision scenario</a> made a falsifiable prediction that trail galaxies should lack dark matter, and DF9 validates this prediction <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>.</li>
    <li>Systematic corrections for stellar broadening (&sigma;<sub>broadening</sub> = 5.4 &plusmn; 2 km/s) and binary orbital motion (&sigma;<sub>binaries</sub> = 2.3 &plusmn; 0.6 km/s) are essential for low-dispersion systems, reducing the measured 8.7 km/s to 6.4 km/s <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</li>
    <li>Star cluster velocities provide an independent consistency check: both DF9 clusters show velocities very close to the diffuse light systemic velocity, consistent with a low-mass system <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.</li>
  </ul>
  <h4>Implications</h4>
  <p>This paper strengthens the case that the NGC 1052 trail represents a fundamentally different galaxy formation channel &mdash; one where dark matter and baryons were physically separated by a high-speed collision. The confirmation of a third <a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">dark-matter-free galaxy</a> transforms what was a two-object curiosity into a systematic pattern, making it much harder to dismiss as measurement error or statistical fluke. For galaxy evolution, this demonstrates that the assumed universality of the dark matter halo framework breaks down in specific extreme environments, with implications for how we interpret the faintest galaxies and scatter in scaling relations.</p>
  <h4>Other works</h4>
  <p>Keim et al. (2026) builds directly on the discoveries of DF2 and DF4 (van Dokkum et al. 2018 <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>; van Dokkum et al. 2019 <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>) and the bullet dwarf collision model (Silk 2019 <a href="#ref5">[5]</a>; Shin et al. 2020 <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>; Lee et al. 2021 <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>, 2024 <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>). It supports the growing recognition that <a href="/pages/ultra-diffuse-galaxy" class="wikilink">ultra-diffuse galaxies</a> span an enormous range of dark matter content. The work challenges the standard bottom-up galaxy formation paradigm by providing a concrete example of top-down formation from collision debris, connecting to broader questions about the nature of dark matter through the demonstrated physical separability of dark and baryonic matter.</p>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>Keim et al. (2026) present Keck/KCWI spectroscopy of NGC 1052-DF9, a <a href="/pages/ultra-diffuse-galaxy" class="wikilink">ultra-diffuse galaxy</a> in the NGC 1052 field that is part of a remarkable linear trail of ~12 faint galaxies kinematically connected to each other <a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. The measured stellar velocity dispersion of &sigma; = 6.4<sup>+4.0</sup><sub>&minus;4.3</sub> km/s is consistent with the prediction from stellar mass alone (8.3 km/s) but far below the 27 &plusmn; 3 km/s expected if DF9 inhabited a standard dark matter halo following the <a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">stellar mass&ndash;halo mass relation</a> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.</p>
<p>This makes DF9 the third galaxy in the NGC 1052 trail confirmed to lack dark matter, after DF2 <a href="#ref3">[3]</a> and DF4 <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. All three galaxies fall below the universal baryon fraction line, meaning any residual dark matter halo would need an unphysically high baryon fraction <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The result validates a specific, falsifiable prediction of the <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf scenario</a>, in which a high-speed collision between two gas-rich galaxies separated baryons from dark matter, producing a trail of dark-matter-free dwarf galaxies <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.</p>
<p>The paper also carefully accounts for systematic effects in low-dispersion integrated-light measurements, including stellar rotation/macroturbulence and binary star orbital motion, which contribute broadening comparable to the gravitational dispersion itself <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.</p>
<h2 id="findings">Key Findings</h2>
<h3>DF9 velocity dispersion and dark matter content</h3>
<p>From 10.83 hours of Keck/KCWI integration, Keim et al. measure a fitted velocity dispersion of &sigma;<sub>fit</sub> = 8.7<sup>+3.4</sup><sub>&minus;3.7</sub> km/s using MCMC template fitting with MIST isochrone synthetic spectra <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. After subtracting stellar broadening (5.4 &plusmn; 2 km/s) and binary orbital motion (2.3 &plusmn; 0.6 km/s) in quadrature, the gravitational velocity dispersion is &sigma; = 6.4<sup>+4.0</sup><sub>&minus;4.3</sub> km/s <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a>. The dynamical mass within the effective radius is M<sub>e</sub> = 0.42<sup>+0.68</sup><sub>&minus;0.37</sub> &times; 10<sup>8</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>, consistent with the stellar mass within that radius (0.71 &plusmn; 0.16 &times; 10<sup>8</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>.</p>
<h3>Comparison to dark matter halo predictions</h3>
<p>Using the <a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">SMHM relation</a> from Zahra et al. (2023) <a href="#ref2">[2]</a> with an <a href="/pages/nfw-profile" class="wikilink">NFW halo</a> profile <a href="#ref14">[14]</a>, the expected velocity dispersion for DF9 would be 27 &plusmn; 3 km/s &mdash; highly inconsistent with the observed 6.4 km/s. Alternative SMHM relations yield even higher predictions (~32 km/s) <a href="#ref15">[15]</a>. DF9 joins DF2 and DF4 as extreme outliers in the r<sub>e</sub>&ndash;&sigma;&ndash;M<sub>&star;</sub> plane: galaxies with similar dispersions are several times smaller, while galaxies with similar stellar masses have much larger dispersions <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</p>
<h3>Trail of dark-matter-free galaxies</h3>
<p>DF2, DF4, and DF9 are all members of a linear alignment of ~12 faint galaxies in the NGC 1052 field, with velocities increasing linearly with position along the trail <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. All three fall below the universal baryon fraction in the dynamical mass vs. stellar mass comparison, requiring either extremely small dark matter halos with unphysical baryon fractions, or complete absence of dark matter <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>.</p>
<div class="dispute">
  <h4>Dispute: Survivability of dark-matter-free galaxies</h4>
  <p><strong>Challenge:</strong> Ogiya (2022) <a href="#ref17">[17]</a> questioned whether dark-matter-free galaxies could survive tidal disruption in the NGC 1052 group environment, arguing they would be destroyed on short timescales.</p>
  <p><strong>Response:</strong> Lee et al. (2024) <a href="#ref7">[7]</a> performed dedicated simulations of the bullet dwarf scenario specific to the NGC 1052 system, demonstrating that trail galaxies can survive under the actual tidal field conditions. The existence of DF9 with properties consistent with DF2/DF4 provides empirical evidence for survivability.</p>
</div>
<h3>Bullet dwarf scenario confirmation</h3>
<p>The <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf scenario</a> &mdash; a high-speed collision between gas-rich dwarf galaxies that separates baryons from dark matter <a href="#ref5">[5]</a> &mdash; predicted the trail before its discovery <a href="#ref6">[6]</a> and predicted that trail galaxies should lack dark matter. DF9 confirms this prediction <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The scenario also explains the extremely luminous globular clusters <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>, large galaxy sizes <a href="#ref18">[18]</a>, and nearly monochromatic cluster colors <a href="#ref19">[19]</a> observed in trail galaxies.</p>
<h2 id="methodology">Methodology</h2>
<p>Observations were obtained with Keck/KCWI over two nights (2024 Oct 2&ndash;3) using the medium image slicer, RH3 grating at 8600&Aring; central wavelength, and 2&times;2 binning <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Total integration was 10.83 hours (24 on-target, 28 sky exposures of 750s each). Custom cosmic ray rejection adapted from CRCombine was applied, and sky subtraction used neighboring sky frames rescaled to match sky line strength <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Template fitting used 12 synthetic spectra from MIST isochrones <a href="#ref12">[12]</a> spanning metallicities [&minus;1.5, &minus;1.25, &minus;1.0] and ages [7, 8, 9, 10] Gyr, with MCMC sampling via emcee <a href="#ref20">[20]</a>. Instrumental resolution was measured at &sigma;<sub>inst</sub> = 0.375&Aring; (13.0 km/s) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Star cluster velocities from prior Keck/LRIS observations <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> provided an independent consistency check.</p>
<h2 id="literature">Literature Context</h2>
<p>The standard paradigm of galaxy formation holds that galaxies form within dark matter halos, with low-mass dwarfs being the most dark-matter-dominated systems (halo masses &gt;100&times; stellar mass) <a href="#ref16">[16]</a><a href="#ref21">[21]</a>. DF2 (van Dokkum et al. 2018 <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>) and DF4 (van Dokkum et al. 2019 <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>) challenged this paradigm as the first galaxies with dynamical masses consistent with stellar masses alone. The discovery of the linear trail of galaxies in the NGC 1052 field <a href="#ref11">[11]</a> and its kinematic connection <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> provided the context for the bullet dwarf collision model <a href="#ref5">[5]</a>, which uniquely explains the full suite of unusual properties: dark matter deficiency, luminous globular clusters, large sizes, and linear alignment.</p>
<h2 id="supports">What This Paper Supports</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">Bullet dwarf collision scenario</a>:</strong> Validates the specific prediction that trail galaxies should lack dark matter <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">Dark-matter-free galaxy</a> class:</strong> Establishes a third member, transforming a two-object curiosity into a systematic pattern <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Physical separability of dark and baryonic matter:</strong> Confirms that dark matter behaves as a collisionless component that can be separated from gas during high-speed encounters <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref22">[22]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Common origin of trail galaxies:</strong> Kinematic connection and shared dark matter deficiency support formation in a single event <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="challenges">What This Paper Challenges</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Universality of dark matter halos:</strong> The standard assumption that all galaxies form within dark matter halos fails for DF2/DF4/DF9 <a href="#ref16">[16]</a><a href="#ref21">[21]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">SMHM relation</a> applicability to all dwarfs:</strong> DF9 is a &gt;5&sigma; outlier from the predicted dispersion <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Alternative formation models:</strong> Tidal dwarf <a href="#ref23">[23]</a>, quasar-driven outflow <a href="#ref24">[24]</a>, and tidal stripping <a href="#ref25">[25]</a> scenarios cannot explain the full combination of trail alignment, luminous GCs, and dark matter deficiency <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
</ul>
