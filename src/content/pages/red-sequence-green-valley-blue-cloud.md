<p>
  Galaxy populations in colour-magnitude or colour-mass diagrams separate into three broad classes <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Red sequence (RS):</strong> Massive, quiescent early-type galaxies with old stellar populations and little ongoing <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Blue cloud (BC):</strong> Star-forming late-type galaxies with younger stellar populations <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Green valley (GV):</strong> Transitional galaxies between the BC and RS, thought to be undergoing quenching <a href="#ref2">[2]</a></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="cluster-kinematics">Kinematic Differences in Clusters</h2>
<p>
  Multiple studies established that LoS velocity distributions in clusters are type-dependent <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>: RS galaxies show virialised Gaussian distributions, while BC galaxies are broader and sometimes asymmetric, deviating from Gaussianity <a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. In the <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">Coma Cluster</a>, Anderson-Darling tests confirm RS as near-Gaussian (A² ≈ 0.29), BC as strongly non-Gaussian (A² ≈ 1.56), and GV as intermediate (A² ≈ 1.27) <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  BC galaxies also have much less concentrated spatial distributions than RS galaxies (r<sub>ν</sub>(BC) ~ 4 r<sub>ν</sub>(RS) in Coma) <a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a>, and show more radial <a href="/pages/orbital-anisotropy-in-galaxy-clusters" class="wikilink">orbital anisotropy</a>, interpreted as evidence for infall <a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="merger-rejuvenation">Merger Rejuvenation</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">Galaxy mergers</a> can temporarily rejuvenate <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> in otherwise quiescent massive galaxies, complicating simple RS/BC classification. O'Connor et al. (2026) demonstrate that G1 and G2 in the "Pika's Halo" system &mdash; both massive galaxies (log M<sub>*</sub>/M<sub>&#9737;</sub> &asymp; 10.5) with predominantly old stellar populations (~11 Gyr) that would normally reside on or near the red sequence &mdash; show renewed star formation activity driven by the merger, with SFRs of 0.41 and 0.10 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr respectively<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup>. The <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring</a> itself shows even stronger SF (SFR ~ 0.91 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr)<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup>. Such merger-driven rejuvenation can move galaxies off the red sequence or delay their arrival there, creating transient green valley or blue cloud membership.
</p>
<h2 id="classification-scheme">Classification Scheme</h2>
<p>
  The (g−r) vs stellar mass diagram is commonly used, following the approach of Boselli et al. (2014) for the Virgo cluster <a href="#ref14">[14]</a>. Stellar masses are computed using SED fitting codes such as CIGALE with a Chabrier IMF <a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>.
</p>
