<p>
  <strong>Ram-pressure stripping</strong> occurs when a galaxy moves through the hot intracluster medium (ICM), and the hydrodynamic pressure exceeds the gravitational restoring force anchoring gas to the galaxy <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. It is widely considered the dominant environmental quenching mechanism in galaxy clusters <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="physics">Physical Mechanism</h2>
<p>
  The ram-pressure condition is P<sub>ram</sub> = ρ<sub>ICM</sub> v² &gt; 2πGΣ<sub>*</sub>Σ<sub>gas</sub>, where ρ<sub>ICM</sub> is the ICM density, v is the galaxy velocity relative to the ICM, Σ<sub>*</sub> is the stellar surface density, and Σ<sub>gas</sub> is the gas surface density <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Stripping is more effective on radial orbits, which bring galaxies closer to the cluster core at higher velocities <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="connection-to-anisotropy">Connection to Orbital Anisotropy</h2>
<p>
  In the <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">Coma Cluster</a>, Pedratti et al. (2026) find that <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">blue cloud</a> galaxies have strongly radial <a href="/pages/orbital-anisotropy-in-galaxy-clusters" class="wikilink">orbital anisotropy</a> (β &gt; 0) and non-Gaussian velocity distributions, while red sequence galaxies are isotropic and virialised <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>. This supports the evolutionary picture: blue infalling galaxies on radial orbits experience intense ram-pressure, lose their gas, transit through the green valley, and settle onto the red sequence as quiescent systems <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Mamon et al. (2019) found similar trends in stacked WINGS clusters, with blue galaxies more radially anisotropic in the outer regions <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>. Biviano et al. (2024) confirmed that ram-pressure stripped members exhibit radial anisotropy <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="observational-evidence">Observational Evidence</h2>
<p>
  Ram-pressure stripping produces dramatic observational signatures including gas tails, truncated gas disks, and enhanced <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> at leading edges. The Virgo cluster has been extensively studied for these signatures <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. Dense cluster environments shape galaxy properties through these environmental interactions <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>.
</p>
