<div class="key-takeaways">
  <div class="key-takeaways-title">Key Takeaways</div>
  <h4>Main Results</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Semi-analytic model extends Bromberg et al. (2011) jet-cocoon hydrodynamics to the sub-relativistic regime, deriving cocoon pressure P<sub>c</sub> ∝ L<sub>j</sub><sup>2/5</sup> ρ<sub>A</sub><sup>3/5</sup> t<sup>−4/5</sup> and recollimation shock location as a function of jet luminosity <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
    <li>Oblique recollimation shocks retain strong normal-component compression ratios sufficient for efficient DSA, with acceleration efficiency parametrized by shock inclination angle ψ <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
    <li>Predicted maximum proton energies span three orders of magnitude by source class: protostellar jets up to TeV (HH80-81 prototype <a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a>), microquasars up to tens of PeV (SS433 <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>), and Seyfert jets PeV–EeV under Bohm diffusion (NGC 4151 <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>, NGC 1068 <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
    <li>Escaping protons diffusing into the jet cocoon produce hadronic gamma-ray and neutrino signatures, potentially explaining TeV detections by HAWC <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>, H.E.S.S. <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>, and LHAASO <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> around known microquasars and Seyferts <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  </ul>
  <h4>Implications</h4>
  <p>This work elevates recollimation shocks from a niche hydrodynamic feature to a potentially dominant particle acceleration site across multiple jet classes. If the predicted PeV–EeV proton energies in Seyfert jets are confirmed, sub-relativistic AGN jets become serious contributors to the extragalactic cosmic-ray budget — reshaping how we interpret stationary bright knots in VLBI maps and broadband jet SEDs. For galaxy evolution, the implied hadronic energy injection into jet cocoons adds a non-thermal feedback channel that could influence circumgalactic gas heating.</p>
  <h4>Other Works</h4>
  <p>The analytic framework builds directly on the relativistic jet-cocoon model of Bromberg et al. (2011) and aligns with proposals that microquasars are Galactic PeVatrons. The oblique-shock acceleration picture complements earlier numerical work by Gómez et al. (1995) and Komissarov &amp; Falle (1997) on standing shocks in jets, while competing with internal-shock and magnetic-reconnection models for the dominant nonthermal emission mechanism. Recent HAWC and LHAASO detections around SS433 provide the most immediate observational test bed.</p>
</div>
<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>
  This paper develops a semi-analytic framework for <a href="/pages/diffusive-shock-acceleration" class="wikilink">diffusive shock acceleration (DSA)</a> at <a href="/pages/recollimation-shocks" class="wikilink">recollimation shocks</a> in sub-relativistic jets, extending the <a href="/pages/bromberg-2011" class="wikilink">Bromberg et al. (2011)</a> analytic <a href="/pages/jet-cocoon-dynamics" class="wikilink">jet-cocoon model</a> to the non-relativistic regime <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The authors solve a spatially-dependent transport equation to predict particle spectra and maximum energies for three jet classes: Seyfert galaxies, microquasars, and protostellar jets <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="key-findings">Key Findings</h2>
<h3>Maximum Particle Energies</h3>
<p>
  Predicted maximum proton energies depend on source class and <a href="/pages/turbulence-and-diffusion" class="wikilink">diffusion regime</a> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Seyfert galaxies (SEY_0):</strong> PeV–EeV under Bohm diffusion; parameters from NGC 4151 <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a> and NGC 1068 <a href="#ref4">[4]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Microquasars (MQ_0):</strong> Up to tens of PeV; SS433 as prototype <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>, consistent with HAWC <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>, H.E.S.S. <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>, and LHAASO <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> detections</li>
  <li><strong>Protostellar jets (PS_0):</strong> Up to TeV; HH80-81 as prototype <a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  E_max scales approximately as ε_B^(1/2) ṁ^(1/2) u₀^(3/2) / θ₀ in the Bohm-limit Hillas estimate <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. Departures from Bohm diffusion modify the velocity scaling <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Diffusion Regime Dependence</h3>
<p>
  The <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> spectrum critically determines E_max <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Bohm (δ = 1):</strong> Most efficient scattering; highest E_max <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Kraichnan (δ = 1/2):</strong> Benchmark regime <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Kolmogorov (δ = 1/3):</strong> Least efficient; can reduce E_max by orders of magnitude <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  Diffusion coefficient forms are justified by quasi-linear theory and pitch-angle scattering studies <a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a>. Alternative mechanisms such as mirror diffusion <a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a> motivate exploring multiple scalings <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Confinement Limits</h3>
<p>
  Upstream confinement — where the particle diffusion length exceeds the shock height — typically sets E_max <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Downstream turbulence decay introduces a competing limit: if turbulence decays rapidly behind the shock, particles escape downstream before reaching maximum energy <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The dominant constraint depends on the specific source parameters and diffusion regime <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="methodology">Methodology</h2>
<h3>Jet-Cocoon Hydrodynamics</h3>
<p>
  The paper extends the Bromberg et al. (2011) analytic jet-cocoon framework <a href="#ref17">[17]</a> to sub-relativistic velocities (u₀ ~ 0.01–0.3c) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This builds on earlier analytic work by Krause (2003) <a href="#ref18">[18]</a> and draws on the wind-blown bubble analogy of Weaver et al. (1977) <a href="#ref19">[19]</a>. The model derives:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>Cocoon pressure P_c(t) ∝ t^(-4/5) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref17">[17]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a></li>
  <li>Jet-head position z_h(t) ∝ t^(3/5) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></li>
  <li>Recollimation-shock location ẑ(t) and oblique compression ratio via k_p parameter <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  Early hydrodynamic simulations by Norman et al. (1982) first identified cocoons and shocked environments <a href="#ref20">[20]</a>. MHD simulations by Mizuno et al. (2015) <a href="#ref21">[21]</a>, Moll et al. (2008) <a href="#ref22">[22]</a>, and Bodo et al. (2018) <a href="#ref23">[23]</a> inform the role of <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> geometry, kink instabilities, and radiative cooling on recollimation shock properties. Giannios (2006) justified the hydrodynamic approximation by showing Poynting flux dissipates rapidly, leaving jets kinetically dominated at large scales <a href="#ref24">[24]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Transport Equation</h3>
<p>
  A semi-analytic 1D spatially-dependent transport equation describes particle injection at the shock, upstream diffusion, and downstream advection <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The methodology follows precedents set by Morlino et al. (2021) for wind termination shocks <a href="#ref25">[25]</a> and prior applications by Peretti et al. (2022, 2023) and Mukhopadhyay (2023) in related systems <a href="#ref26">[26]</a><a href="#ref27">[27]</a><a href="#ref28">[28]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  An iterative algorithm couples the transport solution to the shock geometry, yielding a shock spectrum f_sh(p) with cutoff functions that encode the confinement physics <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="context">Literature Context</h2>
<h3>Jet Launching and Shock Theory</h3>
<p>
  The paper situates itself within a rich history of jet physics. Energy extraction from compact objects was first proposed by Penrose (1969) <a href="#ref29">[29]</a>. The Blandford-Znajek mechanism <a href="#ref30">[30]</a> and magneto-centrifugal disk winds <a href="#ref31">[31]</a> provide the standard jet-launching frameworks, supported by early MHD simulations <a href="#ref32">[32]</a> and modern GRMHD work showing magnetically arrested disks produce powerful jets <a href="#ref33">[33]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The connection between shocks and jet emission was established by Blandford &amp; Rees (1974) <a href="#ref34">[34]</a> and Scheuer (1974) <a href="#ref35">[35]</a>. Shock propagation and standing-shock patterns in jets were studied extensively by Marscher &amp; Gear (1985) <a href="#ref36">[36]</a>, Daly &amp; Marscher (1988) <a href="#ref37">[37]</a>, Gómez et al. (1995, 1997, 2000) <a href="#ref38">[38]</a><a href="#ref39">[39]</a><a href="#ref40">[40]</a>, and Komissarov &amp; Falle (1997) <a href="#ref41">[41]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Observational Motivation</h3>
<p>
  <strong>Microquasars:</strong> Discovery of superluminal motion by Mirabel &amp; Rodríguez (1994, 1998) <a href="#ref42">[42]</a><a href="#ref43">[43]</a> established microquasars as relativistic jet sources. Extensive modeling of microquasar cosmic-ray and gamma-ray production followed <a href="#ref44">[44]</a><a href="#ref45">[45]</a><a href="#ref46">[46]</a><a href="#ref47">[47]</a><a href="#ref48">[48]</a><a href="#ref49">[49]</a><a href="#ref50">[50]</a><a href="#ref51">[51]</a>. Recent high-energy detections — HAWC at SS433 <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>, H.E.S.S. at SS433 <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>, LHAASO Galactic sources <a href="#ref9">[9]</a> — along with studies by Abaroa et al. (2024) <a href="#ref52">[52]</a> and Bykov et al. (2025) <a href="#ref53">[53]</a> reinforce microquasars as PeVatron candidates.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Seyfert galaxies:</strong> Ulvestad &amp; Wilson (2005) <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>, Williams &amp; Bower (2017) <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>, and Salvatore et al. (2023) <a href="#ref4">[4]</a> provide the observational basis for Seyfert jet parameters.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>Protostellar jets:</strong> Rodríguez-Kamenetzky et al. (2017) <a href="#ref10">[10]</a> and Carrasco-González et al. (2021) <a href="#ref11">[11]</a> detected synchrotron emission from protostellar jets. Padovani et al. (2015) <a href="#ref54">[54]</a>, Araudo et al. (2021) <a href="#ref55">[55]</a>, and de Oña Wilhelmi et al. (2023) <a href="#ref56">[56]</a> modeled non-thermal processes in these systems.
</p>
<h2 id="supports">What This Paper Supports</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Bromberg-style analytic jet-cocoon modeling as a useful framework in the sub-relativistic regime <a href="#ref17">[17]</a></li>
  <li>Observational interpretations that microquasars and Seyfert jets can accelerate particles to PeV energies, consistent with HAWC/H.E.S.S./LHAASO detections <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></li>
  <li>Wind-bubble analogies for deriving self-similar cocoon scalings <a href="#ref19">[19]</a><a href="#ref25">[25]</a></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="nuances">What This Paper Nuances or Challenges</h2>
<ul>
  <li>The paper highlights that <strong>not all jet shocks are efficient PeV accelerators</strong>: Kolmogorov-like diffusion can severely limit E_max in low-power/low-velocity jets, contrasting optimistic universal Bohm assumptions in some prior phenomenology <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li>Relativistic-shock DSA inefficiency motivates focusing on sub-relativistic jets as acceleration sites — a shift from the common emphasis on relativistic shocks <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li>Oblique-shock effects remain incompletely captured: the 1D model omits guide-field physics studied by Kirk &amp; Heavens (1989) <a href="#ref57">[57]</a>, Gieseler et al. (1999) <a href="#ref58">[58]</a>, Bell (2011) <a href="#ref59">[59]</a>, Kirk et al. (2023) <a href="#ref60">[60]</a>, and Reville et al. (2025) <a href="#ref61">[61]</a></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="limitations">Model Limitations</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>1D treatment:</strong> Neglects lateral transport and multi-dimensional effects <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Simplified obliquity:</strong> Guide-field effects not fully captured <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref57">[57]</a><a href="#ref59">[59]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Quasi-stationary assumption:</strong> Does not model jet variability or time-dependent shocks <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Sub-relativistic only:</strong> Relativistic generalization needed for fast AGN/blazar jets; Perucho et al. (2007, 2014, 2022) provide relevant simulations <a href="#ref62">[62]</a><a href="#ref63">[63]</a><a href="#ref64">[64]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Cocoon microphysics:</strong> Jet-head details and particle escape into the cocoon are treated simply <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
</ul>
