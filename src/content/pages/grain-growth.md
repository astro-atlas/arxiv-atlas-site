<p>
  <strong>Grain growth</strong> is the process by which <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">interstellar dust</a> grains increase in size and mass through the accretion of gas-phase metals and coagulation in the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a>. Together with stellar dust production (from supernova ejecta, AGB star winds, and Wolf-Rayet stars), grain growth is one of the two primary channels by which the dust mass of a galaxy increases over time. The relative importance of these channels &mdash; and the timescales involved &mdash; is a key question for understanding dust buildup at <a href="/pages/cosmic-dawn" class="wikilink">cosmic dawn</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="mechanisms">Mechanisms</h2>
<p>
  Grain growth in the ISM operates through two main channels:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Accretion of gas-phase metals:</strong> Refractory elements (Fe, Si, Mg, C) condense onto pre-existing grain surfaces in cold, dense ISM regions. The efficiency depends strongly on gas density, metallicity, and temperature &mdash; proceeding fastest in molecular clouds where densities are highest<a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Grain coagulation:</strong> Collisions between grains lead to sticking and growth of larger aggregates, particularly in dense environments. This process shifts the grain size distribution toward larger particles.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  Grain growth is counteracted by <strong>grain destruction</strong> processes: sputtering in hot gas, shattering in grain-grain collisions driven by shocks, and sublimation near radiation sources. The net dust mass evolution depends on the balance between growth and destruction<a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="timescales">Timescale Constraints</h2>
<p>
  Classical ISM grain growth models predict that the characteristic growth timescale scales inversely with metallicity and gas density: &tau;<sub>grow</sub> &prop; (Z &middot; n)<sup>&minus;1</sup>. In Milky Way-like conditions, &tau;<sub>grow</sub> ~ 10<sup>7</sup>&ndash;10<sup>8</sup> yr in molecular clouds. However, in low-metallicity, low-density environments expected at z &gt; 10, growth timescales can be significantly longer<a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Cosmological simulations with dust evolution predict that bulk dust mass growth through ISM grain accretion requires relatively high metallicities and densities, implying a delayed rise in dust content and suggesting that heavily obscured galaxies should be extremely rare at z &gt; 10<a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="challenge">Challenge from Cosmic Dawn</h2>
<p>
  The spectroscopic confirmation of EGS-z11-R0 at z = 11.45 with A<sub>V</sub> ~ 1 mag of dust attenuation<a href="#ref2">[2]</a> poses a significant challenge to slow grain growth models. Only ~400 Myr after the Big Bang, this galaxy already contained substantial <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> reservoirs, with direct spectroscopic evidence for iron enrichment ([Fe V] detection) confirming the availability of refractory elements for grain formation<a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. Additional photometric candidates at z ~ 12 from Mitsuhashi+2025<a href="#ref3">[3]</a> and Donnan+2025<a href="#ref4">[4]</a> suggest that dust-reddened systems may not be as rare as predicted.
</p>
<p>
  These observations point toward alternative rapid dust production channels at early times: efficient grain formation in supernova ejecta, rapid grain growth in the dense compact environments typical of <a href="/pages/red-monsters" class="wikilink">red monsters</a> (where high gas densities could accelerate accretion timescales), or contributions from very massive or Population III stars. Disentangling these channels is a key goal for understanding galaxy evolution at <a href="/pages/cosmic-dawn" class="wikilink">cosmic dawn</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.
</p>
