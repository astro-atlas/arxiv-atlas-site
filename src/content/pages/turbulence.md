<p>
  <strong>Turbulence</strong> — chaotic, multi-scale fluid motion — is ubiquitous in astrophysical systems. In the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a>, turbulence is <strong>supersonic</strong> (velocity dispersions exceed the sound speed) and <strong>magnetized</strong> (coupled to <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> via MHD). It plays a dual role in <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>: providing large-scale support against <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravitational collapse</a> while creating local density enhancements that seed collapse.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Properties in Molecular Clouds</h2>
<p>
  In <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a>, turbulence is observed as non-thermal line broadening — velocity dispersions significantly exceeding the thermal width. The non-thermal component is extracted as ΔV_nt² = ΔV² − 8ln2 kT/m, where m is the molecular mass<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. In G351.77-0.53, the mean non-thermal velocity dispersion from C¹⁸O is ~1.47 km/s, far exceeding the thermal width of ~0.15 km/s<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  Turbulence follows a power spectrum with energy cascading from large injection scales (driven by supernovae, galactic shear, or gravitational infall) down to dissipation scales. The velocity-size relation σ_v ∝ L^(0.5) (Larson's relations) reflects this cascade in molecular clouds.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Turbulence vs. Magnetic Fields vs. Gravity</h2>
<p>
  The relative importance of turbulence, <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a>, and <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a> determines the outcome of cloud evolution. This is quantified through energy budgets: the kinetic energy E_K = Mσ_V², magnetic energy E_B = B²V/8π, and gravitational energy E_G = GM²/L<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  MHD simulations show that the interplay of turbulence and magnetic fields controls filament formation, the B-field orientation relative to density structures, and the rate of fragmentation<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. The polarization morphology of <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">dust emission</a> is directly imprinted by the magnetization level of the turbulent cloud<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Role in the DCF Method</h2>
<p>
  Turbulence is a key input to the <a href="/pages/davis-chandrasekhar-fermi-method" class="wikilink">Davis-Chandrasekhar-Fermi method</a>: the B-field strength is estimated from the ratio of turbulent velocity dispersion to polarization angle dispersion. Higher turbulence produces larger angle dispersions for a given field strength. The accuracy of DCF depends critically on correctly measuring the turbulent component and separating it from ordered motions and stellar feedback<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
