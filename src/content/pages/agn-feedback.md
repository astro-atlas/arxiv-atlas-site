<p>
  <strong>AGN feedback</strong> is the process by which energy and momentum released by an active galactic nucleus regulate the gas supply of its host galaxy and surrounding environment. It is invoked as the primary mechanism preventing runaway cooling in massive galaxies and galaxy clusters, solving the "cooling flow problem" and explaining why the most massive galaxies are red and dead <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="modes">Feedback Modes</h2>
<p>
  AGN feedback operates in two broad regimes <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Kinetic (radio/maintenance) mode:</strong> At low accretion rates (L<sub>bol</sub>/L<sub>Edd</sub> &lesssim; 1%), relativistic jets inflate cavities and radio bubbles in the surrounding hot gas, injecting mechanical energy that offsets radiative cooling. This mode operates in massive ellipticals and BCGs with hot, <a href="/pages/bondi-accretion" class="wikilink">Bondi-fed</a> accretion flows <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Radiative (quasar/wind) mode:</strong> At high accretion rates, radiation-driven winds and outflows expel gas from the host galaxy, quenching <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>. This mode operates during luminous quasar phases <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  NGC 1275, the BCG of the <a href="/pages/perseus-cluster" class="wikilink">Perseus cluster</a>, accretes at L<sub>bol</sub>/L<sub>Edd</sub> &asymp; 0.04&ndash;0.09% — firmly in the kinetic feedback regime <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. At such low Eddington ratios, the accretion flow transitions from a thin disk to a radiatively inefficient accretion flow (RIAF) <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="cavities">Jet-Driven Cavities and Radio Bubbles</h2>
<p>
  The most direct evidence for kinetic AGN feedback comes from X-ray cavities coinciding with radio lobes in cool-core clusters <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. These cavities are regions of low X-ray surface brightness where the ICM has been displaced by relativistic plasma from the AGN jet <a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The Perseus cluster is the textbook example: <em>Chandra</em> observations reveal multiple generations of cavities inflated by the <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84</a> jet, with mechanical power of ~10<sup>44</sup>&ndash;10<sup>45</sup> erg/s — comparable to the ICM radiative cooling luminosity <a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. This energy balance constitutes the self-regulating feedback loop: cooling gas fuels accretion, accretion powers jets, jets heat the gas <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Beyond cavities, additional heating channels include weak <a href="/pages/shock-waves" class="wikilink">shock waves</a> driven by jet inflation, sound waves propagating through the ICM, and turbulent mixing as buoyant bubbles rise <a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="heating-cooling">Heating–Cooling Balance</h2>
<p>
  In cool-core clusters, the ICM cooling time within the central ~50&ndash;100 kpc falls below 10<sup>9</sup> years. Without feedback, this predicts mass deposition rates of hundreds of M<sub>&odot;</sub>/yr, but X-ray spectroscopy and star formation measurements show actual rates 10&ndash;100 times lower <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. The resolution is that AGN mechanical energy, distributed through cavities, shocks, and <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>, approximately balances radiative losses <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The feedback cycle is not perfectly smooth — it involves episodic jet activity, multiphase gas condensation, and chaotic cold accretion <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. Gas cooling below ~10<sup>7</sup> K precipitates into cold clouds and filaments that rain onto the central AGN, triggering new jet episodes <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. In NGC 1275, this manifests as the spectacular emission-line filament network extending over 100 kpc <a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="magnetic-fields"><a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">Magnetic fields</a> and Feedback</h2>
<p>
  Magnetic fields play a key role in the feedback cycle. The optical filaments in NGC 1275 are threaded by ~24 &mu;G fields that support the gas against gravitational collapse <a href="#ref14">[14]</a>. These filaments are dragged outward by rising radio bubbles, redistributing magnetic flux and cold gas through the ICM <a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a> measurements probe the magnetic field structure at all scales. Park et al. (2026) showed that the ambient magnetic field within the Bondi radius of NGC 1275 follows B &prop; r<sup>&minus;1.7 to &minus;2.2</sup>, providing the first spatially resolved measurement of the field connecting the accreting gas to the jet-launching region <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Understanding how this field threads between the accretion flow, jet, and ICM is central to completing the feedback picture.
</p>
<h2 id="quenching">Star Formation Quenching</h2>
<p>
  On galactic scales, AGN feedback is the leading mechanism invoked to explain the red colors and low star formation rates of massive early-type galaxies <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. Cosmological simulations require AGN feedback to reproduce the observed galaxy luminosity function, the bimodality between blue star-forming and red quiescent galaxies, and the scaling relations between black holes and their hosts <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  In the kinetic mode, jet heating prevents the ICM from cooling and collapsing into stars — a "maintenance" mechanism that keeps massive galaxies quenched after an initial quasar-mode blowout <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. The detailed physics of how jet energy thermalizes and couples to the gas remains an active area of research, with Perseus serving as the primary laboratory <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.
</p>
