<p>
  <strong>Star formation</strong> is the process by which dense regions within <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a> collapse under <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">self-gravity</a> to form new stars. It is the fundamental process linking the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a> to stellar populations, governing galaxy evolution by converting gas into stars and returning processed material through stellar feedback.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">The Collapse Paradigm</h2>
<p>
  Star formation begins when a region of a molecular cloud becomes gravitationally unstable — when the inward pull of <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a> overcomes the outward support from thermal pressure, <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>, and <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a>. The critical mass for collapse is described by the Jeans mass, which depends on temperature and density.
</p>
<p>
  Modern understanding recognizes that star formation proceeds through a <strong>filament-to-core pathway</strong>: the ISM first organizes into <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filamentary structures</a>, within which dense cores condense and eventually collapse<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Magnetic fields play a critical role in regulating this process, as quantified by the <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Low-Mass vs. High-Mass Star Formation</h2>
<p>
  <strong>Low-mass star formation</strong> (M &lt; 8 M☉) is relatively well understood: an isolated core contracts quasi-statically, forms a protostellar disk, and accretes material while driving bipolar outflows. The process takes ~10⁵–10⁶ years from core collapse to a T Tauri star.
</p>
<p>
  <strong>High-mass star formation</strong> (M &gt; 8 M☉) remains more debated. Massive stars form in clustered environments within <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">infrared dark clouds (IRDCs)</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup> and hub-filament systems<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. The earliest stages are traced by IRDCs — dense, cold clouds seen in absorption against the mid-infrared Galactic background<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. Active star formation is confirmed by the presence of Class I young stellar objects (YSOs), molecular outflows, and masers<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Regulation by Magnetic Fields and Turbulence</h2>
<p>
  The rate and efficiency of star formation are regulated by the interplay of <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a>, <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a>, and <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>. In magnetically subcritical clouds (where B-fields dominate), collapse can only proceed via ambipolar diffusion — the slow drift of neutral gas through the ion-field coupling<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. In supercritical clouds, gravity overwhelms magnetic support and collapse proceeds more rapidly.
</p>
<p>
  Energy budget analyses in star-forming clouds such as G351.77-0.53 show that gravitational, kinetic, and magnetic energies can be comparable, confirming that all three forces are dynamically important<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-cluster">Clustered Star Formation and Structural Evolution</h2>
<p>
  Most stars form in <a href="/pages/star-cluster-formation" class="wikilink">clustered environments</a>. Young clusters inherit <a href="/pages/fractal-structure-star-clusters" class="wikilink">fractal spatial structure</a> from their parent molecular clouds, reflecting the turbulent fragmentation of the gas. This substructure is typically erased by <a href="/pages/dynamical-relaxation-clusters" class="wikilink">dynamical relaxation</a> within approximately 2.5 free-fall times (t<sub>ff</sub>), after which clusters transition to smooth, centrally concentrated morphologies<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>. <a href="/pages/stellar-feedback" class="wikilink">Stellar feedback</a> from massive stars can stochastically trigger secondary <a href="/pages/subcluster-formation" class="wikilink">subcluster formation</a>, prolonging the fractal phase beyond 4 t<sub>ff</sub> in some cases<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-3">Evolutionary Tracers</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>YSO classification</strong> — Class 0/I (embedded protostars), Class II (disk-dominated T Tauri), Class III (weak-line T Tauri); identified via infrared color-color diagrams<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Molecular outflows</strong> — SiO, CO high-velocity emission traces protostellar jets</li>
  <li><strong>Masers</strong> — CH₃OH, H₂O masers indicate high-mass protostellar activity<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Radio continuum</strong> — free-free emission from HII regions marks the arrival of ionizing radiation from massive stars</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-interaction">Interaction-Triggered Star Formation</h2>
<p>
  Beyond the quiescent filament-to-core pathway described above, <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> can also be triggered on galactic scales by <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy mergers</a> and collisions. During close encounters, tidal torques and collisional <a href="/pages/shock-waves" class="wikilink">shocks</a> compress gas across kpc-scale regions, driving substantial starbursts<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  A striking example is <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring galaxies</a>, where a head-on galaxy encounter launches an expanding density wave that sweeps up gas and triggers star formation at the ring front. In "Pika's Halo," the collisional ring hosting SN 2025adpq, ongoing star formation is observed at SFR ~ 0.91 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr in the ring at subsolar gas-phase metallicity (~0.6 Z<sub>&#9737;</sub>), driven by the propagating density wave from a <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">major merger</a><sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup>. Emission lines ([O II], [O III], H&#946;, H&#945;) extend ~24 kpc along the ring arc<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  Interaction-triggered star formation represents a fundamentally different mode from quiescent cloud collapse: it operates on galaxy-wide scales, can occur in regions that would not otherwise form stars, and involves compression by dynamical (rather than gravitational) instabilities. This mode is especially important at high redshifts where merger rates are elevated<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>.
</p>
