<p>
  <strong>Large-offset supernovae</strong> are transient events found at unusually large projected distances from the nearest identified galaxy nucleus, typically &gt;10 kpc. While some fraction arise from <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia</a> progenitors in extended stellar halos or intracluster populations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>, others reside in tidal structures produced by <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy mergers</a><sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Formation Channels</h2>
<p>
  Several mechanisms can produce large-offset SNe:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Tidal stripping:</strong> Stars redistributed into tidal tails and bridges during <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy interactions</a> can host SNe at tens of kpc from the host nucleus<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">Collisional ring</a> displacement:</strong> Old stellar populations swept outward by density waves in CRGs can produce <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SNe</a> within the expanding ring. SN 2025adpq at 11.4 kpc from G1 is a clear example<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Intracluster stellar populations:</strong> In galaxy clusters, tidally stripped stars form a diffuse intracluster component that can host SNe at &gt;100 kpc from any cluster galaxy<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Progenitor redistribution:</strong> Galaxy interactions can redistribute old <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">SN Ia</a> progenitor systems to large radii, allowing explosions far from the progenitor birthplace<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Offset Ranges</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">Collisional rings</a> can plausibly explain offsets of tens of kpc (ring radii typically 5&ndash;15 kpc)<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. More extreme offsets (&gt;100 kpc), such as the case of SN 2021hem, are unlikely to be explained by CRG displacement alone and likely require intracluster or hypervelocity progenitor explanations<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  Large-offset SNe challenge the standard assumption that a supernova's environment reflects its progenitor formation site. For <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia cosmology</a>, host-galaxy-dependent corrections depend on accurate host identification. When SNe reside in faint tidal structures or <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional rings</a>, the true host may be misidentified or missed entirely, introducing systematic biases<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
