<div class="key-takeaways">
  <div class="key-takeaways-title">Key Takeaways</div>
  <h4>Main Results</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>The M51 group shows signatures of more advanced dynamical evolution than the M101 group, with flattened age&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> and nebular metallicity&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> relations, enhanced dust content, and environmental quenching in some members<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>The interacting pair M51a/b displays sharply distinct properties: M51a has prominent star-forming spiral arms with young populations, while M51b is a fully retired <a href="/pages/early-type-galaxies" class="wikilink">early-type galaxy</a> &mdash; a clean example of interaction-driven divergence within a single system<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>M63 exhibits outside-in <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a> signatures (asymmetric stellar age, dust attenuation, and H&#945; EW maps), likely driven by a past interaction rather than classical inside-out evolution<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>All star-forming galaxies in both groups follow the resolved star-forming main sequence, with M51b the sole exception as a retired system<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>J-PLUS 12-band photometric datacubes processed with AlStar can deliver IFS-like spatially resolved stellar population and emission-line maps for nearby galaxies<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.</li>
  </ul>
  <h4>Implications</h4>
  <p>This paper demonstrates that even low-mass <a href="/pages/galaxy-groups" class="wikilink">galaxy groups</a> can exhibit significant environmental processing of their members, challenging the view that such effects are limited to massive clusters. The contrast between M51 (interaction-processed, flattened gradients, quenched members) and M101 (largely pristine inside-out formation) provides a compelling case study for how group dynamics shape galaxy evolution. The J-PLUS methodology opens a path to extending IFS-like analyses across much larger galaxy samples than traditional integral field surveys.</p>
  <h4>Other works</h4>
  <p>This study builds on the CALIFA IFS results (González Delgado et al. 2014, 2015, 2016<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>; García-Benito et al. 2017<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>) that established spatially resolved scaling relations for stellar populations. The environmental dependence connects to Kauffmann et al. (2004)<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup> and the quenching literature (Bluck et al. 2020<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>). The M51 system has been extensively studied (Dobbs et al. 2010<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>; Mentuch Cooper et al. 2012<sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>), but this is the first uniform multi-band resolved analysis of the entire group compared to another (M101; Thainá-Batista et al. 2023<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>). The resolved SFMS framework follows Sánchez et al. (2020)<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>, while outside-in quenching adds to evidence for diverse quenching pathways (Tacchella et al. 2015<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup>; Nelson et al. 2021<sup><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>).</p>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>Thainá-Batista et al. (2026) characterize the spatially resolved stellar population and emission-line properties of galaxies in the M51 group using multi-band datacubes from the Javalambre Photometric Local Universe Survey (J-PLUS). The same AlStar spectral fitting methodology previously applied to the M101 group<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup> is used, enabling a direct and fair comparison between two nearby <a href="/pages/galaxy-groups" class="wikilink">galaxy groups</a> with different interaction histories<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The M51 group displays clear signatures of environmental processing: flattened age and <a href="/pages/metallicity-gradients" class="wikilink">nebular metallicity gradients</a>, enhanced <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> content, and quenching in some members. The interacting pair M51a/b shows dramatic property contrasts &mdash; star-forming spiral arms in M51a versus a retired companion in M51b. M63 shows asymmetric quenching consistent with a past interaction. In contrast, the M101 group largely preserves the steep radial gradients expected from undisturbed inside-out galaxy formation<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The study highlights J-PLUS's potential for IFS-like analyses of nearby galaxies and suggests that group mass and interactions influence galaxy evolution even in low-mass environments, though the comparison of only two systems limits generalizability<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="findings">Key Findings</h2>
<h3>M51a/b: Interaction-driven property divergence</h3>
<p>The interacting pair M51a (NGC 5194) and M51b (NGC 5195) displays clearly distinct properties in all resolved maps<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. M51a shows prominent <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star-forming</a> spiral arms with young stellar populations, high H&#945; equivalent widths, and enhanced <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust attenuation</a>. M51b, despite being physically connected to M51a, is essentially an <a href="/pages/early-type-galaxies" class="wikilink">early-type retired galaxy</a> with old stellar populations and negligible current star formation<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. This pair provides a textbook example of how <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy interactions</a> can produce divergent evolutionary outcomes.</p>
<h3>M63: Outside-in quenching</h3>
<p>M63 (NGC 5055) exhibits asymmetries in stellar age, dust attenuation, and H&#945; equivalent width that are consistent with outside-in <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a><sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. This pattern &mdash; where the outer regions quench before the inner disk &mdash; is the opposite of classical inside-out formation and likely relates to a past interaction event. This finding adds to evidence that quenching pathways are diverse and environment-dependent<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>Flattened scaling relations in the M51 group</h3>
<p>The age&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> and nebular metallicity&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> relations in the M51 group are flatter than in the M101 group<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. This flattening is interpreted as evidence of more advanced dynamical evolution: interactions mix stellar populations and redistribute metals, erasing the steep gradients that reflect pristine inside-out disk growth. The M101 group, being less dynamically evolved, preserves these original gradients<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>Resolved star-forming main sequence</h3>
<p>All star-forming galaxies in both groups align with the resolved star-forming main sequence (RSFMS), confirming its universality at sub-galactic scales<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>. The exception is M51b, which falls below the RSFMS in the regime of retired galaxies. This demonstrates that the RSFMS holds even in group environments with active interactions<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="methodology">Methodology</h2>
<p>The study uses J-PLUS (Javalambre Photometric Local Universe Survey) data consisting of 12 photometric bands (7 narrowband + 5 broadband filters) covering 3500&ndash;10000 &#197;<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>. Multi-band datacubes are constructed for each galaxy and processed with the AlStar spectral fitting code, which fits stellar population models (Bruzual &amp; Charlot 2003<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup> with Chabrier 2003 IMF<sup><a href="#ref18">[18]</a></sup>) and Calzetti et al. (2000)<sup><a href="#ref19">[19]</a></sup> dust attenuation to each spatial element<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>Emission lines (H&#945;, H&#946;, [O III], [N II]) are extracted from narrowband filter residuals after stellar continuum subtraction. The <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">BPT diagnostic diagram</a> (Baldwin et al. 1981<sup><a href="#ref20">[20]</a></sup>) is used to classify ionization sources. Nebular metallicities are derived from strong-line calibrations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. The identical methodology was previously applied to the M101 group<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>, ensuring a consistent comparison.</p>
<h2 id="literature">Literature Context</h2>
<p>This work extends the CALIFA tradition of spatially resolved stellar population analysis<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup> to photometric survey data, trading spectral resolution for spatial coverage. The M51 system is among the most studied interacting pairs (Dobbs et al. 2010<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>; Tress et al. 2020<sup><a href="#ref21">[21]</a></sup>), but this paper provides the first uniform comparison of its resolved properties against another group using identical methods.</p>
<p>The environmental dependence of galaxy properties in groups connects to a broad literature on quenching mechanisms, including Kauffmann et al. (2004)<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup> on environmental modulation of the mass&ndash;SFR relation, Bluck et al. (2020)<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup> on central velocity dispersion as a quenching predictor, and Tacchella et al. (2015)<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup> on inside-out quenching. The study also contributes to growing interest in J-PLUS for extragalactic science (Rodríguez-Martín et al. 2025<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>; San Roman et al. 2019<sup><a href="#ref22">[22]</a></sup>).</p>
<h2 id="supports">What This Paper Supports</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">Resolved star-forming main sequence</a>:</strong> Star-forming members of both groups follow the RSFMS at sub-galactic scales<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Inside-out formation</strong> (in undisturbed systems): The M101 group preserves steep age and metallicity gradients consistent with inside-out disk growth<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">Galaxy interactions</a> drive environmental processing:</strong> The M51 group's flattened gradients, enhanced dust, and quenched members result from tidal interactions<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Environmental quenching operates in low-mass groups:</strong> Not limited to massive clusters<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="challenges">What This Paper Challenges</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Universal inside-out formation:</strong> Interaction-processed groups show flattened gradients, indicating inside-out signatures can be erased by environment<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Mass-only quenching models:</strong> Stellar mass surface density alone does not predict quenching; interaction history matters even in low-mass groups<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
