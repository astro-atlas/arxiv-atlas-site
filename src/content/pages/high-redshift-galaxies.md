<p>
  <strong>High-redshift galaxies</strong> are systems observed at redshifts z &gt; 6, corresponding to cosmic ages less than ~1 billion years after the Big Bang. Their study probes the earliest phases of galaxy formation, the buildup of stellar mass, the production of heavy elements and <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a>, and the process of <a href="/pages/cosmic-dawn" class="wikilink">cosmic reionization</a>. The launch of JWST in 2022 revolutionized this field, revealing an unexpectedly abundant and diverse population of galaxies at z &gt; 10<a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="jwst-revolution">The JWST Revolution at z &gt; 10</h2>
<p>
  Prior to JWST, galaxies beyond z ~ 10 were largely inaccessible. JWST&rsquo;s infrared sensitivity has uncovered a surprisingly luminous population at z = 10&ndash;16, with UV luminosity functions exceeding pre-launch model predictions<a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. Most of these galaxies exhibit extremely blue <a href="/pages/uv-slope" class="wikilink">UV slopes</a> (&beta;<sub>UV</sub> &lt; &minus;2.5), consistent with young, metal-poor stellar populations with minimal dust<a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>. Their high specific star formation rates suggest rapid early assembly<a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="population-diversity">Population Diversity: Red and Blue</h2>
<p>
  While the majority of z &gt; 10 galaxies are blue and dust-poor, JWST has revealed a minority population of red UV continuum sources that deviate significantly from the canonical <a href="/pages/mv-betauv-relation" class="wikilink">M<sub>UV</sub>&ndash;&beta;<sub>UV</sub> relation</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a>. These objects &mdash; termed &ldquo;<a href="/pages/red-monsters" class="wikilink">red monsters</a>&rdquo; in the Ferrara+2024 framework<a href="#ref9">[9]</a> &mdash; show red UV slopes (&beta;<sub>UV</sub> &gt; &minus;1.5) indicative of significant <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> attenuation. The spectroscopic confirmation of EGS-z11-R0 at z = 11.45 with A<sub>V</sub> ~ 1 mag established that chemically evolved, dust-enriched galaxies were already in place at <a href="/pages/cosmic-dawn" class="wikilink">cosmic dawn</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The coexistence of blue and red populations supports an evolutionary picture in which galaxies alternate between dust-obscured and dust-free phases, regulated by radiative feedback<a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. This diversity has direct implications for estimating the cosmic star formation rate density at z &gt; 10, as UV-selected samples may miss heavily obscured systems<a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="physical-properties">Physical Properties</h2>
<p>
  SED modeling of z &gt; 10 galaxies reveals stellar masses ranging from log(M<sub>*</sub>/M<sub>&odot;</sub>) ~ 7 to 10, with some systems achieving surprisingly high masses (&gt; 10<sup>9</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>) within only ~400 Myr<a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. Star formation rates span ~1&ndash;40 M<sub>&odot;</sub> yr<sup>&minus;1</sup>, with rising or constant star formation histories preferred over instantaneous bursts in many cases<a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">Emission-line diagnostics</a> reveal hard ionizing radiation fields, with UV line ratios (C IV/C III], C III]/He II) often falling in composite AGN/SF regions &mdash; reflecting a fundamental diagnostic degeneracy at these redshifts<a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="chemical-enrichment">Early Chemical Enrichment</h2>
<p>
  High-redshift galaxies show surprisingly rapid chemical enrichment. JWST/NIRSpec detections of iron emission lines at z &gt; 10 demonstrate that iron-peak elements &mdash; key constituents of <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust grains</a> &mdash; were already present in the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">ISM</a> within ~400 Myr<a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a>. Stellar iron abundances at z = 9&ndash;12 range from [Fe/H] ~ &minus;1 to solar, indicating that multiple generations of massive stars and supernovae had already enriched the gas<a href="#ref13">[13]</a>. This rapid enrichment supports models of prompt dust production and <a href="/pages/grain-growth" class="wikilink">grain growth</a>.
</p>
