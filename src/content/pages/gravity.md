<p>
  <strong>Gravitational collapse</strong> is the process by which a self-gravitating body contracts under its own gravity when internal support — from thermal pressure, <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>, or <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> — is insufficient to maintain equilibrium. In astrophysics, gravitational collapse is the fundamental mechanism driving <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>, from the contraction of <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular cloud</a> cores to the formation of galaxies and galaxy clusters.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Jeans Instability</h2>
<p>
  A uniform gas cloud becomes gravitationally unstable when its mass exceeds the <strong>Jeans mass</strong> M_J — the critical mass above which gravity overwhelms thermal pressure. For a cloud of temperature T and number density n, M_J scales as T^(3/2) n^(-1/2). Equivalently, collapse occurs on scales larger than the Jeans length λ_J.
</p>
<p>
  In practice, <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a> are not uniform or purely thermally supported. <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">Turbulence</a> provides additional support on large scales (but can also compress gas locally to trigger collapse), while <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> provide non-thermal support quantified by the <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Magnetic Support and the Critical Mass-to-Flux Ratio</h2>
<p>
  A magnetized cloud is <strong>subcritical</strong> when the <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a> λ &lt; 1 — magnetic pressure prevents collapse. It is <strong>supercritical</strong> when λ &gt; 1 and gravity dominates. Clouds near λ ~ 1 are <strong>transcritical</strong>, as observed in the IRDC G351.77-0.53 where λ ~ 0.9 ± 0.6<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  In subcritical clouds, collapse can still proceed via <strong>ambipolar diffusion</strong> — the slow drift of neutral gas through the magnetically coupled ion-electron fluid<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. As mass accumulates faster than flux can escape, the core transitions to supercritical and undergoes dynamic collapse.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Signatures of Collapse</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">Hourglass B-field morphology</a></strong> — gravitational contraction drags initially uniform field lines inward, creating a pinched geometry<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Infall velocity gradients</strong> — redshifted/blueshifted asymmetries in optically thick lines</li>
  <li><strong>Increasing mass-to-flux ratio</strong> — λ increases from cloud envelope to dense core as gravity wins<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Energy equipartition</strong> — gravitational, magnetic, and kinetic energies become comparable in actively collapsing regions<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
