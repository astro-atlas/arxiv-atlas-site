<div class="key-takeaways">
  <div class="key-takeaways-title">Key Takeaways</div>
  <h4>Main Results</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>SN 2025adpq is a normal <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernova</a> at z = 0.1540 discovered at 11.4 kpc projected offset from its host galaxy G1, located within a <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring</a> ("Pika's Halo") produced by a major merger between two ~10<sup>10.5</sup> M<sub>&#9737;</sub> galaxies<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>Spectroscopy reveals a dual stellar population in the ring: deep Ca H&amp;K absorption indicates old stars displaced from G1, while bright emission lines ([O II], H&#945;) show ongoing merger-triggered <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> at SFR ~0.91 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr with subsolar metallicity (~0.6 Z<sub>&#9737;</sub>)<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>The SN Ia most likely arose from an old progenitor (<a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay time</a> &ge;0.5 Gyr) stripped from G1 by the collisional pressure wave, given that the ring is only ~70&ndash;200 Myr old while G1 formed most of its stars ~11 Gyr ago<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li><a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">Collisional rings</a> provide a new pathway for producing <a href="/pages/large-offset-supernovae" class="wikilink">large-offset</a> and apparently <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless</a> Type Ia SNe by dynamically displacing long-lived progenitor systems from their parent galaxies<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>A systematic crossmatch of low-z CRG catalogs with TNS reveals CRGs as "supernova factories": 15 transients in 11 CRG systems, 3 hosting supernova siblings, with SN 2025adpq being only the 2nd classified Type Ia in a CRG ring<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>.</li>
    <li>At higher redshifts, surface brightness dimming would render collisional rings undetectable, causing these offset SNe to be misclassified as hostless &mdash; a systematic bias that Rubin LSST will help resolve<sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>.</li>
  </ul>
  <h4>Implications</h4>
  <p>This paper establishes <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring galaxies</a> as a previously unrecognized channel for producing large-offset <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernovae</a>, with direct implications for galaxy evolution studies. The demonstration that galaxy-galaxy collisions can <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamically displace</a> old stellar populations to tens of kpc from their birthplace challenges the assumption that SN Ia environments directly reflect their progenitor formation sites. For cosmological surveys using Type Ia SNe as standardizable candles, the systematic misclassification of CRG-offset SNe as "hostless" at higher redshifts could introduce biases in host-galaxy-dependent corrections. More broadly, the coexistence of old displaced stars and newly triggered <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> in collisional rings offers a unique laboratory for studying stellar population mixing, <a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay time distributions</a>, and the interplay between gravitational dynamics and star formation during <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy mergers</a>.</p>
  <h4>Other works</h4>
  <p>This paper builds on the theoretical framework of collisional ring formation (Lynds &amp; Toomre 1976<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>; Appleton &amp; Struck-Marcell 1996<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup>) and extends earlier observations of Type Ia SNe in tidal structures (Schweizer et al. 2008<sup><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>; Ferretti et al. 2017<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a></sup>) to the specific context of collisionally-driven density waves. The proposed <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> mechanism connects to the broader "<a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless SN Ia</a>" problem (Gal-Yam et al. 2003<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>; Sand et al. 2011<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>; Graham et al. 2015<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>; Qin et al. 2024<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup>), offering collisional rings as a complementary explanation alongside intracluster stars and ultra-diffuse dwarf hosts (Strolger et al. 2025<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>). The finding that CRGs are supernova factories aligns with studies showing elevated core-collapse SN rates in interacting galaxies (Habergham et al. 2010, 2012<sup><a href="#ref18">[18]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a></sup>; Michalowski et al. 2020<sup><a href="#ref20">[20]</a></sup>) and extends this to redistributed Type Ia progenitors (Ditrani et al. 2024<sup><a href="#ref21">[21]</a></sup>).</p>

<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>O'Connor et al. (2026) report the discovery and characterization of SN 2025adpq, a <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernova</a> at z = 0.1540 found within a <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring galaxy</a> system dubbed "Pika's Halo." The ring, with a circumference of ~70 kpc, was produced by a <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">major merger</a> between two galaxies of comparable mass (log(M<sub>*</sub>/M<sub>&#9737;</sub>) &asymp; 10.5)<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. The supernova lies at a projected offset of ~11.4 kpc from the nucleus of the primary galaxy G1, along the expanding ring<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>Multi-instrument spectroscopy with SALT/RSS and Gemini/GMOS-S reveals that the ring hosts both ongoing <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> (bright emission lines including [O II], [O III], H&#946;, and H&#945;) and a substantial old stellar population (deep Ca H&amp;K absorption)<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Given that <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SN</a> progenitors have <a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay times</a> &ge;0.5 Gyr<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup> and the ring is estimated to be only ~70&ndash;200 Myr old, the authors argue that SN 2025adpq most likely originated from an old progenitor system that was <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamically displaced</a> from G1 during the head-on collision<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="findings">Key Findings</h2>
<h3>Collisional ring identification and system properties</h3>
<p>The system is confirmed as a major merger (luminosity ratio L<sub>2</sub>/L<sub>1</sub> ~ 0.8) producing a collisional ring, not a gravitational lens<sup><a href="#ref22">[22]</a></sup>. Spectroscopy at consistent z ~ 0.1537&ndash;0.1540 for all components (SN, ring, G1, G2) rejects the lensing hypothesis<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. The ring has a projected semi-major axis of 10.5 kpc and semi-minor axis of 9.4 kpc, implying an inclination of ~26.5&deg;<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. A coherent velocity structure with &Delta;v ~ 350 km/s across a 70&deg; position-angle span is observed, consistent with an expanding ring with some residual rotation<sup><a href="#ref23">[23]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>Dual stellar populations in the ring</h3>
<p>Spectroscopy reveals coexisting old and young stellar populations within the ring<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. The Balmer decrement (H&#945;/H&#946; = 2.65 &plusmn; 0.13) is consistent with Case B recombination, and BPT ratios confirm star-forming ionization<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Gas-phase metallicity is subsolar at 12 + log(O/H) ~ 8.50 &plusmn; 0.05 (~0.6 Z<sub>&#9737;</sub>)<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>. SED modeling with Prospector confirms both galaxies formed the bulk of their stars at early times (~11 Gyr ago), with G1 SFR = 0.41 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr and G2 SFR = 0.10 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref24">[24]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>SN classification and light curve</h3>
<p>SN 2025adpq is classified as a normal <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia</a> via NGSF and SNAIL template matching, with best spectral match to SN 2008C at 15.4 days post-peak<sup><a href="#ref25">[25]</a><a href="#ref26">[26]</a></sup>. SALT2 light-curve fitting yields peak time ~7.5 &plusmn; 1.1 d before discovery, stretch x<sub>1</sub> = &minus;0.81 &plusmn; 0.31, and colour c = 0.07 &plusmn; 0.03 (&chi;<sup>2</sup>/dof = 18.6/12)<sup><a href="#ref27">[27]</a><a href="#ref28">[28]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>Ring age and dynamical displacement argument</h3>
<p>The ring age is estimated at 70&ndash;200 Myr based on the ring radius (~10.5 kpc) and expansion velocities of 50&ndash;150 km/s<sup><a href="#ref29">[29]</a><a href="#ref30">[30]</a></sup>. This is far shorter than the &ge;0.5 Gyr <a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay times</a> expected for most Type Ia progenitors<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. SED modeling shows G1 formed the bulk of its stars ~11 Gyr ago<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>, consistent with an old delayed progenitor channel. The collisional pressure wave both triggered new <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> and swept out pre-existing disk stars, as evidenced by Ca H&amp;K absorption in the ring<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h3>CRGs as supernova factories</h3>
<p>A crossmatch of the Madore et al. (2009) low-z CRG catalog<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup> with TNS identifies 15 transients within 30 kpc of 11 CRG systems (8% of the sample), with 7 in rings and 8 not in rings. Three CRG systems host supernova siblings. NGC 4774 is a "SN factory" with 3 transients all within its ring<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. SN 2025adpq is the 8th classified SN in a CRG ring and only the 2nd Type Ia (after SN 2025khv)<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="methodology">Methodology</h2>
<p>SN 2025adpq was discovered on 2025-11-14 at g = 20.5 AB mag with DECam during gravitational wave follow-up (GW-MMADS survey)<sup><a href="#ref31">[31]</a></sup>. Seven DECam epochs in griz filters spanning 2025-11-14 to 2025-12-31 were obtained for the light curve, processed with a GPU-enabled difference imaging pipeline using the SFFT algorithm<sup><a href="#ref32">[32]</a><a href="#ref33">[33]</a></sup>. Spectroscopic follow-up was performed with SALT/RSS (PA = 90&deg;E) and Gemini/GMOS-S (PA = 129.28&deg;)<sup><a href="#ref34">[34]</a><a href="#ref35">[35]</a></sup>. Archival imaging from KiDS (optical, ugri) and VIKING (NIR, YJHKs) provided the best pre-explosion views<sup><a href="#ref36">[36]</a><a href="#ref37">[37]</a></sup>. Stellar population properties were derived using pPXF spectral fitting<sup><a href="#ref38">[38]</a></sup> and Prospector SED modeling with nonparametric star formation histories<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="literature">Literature Context</h2>
<p><a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">Collisional ring galaxies</a> were first theorized by Lynds &amp; Toomre (1976)<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup> as resulting from near head-on encounters that produce density waves propagating through disk galaxies. Only ~130 CRGs have been identified at low redshift (z ~ 0.002&ndash;0.09), representing ~1% of all ring galaxies<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>. Their short lifetimes (&le;0.5 Gyr)<sup><a href="#ref29">[29]</a><a href="#ref30">[30]</a><a href="#ref39">[39]</a></sup> make them rare. Enhanced <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> in rings has been documented by multiple studies<sup><a href="#ref40">[40]</a><a href="#ref41">[41]</a><a href="#ref30">[30]</a></sup>.</p>
<p>The paper sits at the intersection of two established fields: <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernova</a> host environments<sup><a href="#ref42">[42]</a><a href="#ref43">[43]</a><a href="#ref44">[44]</a></sup> and the <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless SN Ia problem</a><sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup>. Previous work found Type Ia SNe in tidal structures<sup><a href="#ref14">[14]</a><a href="#ref15">[15]</a></sup>, and Ditrani et al. (2024)<sup><a href="#ref21">[21]</a></sup> modeled the redistribution of old progenitors by interactions. This paper extends these findings to the specific context of collisional density waves.</p>
<h2 id="supports">What This Paper Supports</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">Star formation</a> triggered by galaxy interactions:</strong> The SFR of ~0.91 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr in the collisional ring confirms that galaxy-scale dynamical events can drive substantial star formation in propagating density waves<sup><a href="#ref40">[40]</a><a href="#ref41">[41]</a><a href="#ref30">[30]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">Long delay times</a> for Type Ia progenitors:</strong> The mismatch between ring age (~70&ndash;200 Myr) and required SN Ia delay times (&ge;0.5 Gyr) strongly supports the old progenitor channel<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Type Ia SNe trace old stellar populations:</strong> Consistent with van den Bergh &amp; Tammann (1991)<sup><a href="#ref45">[45]</a></sup> and Anderson et al. (2015)<sup><a href="#ref46">[46]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Enhanced core-collapse SN rates in interacting galaxies:</strong> The finding that CRGs are SN factories aligns with Habergham et al. (2010, 2012)<sup><a href="#ref18">[18]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="challenges">What This Paper Challenges</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>SN Ia environments = progenitor birthplaces:</strong> The paper demonstrates that <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> can relocate old progenitors tens of kpc from their formation sites, decoupling environment from origin<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Simple "hostless" classification:</strong> Some apparently hostless SNe Ia at higher z may actually reside in low surface-brightness tidal or ring structures rendered invisible by cosmological dimming<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
