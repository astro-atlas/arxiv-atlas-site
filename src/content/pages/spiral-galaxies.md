<p>
  <strong>Spiral galaxies</strong> are disk-dominated systems characterized by spiral arm structure, ongoing <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>, and ordered rotation. They populate the <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">blue cloud</a> and represent the most common morphological type among luminous galaxies in the local universe. Their spiral arms trace regions of enhanced gas density and active star formation, often highlighted by young blue stars, HII regions, and <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> lanes<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="structure">Structure</h2>
<p>
  Spirals consist of a rotating disk (containing gas, dust, and young stars), a central bulge (older stars), and a diffuse stellar halo. Spiral arms can be grand-design (two well-defined arms, often interaction-driven) or flocculent (multiple fragmented arm segments). M51a (NGC 5194) is the prototypical grand-design spiral, with its prominent arm structure enhanced by tidal interaction with its companion M51b<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="resolved-properties">Resolved Stellar Population Properties</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/spatially-resolved-stellar-populations" class="wikilink">Spatially resolved analysis</a> of spirals reveals strong internal gradients: younger, more metal-poor populations in the outer disk and older, more metal-rich populations in the center, consistent with inside-out formation<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. Spiral arms show enhanced star formation, lower mean stellar ages, and elevated H&#945; equivalent widths compared to inter-arm regions<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. In interacting spirals like M51a, these patterns may be amplified by the interaction<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
