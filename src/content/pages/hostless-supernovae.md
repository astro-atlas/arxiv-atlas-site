<p>
  <strong>Hostless supernovae</strong> are transient events that appear to lack a detectable host galaxy at their explosion site. They are predominantly <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernovae</a>, since the long <a href="/pages/supernova-delay-times" class="wikilink">delay times</a> of SN Ia progenitors allow them to explode far from obvious star-forming regions<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Proposed Explanations</h2>
<p>
  Several mechanisms have been invoked to explain apparently hostless SNe Ia:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Intracluster stars:</strong> Stars stripped from cluster galaxies into the diffuse intracluster medium can produce SNe Ia far from any individual galaxy<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Ultra-faint dwarf hosts:</strong> The host galaxy may exist but fall below the surface brightness detection limit of the survey<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Hypervelocity progenitors:</strong> Binary systems ejected at high velocity from their parent galaxy<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">Dynamical displacement</a> in mergers:</strong> <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">Galaxy mergers</a> can strip old stellar populations into tidal features or <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional rings</a> that are too faint to detect at higher redshifts<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2>The Surface Brightness Dimming Problem</h2>
<p>
  O'Connor et al. (2026) highlight that <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring structures</a> like "Pika's Halo" would be undetectable at higher redshifts due to cosmological surface brightness dimming<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. SN 2025adpq, clearly located within a collisional ring at z = 0.154, would appear "hostless" if observed at z &gt; 0.3&ndash;0.5 where the low-surface-brightness ring falls below detection limits<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. This represents a systematic bias in current surveys: some fraction of apparently hostless SNe Ia may reside in undetected tidal or ring structures<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  Hostless SN Ia classification affects cosmological analyses: host-galaxy corrections for SN Ia standardization cannot be applied when no host is identified, potentially introducing systematic biases in Hubble diagram measurements<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. Rubin LSST, with its deeper imaging and larger survey area, is expected to detect low-surface-brightness features around many currently "hostless" events<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. Targeted deep imaging of known hostless SN Ia sites could already reveal faint tidal structures or ring remnants<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
