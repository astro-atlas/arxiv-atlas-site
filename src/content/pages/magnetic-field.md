<p>
  <strong>Magnetic fields</strong> pervade the universe on all scales, from planetary magnetospheres to the vast intergalactic medium. In astrophysics, magnetic fields are generated and amplified by dynamo processes in conducting plasmas, and once established they influence gas dynamics, regulate <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>, channel outflows and jets, and shape the structure of the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Origin and Amplification</h2>
<p>
  Astrophysical magnetic fields are thought to originate from weak seed fields in the early universe, subsequently amplified by <strong>dynamo processes</strong> — the conversion of kinetic energy from plasma motions into magnetic energy. In galaxies, the α–Ω dynamo operates through the combination of differential rotation (Ω effect) and helical <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> (α effect) to maintain large-scale ordered fields of a few μG.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Magnetic Fields in the ISM</h2>
<p>
  In the diffuse <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">ISM</a>, B-field strengths are typically 1–10 μG. As gas contracts into <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a>, flux freezing (the coupling of field lines to the ionized gas component) causes the field to strengthen roughly as B ∝ n^(0.5–0.65) at densities above ~300 cm⁻³<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. In the densest protostellar cores, fields reach milligauss levels.
</p>
<p>
  The dynamical importance of the magnetic field is quantified by the <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">mass-to-flux ratio</a> — the ratio of gravitational mass to magnetic flux, normalized to the critical value above which <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a> overwhelms magnetic support<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Observational Techniques</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">Dust polarimetry</a></strong> — aligned <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> grains emit polarized thermal radiation; rotating the polarization angle by 90° reveals the plane-of-sky B-field direction</li>
  <li><strong>Zeeman effect</strong> — splitting of spectral lines by the magnetic field gives the line-of-sight field strength directly; the most direct measurement but requires strong fields or very sensitive observations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a></strong> — rotation of the polarization angle of background radio sources through a magnetized plasma; traces B-fields in ionized gas</li>
  <li><strong>Synchrotron <a href="/pages/polarimetry" class="wikilink">polarimetry</a></strong> — polarized radio emission from relativistic electrons spiraling around field lines; traces fields in supernova remnants and AGN jets</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/davis-chandrasekhar-fermi-method" class="wikilink">Davis-Chandrasekhar-Fermi method</a></strong> — estimates B-field strength from polarization angle dispersion combined with gas density and velocity dispersion</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-3">Astrophysical Contexts</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/magnetic-fields-in-star-formation" class="wikilink">Star formation</a></strong> — B-fields regulate collapse, generate <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass morphologies</a>, and launch protostellar outflows</li>
  <li><strong>Galaxy evolution</strong> — large-scale galactic fields influence gas flows, cosmic ray propagation, and <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a></li>
  <li><strong>Galaxy clusters</strong> — μG-level fields in the intracluster medium affect thermal conduction and particle acceleration at <a href="/pages/shock-waves" class="wikilink">shocks</a></li>
  <li><strong>AGN jets</strong> — highly ordered fields collimate relativistic jets over kpc scales</li>
</ul>
