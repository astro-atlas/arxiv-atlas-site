<h2 id="definition">Definition</h2>
<p>An <strong>ultra-diffuse galaxy</strong> (UDG) is a galaxy with extremely low surface brightness (&mu;<sub>0,g</sub> &ge; 24 mag/arcsec<sup>2</sup>) but large physical size (effective radius r<sub>e</sub> &ge; 1.5 kpc), giving it the spatial extent of a Milky Way-like galaxy but only ~0.1% of its stars <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. UDGs span an enormous range of dark matter content, from the most dark-matter-dominated systems known to systems with essentially no dark matter <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="discovery">Discovery and Prevalence</h2>

<h3>Foundational: Low surface brightness galaxies</h3>
<p>Low surface brightness galaxies have been known since the 1980s, but systematic surveys were limited by detector sensitivity. UDGs were named as a distinct class by van Dokkum et al. (2015) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a> after the Dragonfly Telephoto Array discovered 47 such objects in the Coma Cluster. Subsequent surveys revealed hundreds in galaxy clusters and groups <a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.</p>

<h3>Intermediate: Properties and diversity</h3>
<p>UDGs are remarkably diverse:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Dark-matter-rich UDGs:</strong> Some have massive dark matter halos (&gt;10<sup>11</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>), making them "failed galaxies" that began forming like massive systems but were quenched early <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">Dark-matter-free UDGs</a>:</strong> DF2, DF4, and DF9 in the NGC 1052 field have dynamical masses consistent with stellar mass alone <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Globular cluster populations:</strong> UDGs can have rich GC systems; DF2 and DF4 have unusually luminous clusters <a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
</ul>

<h3>Specialist: Formation channels</h3>
<p>Multiple formation pathways are proposed:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Failed massive galaxies:</strong> High-spin halos that formed extended disks but were quenched before building significant stellar mass</li>
  <li><strong>Tidal heating/stripping:</strong> Normal dwarfs puffed up by tidal interactions in cluster environments</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">Bullet dwarf collisions</a>:</strong> High-speed collisions producing dark-matter-free dwarfs with large effective radii <a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></li>
</ul>

<h2 id="ngc1052">The NGC 1052 UDGs</h2>
<p>The NGC 1052 field hosts the best-studied dark-matter-free UDGs. DF2 (r<sub>e</sub> &asymp; 2.2 kpc), DF4 (r<sub>e</sub> &asymp; 1.6 kpc), and DF9 (r<sub>e</sub> &asymp; 1.1 kpc) are all part of a linear trail of ~12 galaxies believed to have formed in a single <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf collision</a> <a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. Their velocity dispersions (6&ndash;8 km/s) are consistent with stellar mass alone and &gt;5&sigma; below <a href="/pages/stellar-mass-halo-mass-relation" class="wikilink">SMHM predictions</a> <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>.</p>
