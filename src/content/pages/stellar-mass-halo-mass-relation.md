<h2 id="definition">Definition</h2>
<p>The <strong>stellar mass&ndash;halo mass (SMHM) relation</strong> describes the empirical relationship between a galaxy's stellar mass and the mass of its dark matter halo. It encodes the integrated efficiency of galaxy formation: the fraction of available baryons that a halo converts into stars <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. The relation peaks at M<sub>halo</sub> ~ 10<sup>12</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub> (where ~20&ndash;30% of baryons become stars) and drops steeply toward both lower and higher halo masses <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="physics">Physical Origin</h2>

<h3>Foundational: Why efficiency varies with mass</h3>
<p>At low halo masses, supernovae and stellar winds efficiently expel gas, suppressing <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> and producing stellar-to-halo mass ratios of ~10<sup>&minus;2</sup> to 10<sup>&minus;4</sup> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. At high halo masses, <a href="/pages/agn-feedback" class="wikilink">AGN feedback</a> heats gas and prevents cooling, similarly suppressing star formation efficiency <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. The peak efficiency occurs where neither mechanism dominates.</p>

<h3>Intermediate: Observational calibration</h3>
<p>The SMHM relation is calibrated through abundance matching (ranking galaxies by stellar mass and halos by halo mass), weak gravitational lensing, satellite kinematics, and halo occupation distribution modeling <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. The intrinsic scatter is ~0.2 dex at the Milky Way mass scale but increases toward lower masses <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</p>

<h3>Specialist: Dwarf galaxy regime</h3>
<p>For dwarf galaxies with M<sub>&star;</sub> ~ 10<sup>8</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>, the SMHM relation predicts dark matter halos of M<sub>halo</sub> ~ 10<sup>10</sup>&ndash;10<sup>11</sup> M<sub>&odot;</sub>, implying these systems are overwhelmingly dark-matter-dominated <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This prediction makes the NGC 1052 <a href="/pages/dark-matter-free-galaxy" class="wikilink">dark-matter-free galaxies</a> (DF2, DF4, DF9) extreme outliers: their measured velocity dispersions (~6&ndash;8 km/s) imply halo masses consistent with zero, versus the ~27 km/s expected from the SMHM relation with an <a href="/pages/nfw-profile" class="wikilink">NFW profile</a> <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.</p>

<h2 id="outliers">Notable Outliers</h2>
<p>The NGC 1052 trail galaxies represent the most extreme known outliers from the SMHM relation:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>DF9:</strong> Expected &sigma; = 27 &plusmn; 3 km/s from SMHM + NFW; observed 6.4 km/s (&gt;5&sigma; discrepancy) <a href="#ref5">[5]</a></li>
  <li><strong>DF2:</strong> Similar discrepancy with &sigma; = 6.1 km/s <a href="#ref7">[7]</a></li>
  <li><strong>DF4:</strong> &sigma; = 7.7 km/s, also far below predictions <a href="#ref8">[8]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>These galaxies are not merely scattered low &mdash; they fall below the universal baryon fraction, requiring a physical mechanism (such as the <a href="/pages/bullet-dwarf-scenario" class="wikilink">bullet dwarf scenario</a>) that removes dark matter entirely <a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.</p>
