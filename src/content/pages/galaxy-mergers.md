<p>
  <strong>Galaxy mergers</strong> are gravitational encounters between two or more galaxies that result in their eventual coalescence into a single system. Mergers are a key driver of galaxy evolution, transforming galaxy morphology, triggering <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>, redistributing stellar populations, and building galaxy mass hierarchically<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Major vs Minor Mergers</h2>
<p>
  Mergers are classified by the mass (or luminosity) ratio of the interacting galaxies<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Major mergers</strong> (mass ratio &ge;1:4): Both galaxies are of comparable mass. These produce the strongest tidal features, most dramatic morphological transformations, and greatest enhancement of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Minor mergers</strong> (mass ratio &lt;1:4): A smaller satellite is accreted by a larger host. These can thicken disks, build stellar halos, and create tidal streams without destroying the primary disk<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  The "Pika's Halo" system hosting SN 2025adpq involves two galaxies with luminosity ratio L<sub>2</sub>/L<sub>1</sub> ~ 0.8, making it a clear major merger<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Tidal Structures and Morphological Signatures</h2>
<p>
  Mergers produce a variety of transient structures including tidal tails, bridges, shells, and &mdash; in the case of near head-on encounters &mdash; <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional rings</a><sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>. The strongest tidal features arise in major mergers during the brief merging phase (&le;0.5 Gyr)<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>. These structures offer unique laboratories for studying star formation in displaced gas and the <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> of pre-existing stellar populations<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Star Formation Enhancement</h2>
<p>
  Galaxy mergers can trigger substantial bursts of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> by compressing gas through tidal torques and collisional shocks<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>. In <a href="/pages/collisional-ring-galaxies" class="wikilink">collisional ring galaxies</a>, the propagating density wave sweeps up gas and triggers star formation at the ring front, producing SFRs of ~1 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr even in modest-mass systems<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. Mergers can also temporarily rejuvenate quiescent massive galaxies that would otherwise reside on the <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">red sequence</a><sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Impact on Supernova Populations</h2>
<p>
  Merger-triggered star formation enhances core-collapse supernova rates<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a><a href="#ref17">[17]</a><a href="#ref18">[18]</a></sup>. However, mergers also redistribute pre-existing old stellar populations to large radii through gravitational stripping, enabling <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia supernovae</a> at large galactocentric offsets<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a><a href="#ref20">[20]</a></sup>. This <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a> creates a new channel for producing <a href="/pages/large-offset-supernovae" class="wikilink">large-offset</a> and <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless</a> supernovae<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  Galaxy mergers are one of the primary drivers of galaxy evolution, transforming disks into ellipticals, building stellar mass, and connecting star formation history to large-scale structure. The discovery that mergers can produce <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SNe</a> at large offsets via dynamical displacement of old progenitors<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup> has implications for cosmological surveys that rely on accurate host-galaxy associations for distance measurements.
</p>
