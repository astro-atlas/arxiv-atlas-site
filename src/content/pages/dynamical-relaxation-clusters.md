<p>
  <strong>Dynamical relaxation</strong> in star clusters refers to the gradual smoothing of a cluster's spatial and velocity structure through gravitational interactions among stars. For newly formed clusters, the most observationally relevant consequence is the erasure of the initial <a href="/pages/fractal-structure-star-clusters" class="wikilink">fractal substructure</a> inherited from the parent molecular cloud — a transition from a hierarchically clumped distribution toward a smooth, centrally concentrated one.
</p>
<h2 id="fractal-erasure">Fractal Erasure Timescale</h2>
<p>
  Simulations consistently find that the transition from fractal (low Q) to centrally concentrated (high Q) structure occurs over a few free-fall times:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>Akhmetali et al. (2026)<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup>: ~2.5 t<sub>ff</sub> for 13 simulations of a 2513 M<sub>☉</sub> centrally concentrated cloud, independent of resolution and initial fractality.</li>
  <li>Laverde-Villarreal et al. (2025)<sup><a href="#ref2">2</a></sup>: ~2.5 t<sub>ff</sub> for clouds of 2 &times; 10<sup>4</sup>, 8 &times; 10<sup>4</sup>, and 3.2 &times; 10<sup>5</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> — consistent across an order of magnitude in mass.</li>
  <li>Maschberger et al. (2010)<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup>: low Q rises to non-fractal values within a couple of free-fall times in a gravitationally bound cluster.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  The consistency across cloud masses and resolutions suggests that this timescale is primarily set by the global free-fall time of the parent cloud, rather than local properties.<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="prolonged-fractality">Prolonged Fractality via Subclusters</h2>
<p>
  Dynamical relaxation can be countered by the formation of new <a href="/pages/subcluster-formation" class="wikilink">subclusters</a>, either through <a href="/pages/stellar-feedback" class="wikilink">stellar feedback</a>-triggered compression or through the repeated merger and re-fragmentation of subcluster systems. In such cases, the Q parameter can remain below the fractal threshold beyond 4 t<sub>ff</sub>, with Q oscillating as subclusters merge (Q rises) and re-separate (Q falls).<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="two-body">Two-Body Relaxation</h2>
<p>
  On longer timescales, two-body (star-star) gravitational scattering drives energy equipartition among stars and leads to mass segregation (heavy stars sink to the center) and eventual evaporation of the lightest stars from the cluster. The two-body relaxation timescale scales as t<sub>relax</sub> ~ N / ln N &times; t<sub>cross</sub>, where N is the number of stars and t<sub>cross</sub> is the crossing time — typically much longer than the initial fractal-erasure timescale.
</p>
