<p>
  <strong>Metallicity gradients</strong> describe the radial variation of chemical abundances &mdash; both stellar and gas-phase (nebular) &mdash; within galaxies. Most disk galaxies show negative gradients (higher metallicity in the center, lower in the outskirts), reflecting inside-out formation where the denser central regions formed stars earlier and enriched more rapidly<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="stellar-vs-nebular">Stellar vs. Nebular Metallicity</h2>
<p>
  <strong>Stellar metallicity</strong> reflects the integrated chemical enrichment history, measured through absorption-line fitting of stellar populations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. <strong>Nebular (gas-phase) metallicity</strong> traces the current ISM enrichment level, measured through <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">emission-line ratios</a> using strong-line calibrations (e.g., O3N2, N2 methods)<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. Both types of gradients carry diagnostic information about galaxy formation history, but they can differ &mdash; gas-phase metallicity responds more quickly to inflows and outflows.
</p>
<h2 id="formation-signatures">Inside-Out Formation Signatures</h2>
<p>
  Steep negative metallicity gradients are a hallmark of undisturbed inside-out disk formation: the central regions formed stars earlier, reaching higher metallicities through extended chemical enrichment<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. The M101 group preserves these steep gradients, consistent with relatively pristine evolution<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="interaction-flattening">Flattening by Interactions</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">Galaxy interactions</a> can flatten metallicity gradients through radial gas mixing, interaction-triggered <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>, and redistribution of enriched material<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. The M51 group shows flatter age&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> and nebular metallicity&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> relations compared to M101, interpreted as evidence of more advanced dynamical evolution driven by interactions<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>. This contrast provides direct observational evidence that group environment modulates chemical abundance gradients.
</p>
