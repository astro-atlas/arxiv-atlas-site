<p>
  <strong>Stellar feedback</strong> refers to the energy, momentum, and mass injected by stars into their surrounding interstellar medium. Feedback from massive stars (O and early-B type) dominates: their extreme UV luminosities create HII regions via photoionization, their fast stellar winds drive shells and bubbles, and their eventual core-collapse supernovae deposit ~10<sup>51</sup> erg per event. Together, these processes regulate <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> on cloud scales and shape the structural and dynamical evolution of embedded star clusters.
</p>
<h2 id="mechanisms">Feedback Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Photoionization:</strong> Lyman-continuum photons from O stars ionize surrounding hydrogen, creating expanding HII regions with T ~ 10<sup>4</sup> K and sound speed ~10 km/s. The overpressure drives ionization fronts into neutral gas, compressing it into shells.</li>
  <li><strong>Stellar winds:</strong> Fast (v ~ 10<sup>3</sup> km/s) wind from massive stars sweeps up a bubble of hot gas; the wind momentum couples to GMC material.</li>
  <li><strong>Supernovae:</strong> Core-collapse SNe (from stars &gt; ~8 M<sub>☉</sub>) deposit kinetic energy ~10<sup>51</sup> erg; repeated SNe from a massive cluster can drive superbubbles.</li>
  <li><strong>Radiation pressure:</strong> Direct radiation pressure on dust grains can contribute to gas expulsion in the most massive clusters.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="star-cluster-evolution">Impact on Star Cluster Structure</h2>
<p>
  Stellar feedback shapes both the formation and fate of star clusters:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Triggered subcluster formation:</strong> Gas compressed in feedback-driven shells can reach the Jeans mass and collapse to form a secondary subcluster, prolonging the <a href="/pages/fractal-structure-star-clusters" class="wikilink">fractal structure</a> of the cluster system beyond 4 t<sub>ff</sub> in some simulations.<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Stochastic effects:</strong> Whether feedback enhances or suppresses cluster substructure depends on the mass and formation timing of the massive star — not a universal outcome.<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Gas expulsion:</strong> Feedback eventually disperses the GMC, ending star formation. The fraction of stars that remain bound after gas expulsion depends on the star formation efficiency (SFE) and density profile.<sup><a href="#ref2">2</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>
  Some simulations (Parker &amp; Dale 2015<sup><a href="#ref4">4</a></sup>; Gavagnin et al. 2017<sup><a href="#ref5">5</a></sup>) find that stellar feedback has little effect on the Q parameter. Others (Akhmetali et al. 2026<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup>) show that massive star feedback can trigger subclusters and maintain fractality — but only stochastically, depending on stellar mass and timing.
</p>
<h2 id="galaxy-scale">Galaxy-Scale Feedback</h2>
<p>
  At galactic scales, stellar feedback from clustered star formation is a principal driver of the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">multiphase ISM</a> and galactic winds. The efficiency with which feedback energy couples to the ISM determines the star formation rate in disk galaxies and the mass loading of outflows — a central topic in galaxy evolution. See also <a href="/pages/agn-feedback" class="wikilink">AGN feedback</a> for the analogous process driven by supermassive black holes.
</p>
