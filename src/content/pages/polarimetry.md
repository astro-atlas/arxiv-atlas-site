<p>
  <strong>Polarimetry</strong> is the measurement of the polarization state of electromagnetic radiation. Light becomes polarized when it is emitted, absorbed, or scattered in an asymmetric environment — such as by aligned <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust grains</a> in a <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a>, by scattering off electrons, or by synchrotron radiation from relativistic particles. Polarimetry is one of the most powerful tools in astrophysics for probing magnetic field structure, geometry, and physical conditions that cannot be accessed through intensity measurements alone.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Stokes Parameters</h2>
<p>
  Polarization is fully described by four <strong>Stokes parameters</strong>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>I</strong> — total intensity</li>
  <li><strong>Q, U</strong> — linear polarization; the polarization angle is θ = ½ arctan2(U, Q) and the polarization fraction is p = √(Q² + U²)/I</li>
  <li><strong>V</strong> — circular polarization (important for Zeeman measurements)</li>
</ul>
<p>Standard quality criteria for polarimetric data include signal-to-noise thresholds such as p/σ_p > 3 and I/σ_I > 100<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Astrophysical Mechanisms</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Thermal dust emission</strong> — non-spherical <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> grains aligned by radiative torques emit polarized far-infrared light perpendicular to the <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">B-field</a>; rotating by 90° gives the field direction. This is the basis of <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">far-infrared/submm dust polarimetry</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Dichroic absorption</strong> — aligned grains preferentially absorb one polarization of background starlight, producing optical/NIR polarization parallel to the B-field</li>
  <li><strong>Synchrotron radiation</strong> — relativistic electrons spiraling in B-fields produce intrinsically polarized radio emission; used in AGN jets and supernova remnants</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a></strong> — the polarization angle of linearly polarized radio waves rotates as they pass through a magnetized plasma</li>
  <li><strong>Scattering</strong> — Thomson/Rayleigh scattering produces polarization; used to study protoplanetary disks and the CMB</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-2">Key Instruments</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>SOFIA/HAWC+</strong> — far-infrared polarimetry at 53–214 μm; mapped B-fields in <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">IRDCs</a> and star-forming regions<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>JCMT/POL-2</strong> — 850 μm polarimetry (BISTRO survey)</li>
  <li><strong>ALMA</strong> — interferometric mm/submm polarimetry at sub-arcsecond resolution</li>
  <li><strong>Planck</strong> — all-sky 353 GHz polarization at 5′ resolution<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
