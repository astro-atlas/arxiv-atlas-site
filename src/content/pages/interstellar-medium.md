<p>
  The <strong>interstellar medium (ISM)</strong> is the matter and radiation that fills the space between stars within a galaxy. It consists of gas (mostly hydrogen and helium) and <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">interstellar dust</a>, threaded by <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> and permeated by cosmic rays. The ISM is the raw material from which new stars form and into which dying stars return processed material — it is the ecosystem of galactic evolution.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Phases of the ISM</h2>
<p>The ISM exists in several distinct thermal phases in approximate pressure equilibrium:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Hot ionized medium (HIM)</strong> — T ~ 10⁶ K, n ~ 0.003 cm⁻³; fills most of the volume, heated by supernova remnants</li>
  <li><strong>Warm ionized medium (WIM)</strong> — T ~ 8000 K, n ~ 0.3 cm⁻³; traced by Hα emission and pulsar dispersion measures</li>
  <li><strong>Warm neutral medium (WNM)</strong> — T ~ 6000–8000 K, n ~ 0.5 cm⁻³; traced by 21 cm H I emission</li>
  <li><strong>Cold neutral medium (CNM)</strong> — T ~ 50–100 K, n ~ 30 cm⁻³; traced by 21 cm H I absorption</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">Molecular clouds</a></strong> — T ~ 10–20 K, n ~ 10²–10⁶ cm⁻³; traced by CO and other molecular lines; sites of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-1">Key Physical Processes</h2>
<p>
  Three forces dominate the dynamics of the ISM: <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">self-gravity</a>, <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a>, and <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a>. Their interplay determines whether gas collapses to form stars or remains supported against collapse. In dense <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a>, gravity eventually wins, but magnetic fields and turbulence regulate the rate and efficiency of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The ISM is highly structured, with <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filamentary morphologies</a> observed at all scales from parsecs to hundreds of parsecs<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. These filaments are intimately connected to the magnetic field geometry — Planck observations show that B-fields tend to be perpendicular to dense filaments but parallel to diffuse striations<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Observational Tracers</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>21 cm H I line</strong> — traces atomic hydrogen in warm and cold neutral phases</li>
  <li><strong>CO rotational lines</strong> — primary tracer of <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular gas</a></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">Dust</a> continuum emission</strong> — traces total gas <a href="/pages/column-density" class="wikilink">column density</a> at far-infrared/submm wavelengths</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">Dust polarimetry</a></strong> — maps <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> morphology via aligned grain emission</li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation</a></strong> — traces magnetic fields in ionized gas</li>
</ul>
