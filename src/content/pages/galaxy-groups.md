<p>
  <strong>Galaxy groups</strong> are gravitationally bound associations of typically 3&ndash;50 galaxies, intermediate in mass between isolated galaxies and massive <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">galaxy clusters</a>. Groups are the most common galaxy environment in the local universe &mdash; the majority of galaxies reside in groups rather than clusters or the field<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Despite their lower masses and velocity dispersions compared to clusters, groups host significant environmental processing through tidal interactions, <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">mergers</a>, and mild hydrodynamic effects<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="properties">Physical Properties</h2>
<p>
  Galaxy groups have total masses of ~10<sup>12</sup>&ndash;10<sup>14</sup> M<sub>&#9737;</sub>, velocity dispersions of ~100&ndash;500 km/s, and virial radii of ~0.5&ndash;2 Mpc<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Their lower velocity dispersions compared to clusters mean that galaxy&ndash;galaxy interactions proceed more slowly, making tidal interactions and mergers more effective than in clusters where high-speed encounters produce weaker tidal perturbations<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="environment">Environmental Effects on Galaxy Evolution</h2>
<p>
  Groups are key sites for galaxy transformation. The environmental dependence of galaxy properties &mdash; including <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> rates, stellar ages, and morphology &mdash; extends down to group-mass scales<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. Even in low-mass groups, interactions can flatten age and <a href="/pages/metallicity-gradients" class="wikilink">metallicity gradients</a>, enhance <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> content, and trigger <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a> in some members<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  A striking demonstration comes from comparing the M51 and M101 groups: the more dynamically evolved M51 group shows flattened age&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> and nebular metallicity&ndash;&Sigma;<sub>&#9733;</sub> relations, while the less evolved M101 group preserves steep gradients consistent with undisturbed inside-out formation<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. The M51a/b interacting pair further illustrates how group interactions produce divergent outcomes &mdash; a star-forming spiral alongside a retired <a href="/pages/early-type-galaxies" class="wikilink">early-type</a> companion<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="quenching">Quenching in Groups</h2>
<p>
  Galaxy groups contribute significantly to the overall quenching budget in the universe. Mechanisms include tidal interactions that strip or heat gas, strangulation (removal of the hot gas halo that feeds future star formation), and in denser groups, mild <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. M63's outside-in quenching pattern in the M51 group<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup> and the fully retired status of M51b<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup> demonstrate that these mechanisms operate even in modest group environments.
</p>
