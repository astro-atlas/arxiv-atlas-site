<p>
  <strong>3C 84</strong> is the powerful radio source associated with <strong>NGC 1275</strong> (Perseus A), the brightest cluster galaxy (BCG) at the center of the Perseus cluster. It is one of the brightest radio sources in the sky and hosts a sub-relativistic jet that has been monitored for decades with VLBI <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. The jet viewing angle is approximately 45° <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. The current episode of jet activity began around 2003 <a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="jet-structure">Parsec-Scale Jet Structure</h2>
<p>
  VLBI monitoring reveals several distinct jet components <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Core:</strong> The 43 GHz VLBI core lies within ~0.03–0.08 mas of the black hole <a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. Core polarization remains undetected <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>C4 / C4a:</strong> Inner jet components at ~3 mas from core. First detected in polarization in 2022 December with RM = 2.6–3.5&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup>; C4a showed RM = (6.1&plusmn;1.1)&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>C3:</strong> A hotspot region at ~4–5 mas. The bright eastern limb emerged in late 2015 <a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a> and was disrupted after 2017 May <a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. Average RM = (3.9&plusmn;0.6)&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> from VLBA <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>; KVN values of 1.0–3.6&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> (2018–2024) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>K:</strong> Downstream at 5–7 mas, with the smallest RM values (0.5–0.9&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup>) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  <li><strong>Southern lobe:</strong> At ~15 mas (~7.5 pc). RM = 7000 rad/m<sup>2</sup>, confirmed stable over 20 years <a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  The jet shows evidence of instabilities and precession <a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a>. Gamma-ray flares in 2022 November–2023 January coincided with polarization emergence at C4 <a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="faraday-rotation">Faraday Rotation Profile</h2>
<p>
  The defining observational result is that all RM measurements from 1–15 pc follow a single power-law RM &prop; r<sup>&minus;2.7&plusmn;0.2</sup> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This unifies decades of seemingly discrepant measurements:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>IRAM PdBI non-detections at 81–348 GHz with RM dispersion &ge; 10<sup>6</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> <a href="#ref17">[17]</a><a href="#ref18">[18]</a></li>
  <li>CARMA/SMA 1.3 mm detection with RM = 8.7&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> <a href="#ref19">[19]</a></li>
  <li>VLBA 43 GHz C3 RM = (3–6)&times;10<sup>5</sup> rad/m<sup>2</sup> <a href="#ref2">[2]</a></li>
  <li>VLBA 8–22 GHz southern lobe RM = 7000 rad/m<sup>2</sup> <a href="#ref12">[12]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  The <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday screen</a> is the circumnuclear ambient medium within the <a href="/pages/bondi-accretion" class="wikilink">Bondi radius</a> (11.8 pc), not a <a href="/pages/riaf-adaf-models" class="wikilink">RIAF</a>, jet boundary layer, or individual gas cloud <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="magnetic-field"><a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a></h2>
<p>
  The RM profile constrains B &prop; r<sup>&minus;1.7 to &minus;2.2</sup> within the Bondi radius, with B = 0.1–1.5 &mu;G at 11.8 pc increasing rapidly inward <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. This is the first spatially resolved ambient magnetic field measurement in an elliptical galaxy <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Previous estimates were galaxy-averaged <a href="#ref20">[20]</a> or theoretical <a href="#ref21">[21]</a><a href="#ref22">[22]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="environment">Perseus Cluster Environment</h2>
<p>
  NGC 1275 hosts extensive optical emission-line filaments extending &gt;100 kpc, dragged outward by radio bubbles and carrying magnetic fields of ~24 &mu;G <a href="#ref23">[23]</a><a href="#ref24">[24]</a>. X-ray observations show electron density n<sub>e</sub> &prop; r<sup>&minus;0.67</sup> on kpc scales <a href="#ref25">[25]</a>. The interplay between AGN feedback, cold gas precipitation, and the hot ICM creates a complex multiphase environment <a href="#ref26">[26]</a><a href="#ref27">[27]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="accretion-state">Accretion State</h2>
<p>
  The Eddington ratio of 3C 84 is revised from the commonly quoted 0.4% <a href="#ref19">[19]</a> down to ~0.04–0.09% <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>, placing it at the boundary of the accretion state transition where BLR emission weakens and RIAF models apply <a href="#ref28">[28]</a><a href="#ref29">[29]</a>. The coexistence of broad emission lines, an FFA disk, and a parsec-scale ambient medium places interesting constraints on accretion models <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
