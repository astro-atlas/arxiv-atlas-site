<div class="key-takeaways">
  <div class="key-takeaways-title">Key Takeaways</div>
  <h4>Main Results</h4>
  <ul>
    <li>Jeans-equation analysis via MG-MAMPOSSt of 1651 DESI DR1 member galaxies yields M<sub>200</sub> = 1.08<sup>+0.08</sup><sub>−0.09</sub> (stat) ± 0.09 (syst) × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> with r<sub>200</sub> = 2.12 ± 0.06 Mpc for the Coma Cluster <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a>.</li>
    <li>Blue cloud galaxies show strongly non-Gaussian velocity distributions (A² ≈ 1.56) and less concentrated spatial profiles (r<sub>ν</sub>(BC) ~ 4 r<sub>ν</sub>(RS)), consistent with an infalling population on radial orbits <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a>.</li>
    <li>Red sequence galaxies are near-Gaussian (A² ≈ 0.29) and virialised, while green valley galaxies are intermediate (A² ≈ 1.27), supporting a BC → GV → RS evolutionary sequence driven by ram-pressure stripping <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>.</li>
    <li>Baryon fraction f<sub>b</sub> = 0.15 ± 0.02 at r<sub>200</sub> is consistent with the cosmological Ω<sub>b</sub>/Ω<sub>m</sub> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>.</li>
    <li>The kinematic mass sits above weak lensing (Gavazzi+2009 <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>; Cha+2025 <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>) and X-ray+SZ estimates (Mirakhor+2020 <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>), but below Bayesian ML (Ho+2022 <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>) and caustic methods (Benisty+2025 <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>), with 2σ overlap with most <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.</li>
  </ul>
  <h4>Implications</h4>
  <p>The population-dependent orbital anisotropy directly connects cluster dynamics to galaxy evolution: blue <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star-forming</a> galaxies plunge through the cluster core on radial orbits, experiencing efficient ram-pressure stripping that transforms them through the green valley onto the red sequence. This provides kinematic evidence for the environmental quenching pathway in the nearest massive cluster, calibrating the timescale and orbital geometry of the transformation. The precise mass measurement also benchmarks cluster mass estimation methods, revealing systematic tensions between kinematic, lensing, and X-ray approaches.</p>
  <h4>Other Works</h4>
  <p>The orbital anisotropy trends align with Mamon et al. (2019) who found more radial blue-galaxy orbits in stacked WINGS clusters, and with Biviano et al. (2025) who measured similar profiles in nine CLASH-VLT clusters. The Coma mass is consistent with the classic Łokas &amp; Mamon (2003) kinematic estimate (1.2 × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub>) but sits ~2σ above recent weak-lensing values. Pizzuti et al. (2025) found a comparable anisotropy pattern in PSZ2 G067.17+67.46. The ram-pressure stripping interpretation follows the framework of Gunn &amp; Gott (1972) and the observational synthesis of Boselli, Fossati &amp; Sun (2022).</p>
</div>
<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>
  This paper presents a kinematic analysis of the <a href="/pages/coma-cluster" class="wikilink">Coma Cluster</a> (Abell 1656, z = 0.023) using 1651 member galaxies from the <a href="/pages/desi-survey" class="wikilink">DESI DR1</a> spectroscopic survey <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. The authors apply the <a href="/pages/jeans-equation" class="wikilink">Jeans equation</a> via the <a href="/pages/mamposst" class="wikilink">MG-MAMPOSSt</a> code to jointly reconstruct the total mass profile and <a href="/pages/orbital-anisotropy-in-galaxy-clusters" class="wikilink">orbital anisotropy</a> profile <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. They find a best-fit virial mass M<sub>200</sub> = 1.08<sup>+0.08</sup><sub>−0.09</sub> (stat) ± 0.09 (syst) × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> with r<sub>200</sub> = 2.12 ± 0.06 Mpc <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Splitting galaxies by colour into <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">red sequence, green valley, and blue cloud</a> populations reveals population-dependent orbital anisotropy consistent with <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a> along radial orbits <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="key-findings">Key Findings</h2>
<h3>Mass Profile</h3>
<p>
  The preferred mass model is <a href="/pages/nfw-profile" class="wikilink">NFW</a> with a generalised Osipkov-Merritt (gOM) anisotropy profile <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>. Key parameters:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>M<sub>200</sub>:</strong> 1.08<sup>+0.08</sup><sub>−0.09</sub> (stat) ± 0.09 (syst) × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>r<sub>200</sub>:</strong> 2.12 ± 0.06 (stat) ± 0.07 (syst) Mpc <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Scale radius r<sub>s</sub>:</strong> 0.48<sup>+0.27</sup><sub>−0.13</sub> Mpc <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Dark matter mass M<sub>200</sub>(DM):</strong> 8.6<sup>+1.2</sup><sub>−0.8</sub> × 10<sup>14</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong><a href="/pages/baryon-fraction-in-galaxy-clusters" class="wikilink">Baryon fraction</a> f<sub>b</sub>:</strong> 0.15 ± 0.02, consistent with Ω<sub>b</sub>/Ω<sub>m</sub> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  This mass sits above weak lensing estimates (5.1 × 10<sup>14</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>; 7.4 × 10<sup>14</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>) and X-ray+<a href="/pages/sunyaev-zeldovich-effect" class="wikilink">SZ</a> estimates (8.5 × 10<sup>14</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>), but below Bayesian deep learning (1.8 × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>) and caustic/virial methods (1.1–2.9 × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>), with 2σ overlap with most estimates <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Population-Dependent Orbital Anisotropy</h3>
<p>
  The paper's key novel contribution is the measurement of anisotropy profiles for different galaxy populations <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Red sequence (RS):</strong> Anderson-Darling A² ≈ 0.29 — near-Gaussian velocity distribution, consistent with a virialised population <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Blue cloud (BC):</strong> A² ≈ 1.56 — strongly non-Gaussian, indicating an infalling population on radial orbits <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Green valley (GV):</strong> A² ≈ 1.27 — intermediate non-Gaussianity, transitional between RS and BC <a href="#ref1">[1]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  BC galaxies have a much less concentrated spatial distribution than RS galaxies, with r<sub>ν</sub>(BC) ~ 4 r<sub>ν</sub>(RS) <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a>. The radial orbital preference of BC galaxies supports the picture of infalling star-forming galaxies being quenched by <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a> as they plunge through the cluster core <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Coma Cluster Substructure Context</h3>
<p>
  Although Coma was long considered a prototype relaxed cluster, extensive <a href="/pages/galaxy-cluster-substructures" class="wikilink">substructure</a> is now recognized <a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>. The NGC 4839 group, identified in X-ray and galaxy distributions <a href="#ref17">[17]</a><a href="#ref18">[18]</a>, is understood to be on secondary infall after its first orbital apocentre <a href="#ref19">[19]</a><a href="#ref20">[20]</a><a href="#ref21">[21]</a>. A recent or ongoing merger between the main cluster and a group associated with NGC 4889/NGC 4874 is also identified <a href="#ref22">[22]</a>. These complex interactions may explain the post-starburst galaxies observed in the Coma periphery <a href="#ref23">[23]</a><a href="#ref24">[24]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="methodology">Methodology</h2>
<h3>Data</h3>
<p>
  The sample is drawn from DESI DR1 with >2000 spectra in a single pointing toward Coma, achieving 91% spectroscopic completeness <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. The cluster centre is defined at NGC 4874 (RA=194.90°, DEC=27.96°) <a href="#ref25">[25]</a>. Interlopers are removed using the Clean algorithm of Mamon et al. (2013) <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Stellar masses are computed using CIGALE SPS code with Chabrier IMF <a href="#ref26">[26]</a><a href="#ref27">[27]</a>. Galaxy colour classification follows the (g−r) vs stellar mass scheme of Boselli et al. (2014) <a href="#ref28">[28]</a>.
</p>
<h3>MG-MAMPOSSt Analysis</h3>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/mamposst" class="wikilink">MG-MAMPOSSt</a> applies the <a href="/pages/jeans-equation" class="wikilink">Jeans equation</a> to <a href="/pages/projected-phase-space" class="wikilink">projected phase-space</a> data via maximum likelihood <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. The method assumes spherical symmetry and a 3D Gaussian velocity distribution, which remains valid even when the LoS distribution departs from Gaussianity <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Mass models tested include <a href="/pages/nfw-profile" class="wikilink">NFW</a>, generalised NFW (gNFW) <a href="#ref29">[29]</a>, and Burkert <a href="#ref30">[30]</a> profiles. Anisotropy models include Tiret <a href="#ref31">[31]</a> and Osipkov-Merritt <a href="#ref32">[32]</a><a href="#ref33">[33]</a> parameterisations. MCMC convergence verified with Gelman-Rubin R̂ &lt; 1.1 <a href="#ref34">[34]</a> using 110,000-step chains with 10,000 burn-in <a href="#ref35">[35]</a><a href="#ref36">[36]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Robustness</h3>
<p>
  Results are tested against alternative centres (X-ray peak at RA=194.94°, DEC=27.95° <a href="#ref37">[37]</a>), different radial cuts, and multi-component mass models separating dark matter from baryons <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="context">Literature Context</h2>
<p>
  The Coma Cluster has been central to dark matter studies since Zwicky's (1933) pioneering mass estimate <a href="#ref38">[38]</a>. Kinematic mass estimates from elliptical galaxies gave M<sub>200</sub> = 1.2 × 10<sup>15</sup> M<sub>☉</sub> with ~30% accuracy <a href="#ref39">[39]</a>. The diversity of <a href="/pages/galaxy-cluster-mass-estimation-methods" class="wikilink">mass estimation methods</a> applied to Coma — kinematics, weak lensing, X-ray, SZ, machine learning — makes it a benchmark for inter-method comparison <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref10">[10]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The finding that anisotropy profiles are population-dependent aligns with previous work: Mamon et al. (2019) found more radial behaviour for blue galaxies in stacked WINGS clusters <a href="#ref40">[40]</a>, while Biviano et al. (2025) found similar trends in CLASH-VLT clusters <a href="#ref36">[36]</a>. However, Hwang &amp; Lee (2008) reported no significant differences between passive and star-forming galaxy orbits <a href="#ref41">[41]</a>, suggesting sample size or cluster-specific effects may matter.
</p>
