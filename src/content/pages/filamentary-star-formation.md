<p>
  <strong>Filamentary star formation</strong> refers to the paradigm — established through Herschel observations — that <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> proceeds through a hierarchical pathway: the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">ISM</a> organizes into filamentary networks, within which dense cores condense along supercritical filaments and collapse to form stars<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Filaments are ubiquitous structures observed at all scales in <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular clouds</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Filament Properties</h2>
<p>
  Herschel observations revealed that interstellar filaments have a characteristic width of ~0.1 pc across a wide range of environments<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>, though this universality is debated. Filament radial profiles are well-described by Plummer-like functions with a flattening radius R_flat<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Tools such as RadFil are used to identify filament spines and extract profiles<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  Filaments host embedded clumps and cores identified by surveys like ATLASGAL<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. In G351.77-0.53, the filament extends ~7.8 pc with a flattening radius of ~0.55 pc and total mass ~4150 M☉<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Hub-Filament Systems</h2>
<p>
  <strong>Hub-filament systems (HFS)</strong> are configurations where multiple filaments converge on a central dense hub — the sites of the most massive star formation<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. Material flows along filaments toward the hub, channeled by <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a> that align with the low-density striations but are perpendicular to the dense spine<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  In G351.77-0.53, multiple sub-filaments converge toward the central clump c1, potentially contributing to the observed <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass B-field morphology</a> through their alignment with the field<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Magnetic Fields and Filaments</h2>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/magnetic-fields-in-star-formation" class="wikilink">Magnetic fields</a> are intimately linked to filament dynamics. Planck observations show B-fields perpendicular to dense filaments, consistent with matter flowing along field lines to build up the filament<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>. MHD simulations confirm that <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> and B-field orientation significantly impact filament structure and fragmentation<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>.
</p>
