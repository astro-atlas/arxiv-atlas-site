<p>
  <strong>Infrared dark clouds (IRDCs)</strong> are dense, cold <a href="/pages/molecular-cloud" class="wikilink">molecular cloud</a> structures seen as dark silhouettes against the bright diffuse mid-infrared emission of the Galactic plane. They represent the earliest observable stages of high-mass star and stellar cluster formation<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>, with high column densities (N(H₂) ≥ 10²² cm⁻²) and low temperatures (10–25 K) that make them opaque at wavelengths from ~8–24 μm<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Physical Properties</h2>
<p>IRDCs are characterized by:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>High column densities</strong> — typically N(H₂) > 10²² cm⁻², sufficient to absorb the diffuse Galactic mid-infrared background</li>
  <li><strong>Low temperatures</strong> — <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> temperatures of 10–25 K, indicating minimal internal heating prior to <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a></li>
  <li><strong>Filamentary morphology</strong> — most IRDCs display elongated, filamentary structures consistent with the broader paradigm of <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filamentary star formation</a><sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>High masses</strong> — cloud masses of 10²–10⁴ M☉, hosting multiple embedded clumps and cores</li>
</ul>
<p>
  IRDCs harbor cores at various evolutionary stages, from quiescent starless cores to active protostellar sources with molecular outflows and masers<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>. Molecular line chemistry (e.g., N₂H⁺, NH₃) confirms their cold, dense conditions and traces the earliest chemical signatures of collapse<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Role in High-Mass Star Formation</h2>
<p>
  IRDCs are widely regarded as the precursors to massive stellar clusters and OB associations<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. Their importance stems from the fact that they capture the initial conditions — density structure, temperature, kinematics, and <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a> configuration — before feedback from newly formed massive stars significantly alters the environment.
</p>
<p>
  Magnetic field studies of IRDCs remain relatively rare but are critical for understanding how <a href="/pages/mass-to-flux-ratio" class="wikilink">magnetic support</a> regulates fragmentation and collapse. Observations using <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">dust polarimetry</a> have revealed ordered B-field morphologies in several IRDCs, including G11.11-0.12<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>, G14.225-0.506<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>, G28.37+0.07<sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a></sup>, G34.43+0.24<sup><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>, and G351.77-0.53<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Notable IRDCs</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>G351.77-0.53</strong> — hosts the massive protocluster IRAS 17233-3606 and displays an <a href="/pages/hourglass-magnetic-field-morphology" class="wikilink">hourglass-shaped magnetic field</a> revealed by SOFIA/HAWC+ polarimetry<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a></sup>. Distance ~2.0 kpc (Gaia DR3)<sup><a href="#ref11">[11]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>G11.11-0.12 ("The Snake")</strong> — a well-studied filamentary IRDC with B-field structure mapped at multiple scales<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G28.34+0.06</strong> — shows dynamical substructures forming in magnetically supported environments<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>G34.43+0.24</strong> — <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">gravity</a>, magnetic field, and turbulence all contribute to fragmentation<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="section-3">Observational Surveys</h2>
<p>
  Key surveys targeting IRDCs include the ATLASGAL 870 μm survey<sup><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>, which cataloged ~8000 dense Galactic clumps with distances and masses<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a></sup>, the ALMA-IMF large program investigating the origin of stellar masses<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>, and the MagMaR survey characterizing magnetic fields at the onset of high-mass star formation<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a></sup>.
</p>
