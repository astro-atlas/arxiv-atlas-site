<p>
  <strong>Collisional ring galaxies (CRGs)</strong> are a class of peculiar galaxies formed when a companion galaxy passes nearly head-on through the disk of a target galaxy, producing a collisionally driven density wave that propagates radially outward as an expanding ring<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>. Unlike resonance rings (formed by bar-driven orbital resonances) or polar rings (formed by accretion onto orthogonal orbits), CRGs arise from impulsive gravitational perturbations during "bullseye" collisions<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Formation and Properties</h2>
<p>
  CRGs require a specific encounter geometry: the intruder galaxy must pass close to the center of the target disk<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. The strongest ring features arise in <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">major mergers</a> (comparable-mass encounters) during the brief merging phase<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>. The expanding density wave sweeps up disk gas, compressing it and triggering enhanced <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> at the ring front<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>. Rings can extend tens of kpc from the host galaxy center, with expansion velocities of 50&ndash;150 km/s<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  CRGs have short lifetimes (&le;0.5 Gyr from formation to dissolution)<sup><a href="#ref10">[10]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>, making them intrinsically rare. Only ~130 have been identified at low redshift (z ~ 0.002&ndash;0.09), representing ~1% of all ring galaxies<sup><a href="#ref12">[12]</a></sup>. However, ring morphologies have been detected at z &gt; 1&ndash;3 in deep surveys<sup><a href="#ref13">[13]</a><a href="#ref14">[14]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Dual Stellar Populations</h2>
<p>
  A key property of CRGs is that the expanding density wave not only triggers new star formation but also <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamically displaces</a> pre-existing old stellar populations from the disk<sup><a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>. This produces a characteristic dual population within the ring: young stars formed in situ alongside old stars stripped from the parent galaxy. In "Pika's Halo," deep Ca H&amp;K absorption (old stars) coexists with bright emission lines (ongoing SF at SFR ~ 0.91 M<sub>&#9737;</sub>/yr)<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>CRGs as Supernova Factories</h2>
<p>
  CRGs host elevated transient rates due to their combination of enhanced star formation and displaced old stellar populations<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>. A crossmatch of the Madore et al. (2009) low-z CRG catalog with TNS reveals 15 transients in 11 CRG systems (8% of the catalog), with 3 systems hosting supernova siblings<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>. The displaced old populations provide a channel for <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SNe</a> at <a href="/pages/large-offset-supernovae" class="wikilink">large offsets</a> from the host nucleus<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Detection Methods</h2>
<p>
  CRGs have been identified through visual inspection of peculiar galaxy catalogs<sup><a href="#ref17">[17]</a><a href="#ref18">[18]</a></sup>, citizen science projects<sup><a href="#ref19">[19]</a></sup>, and increasingly through machine learning classification<sup><a href="#ref20">[20]</a><a href="#ref21">[21]</a></sup>. At higher redshifts, surface brightness dimming makes faint ring structures difficult to detect, potentially causing <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless SN</a> misclassification<sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2>Why This Matters</h2>
<p>
  CRGs serve as unique laboratories for studying the interplay between gravitational dynamics and <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> during <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">galaxy mergers</a>. Their expanding rings provide dynamically constrained age estimates, enabling studies of stellar population evolution with known formation timescales. The discovery that CRGs can produce <a href="/pages/large-offset-supernovae" class="wikilink">large-offset</a> <a href="/pages/type-ia-supernovae" class="wikilink">Type Ia SNe</a> via <a href="/pages/dynamical-stellar-displacement" class="wikilink">dynamical displacement</a><sup><a href="#ref16">[16]</a></sup> has implications for supernova cosmology and the <a href="/pages/hostless-supernovae" class="wikilink">hostless SN problem</a>.
</p>
