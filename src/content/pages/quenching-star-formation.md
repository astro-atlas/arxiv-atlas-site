<p>
  <strong>Star formation quenching</strong> refers to the suppression or cessation of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> in a galaxy, transforming it from an actively star-forming system to a quiescent or "retired" one. Quenching is central to galaxy evolution, producing the observed bimodality between star-forming (blue cloud) and quiescent (<a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">red sequence</a>) galaxies<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="mechanisms">Quenching Mechanisms</h2>
<p>
  Quenching mechanisms are broadly divided into <strong>internal</strong> (mass-driven) and <strong>environmental</strong> processes:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Mass quenching:</strong> In massive galaxies, <a href="/pages/agn-feedback" class="wikilink">AGN feedback</a>, virial shock heating of infalling gas, and morphological stabilization (bulge growth) suppress star formation. Central velocity dispersion is a strong predictor of quenching<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Environmental quenching:</strong> Processes driven by a galaxy's surroundings, including <a href="/pages/ram-pressure-stripping" class="wikilink">ram-pressure stripping</a>, tidal interactions, strangulation (removal of the hot gas reservoir), and harassment (repeated high-speed encounters)<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="spatial-patterns">Spatial Patterns of Quenching</h2>
<p>
  The spatial pattern of quenching within a galaxy provides diagnostic power for identifying the responsible mechanism:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Inside-out quenching:</strong> Star formation ceases first in the central regions and progressively shuts off outward. Often associated with AGN feedback or bulge growth<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.</li>
  <li><strong>Outside-in quenching:</strong> The outer disk quenches first while the center continues forming stars. Typically associated with environmental stripping. M63 in the M51 group shows this pattern, with asymmetric age, <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> attenuation, and H&#945; EW maps consistent with a past interaction-driven outside-in quenching<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.</li>
</ul>
<p>
  The contrast between the M51 group (interaction-processed, with quenched member M51b and outside-in quenching in M63) and the M101 group (undisturbed, preserving inside-out formation signatures) demonstrates that group environment can drive environmental quenching even in low-mass systems<sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="retired-galaxies">Retired Galaxies</h2>
<p>
  Fully quenched galaxies are sometimes termed "retired" galaxies. Their weak emission lines, when present, are powered by hot low-mass evolved stars (HOLMES) rather than young massive stars, and can be identified through <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">emission-line diagnostics</a><sup><a href="#ref8">[8]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a></sup>. M51b (NGC 5195) is a nearby example: despite being part of an interacting pair, it is a fully retired <a href="/pages/early-type-galaxies" class="wikilink">early-type galaxy</a><sup><a href="#ref7">[7]</a></sup>.
</p>
