<p>
  <strong>Recollimation shocks</strong> are oblique, quasi-stationary standing shocks that form where astrophysical jets refocus after lateral expansion, mediated by the confining pressure of the surrounding <a href="/pages/jet-cocoon-dynamics" class="wikilink">cocoon</a> <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. Analytic models <a href="#ref1">[1]</a> and MHD simulations <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a> show that these shocks can be strong and long-lived, making them plausible sites for <a href="/pages/diffusive-shock-acceleration" class="wikilink">diffusive shock acceleration (DSA)</a> capable of accelerating particles to PeV energies or beyond <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="formation">Formation Mechanism</h2>
<p>
  A jet propagating through an ambient medium drives a bow shock and inflates a hot, pressurized cocoon of shocked material <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref7">[7]</a>. Early hydrodynamic simulations by <a href="/pages/norman-1982" class="wikilink">Norman et al. (1982)</a> first identified cocoons around simulated jets <a href="#ref7">[7]</a>. The cocoon exerts an inward pressure on the jet, causing the laterally expanding flow to converge back toward the axis. At the convergence point, the jet material undergoes a sudden compression and deceleration, forming an oblique standing shock <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The analytic framework of <a href="/pages/bromberg-2011" class="wikilink">Bromberg et al. (2011)</a> describes this process quantitatively for relativistic jets <a href="#ref1">[1]</a>, deriving self-similar scalings for cocoon pressure and recollimation-shock location. Krause (2003) provided earlier analytic models of cocoon-driven collimation dependence on ambient conditions <a href="#ref2">[2]</a>. Peretti et al. (2026) extended the Bromberg framework to the sub-relativistic regime, enabling application to Seyfert galaxies, microquasars, and protostellar jets <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Role of <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic field</a>s</h3>
<p>
  MHD simulations reveal that magnetic field geometry significantly affects recollimation shock formation and structure. Mizuno et al. (2015) showed that axial versus toroidal field configurations produce different shock morphologies <a href="#ref3">[3]</a>. Moll et al. (2008) demonstrated that 3D kink instabilities can create additional shocks and knots along the jet <a href="#ref4">[4]</a>. Bodo et al. (2018) found that radiative cooling influences downstream flow focusing and can modify recollimation dynamics <a href="#ref5">[5]</a>. Giannios (2006) argued that rapid dissipation of Poynting flux leads to kinetically dominated jets at large scales, motivating hydrodynamic (rather than purely MHD) treatment of recollimation shocks <a href="#ref8">[8]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="oblique-geometry">Oblique Geometry and Compression</h2>
<p>
  Unlike head-on shocks, recollimation shocks are highly inclined to the jet axis. The shock's inclination angle ψ determines the compression ratio through the normal component of the upstream velocity <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. Peretti et al. (2026) parameterize this via k_p, which encodes the relationship between downstream and upstream pressure as a function of obliquity <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Compression ratio:</strong> Depends on the normal Mach number, which is reduced by obliquity</li>
  <li><strong>Reverse-shock strength:</strong> M₁ ~ 1/√(γ k_p), where γ is the adiabatic index <a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Despite obliquity:</strong> Shocks can still be strong enough for efficient DSA when the upstream Mach number is high <a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  The physics of particle acceleration at oblique shocks introduces additional complications. Kirk &amp; Heavens (1989) showed that oblique-shock DSA produces spectra that depend on the angle between the magnetic field and shock normal <a href="#ref9">[9]</a>. Gieseler et al. (1999) extended this to include cross-field diffusion effects <a href="#ref10">[10]</a>. Bell (2011) and Kirk et al. (2023) discussed guide-field effects that can suppress or enhance acceleration depending on geometry <a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a>. Reville et al. (2025) provided recent theoretical developments on oblique-shock acceleration efficiency <a href="#ref13">[13]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="quasi-stationarity">Quasi-Stationarity</h2>
<p>
  A crucial property for particle acceleration is that recollimation shocks remain at roughly fixed positions for extended times <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. This quasi-stationarity was predicted by analytic models <a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a> and confirmed in simulations <a href="#ref3">[3]</a><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a>. The cocoon pressure continuously balances jet ram pressure, maintaining stable shock locations and allowing particles to undergo many acceleration cycles <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  This contrasts with propagating shocks (e.g., internal shocks from jet variability), which were studied extensively in early work on jet emission. Marscher &amp; Gear (1985), Daly &amp; Marscher (1988), and Gómez et al. (1995, 1997) linked propagating shocks to observed variability and knot formation <a href="#ref14">[14]</a><a href="#ref15">[15]</a><a href="#ref16">[16]</a>. Komissarov &amp; Falle (1997) modeled standing versus propagating shocks in relativistic flows <a href="#ref17">[17]</a>. Recollimation shocks offer a distinct advantage: their longevity enables sustained, efficient acceleration rather than transient bursts <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="particle-acceleration">Particle Acceleration</h2>
<p>
  Peretti et al. (2026) developed a semi-analytic 1D transport model for DSA at recollimation shocks, predicting maximum proton energies that depend strongly on source class and <a href="/pages/turbulence-and-diffusion" class="wikilink">diffusion regime</a> <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Seyfert galaxies:</strong> PeV–EeV (Bohm optimistic); parameters from NGC 4151 and NGC 1068 observations <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref18">[18]</a><a href="#ref19">[19]</a><a href="#ref20">[20]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Microquasars:</strong> Up to tens of PeV; SS433 as prototype <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref21">[21]</a><a href="#ref22">[22]</a><a href="#ref23">[23]</a><a href="#ref24">[24]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Protostellar jets:</strong> Up to TeV; HH80-81 as prototype <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref25">[25]</a><a href="#ref26">[26]</a></li>
</ul>
<p>
  The maximum energy E_max is typically limited by upstream confinement (particles diffuse farther than the shock height) <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. However, downstream <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">turbulence</a> decay can become the limiting factor in some regimes <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. The diffusion regime matters enormously: Bohm diffusion yields the highest E_max, Kraichnan intermediate values, and Kolmogorov the lowest — potentially reducing E_max by orders of magnitude <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref27">[27]</a><a href="#ref28">[28]</a>.
</p>
<p>
  The Hillas (1984) confinement criterion provides order-of-magnitude estimates: E_max scales with the product of shock size and magnetic field strength <a href="#ref29">[29]</a>. Peretti et al. (2026) refine this to E_max ~ ε_B^(1/2) ṁ^(1/2) u₀^(3/2) / θ₀ in the Bohm limit <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h2 id="observational-evidence">Observational Evidence</h2>
<h3>Microquasars</h3>
<p>
  Recent gamma-ray and TeV detections strongly motivate recollimation shocks as particle accelerators. HAWC detected extended TeV emission from SS433/W50 <a href="#ref22">[22]</a>. H.E.S.S. confirmed TeV emission from SS433 jets <a href="#ref23">[23]</a>. LHAASO reported PeV-range detections from Galactic sources consistent with microquasar origins <a href="#ref24">[24]</a>. These observations, combined with earlier work establishing microquasars as relativistic jet sources <a href="#ref30">[30]</a><a href="#ref31">[31]</a>, support recollimation shocks as viable PeVatron candidates <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>. Recent studies by Abaroa et al. (2024) and Bykov et al. (2025) re-examine microquasar contributions to Galactic cosmic rays in this context <a href="#ref32">[32]</a><a href="#ref33">[33]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Seyfert Galaxies</h3>
<p>
  Sub-relativistic jets in Seyfert galaxies show radio emission consistent with shock-accelerated particles. Ulvestad &amp; Wilson (2005) mapped the radio jet in NGC 4151 <a href="#ref18">[18]</a>. Williams &amp; Bower (2017) provided further constraints on Seyfert jet properties <a href="#ref19">[19]</a>. Salvatore et al. (2023) characterized the jet in NGC 1068 <a href="#ref20">[20]</a>. These observations provide the prototype parameters used in recollimation shock models <a href="#ref6">[6]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Protostellar Jets</h3>
<p>
  Non-thermal emission from protostellar jets provides evidence for particle acceleration at shocks. Rodríguez-Kamenetzky et al. (2017) detected synchrotron emission from the HH80-81 jet <a href="#ref25">[25]</a>. Carrasco-González et al. (2021) provided further radio constraints <a href="#ref26">[26]</a>. Padovani et al. (2015) modeled ionization from cosmic rays accelerated in protostellar jets <a href="#ref34">[34]</a>.
</p>
<h3>Polarization Signatures</h3>
<p>
  Jet termination regions and recollimation shocks show enhanced polarization due to magnetic-field compression <a href="#ref38">[38]</a>. In <a href="/pages/3c84-ngc1275-jet" class="wikilink">3C 84</a>, the hotspot component C3 consistently shows polarized emission at 43–141 GHz, with <a href="/pages/faraday-rotation" class="wikilink">Faraday rotation measures</a> reflecting the surrounding circumnuclear medium rather than internal jet conditions <a href="#ref39">[39]</a>. This provides a complementary diagnostic for shock environments beyond non-thermal particle signatures.
</p>
<h2 id="open-questions">Open Questions</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Oblique-shock efficiency:</strong> How do guide fields and full obliquity physics modify acceleration? Current models use simplified treatments <a href="#ref6">[6]</a><a href="#ref9">[9]</a><a href="#ref11">[11]</a><a href="#ref12">[12]</a><a href="#ref13">[13]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Multi-dimensional effects:</strong> Kink instabilities <a href="#ref4">[4]</a> and lateral transport are not captured by 1D models <a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Relativistic extension:</strong> The sub-relativistic framework needs generalization for fast AGN/blazar jets; Perucho et al. (2007, 2014, 2022) provide relevant relativistic simulations <a href="#ref35">[35]</a><a href="#ref36">[36]</a><a href="#ref37">[37]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Cocoon hadronic signatures:</strong> Escaped particles may illuminate cocoons, producing extended gamma-ray/neutrino emission <a href="#ref6">[6]</a></li>
  <li><strong>Time-dependence:</strong> How does jet variability affect the quasi-stationary picture?</li>
</ul>
