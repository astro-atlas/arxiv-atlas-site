<p>
  <strong>Subclusters</strong> are local stellar overdensities within a forming or young star cluster — smaller groups of stars that formed from the same parent molecular cloud but in distinct spatial locations. Subcluster formation is a natural outcome of the hierarchical, <a href="/pages/fractal-structure-star-clusters" class="wikilink">fractal</a> nature of star cluster assembly from turbulent molecular clouds.<sup><a href="#ref1">1</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref2">2</a></sup>
</p>
<h2 id="mechanisms">Formation Mechanisms</h2>
<p>
  Two primary mechanisms for subcluster formation have been identified in MHD+N-body simulations<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>
    <strong>Feedback-triggered:</strong> A massive star forms in a primary cluster and expels surrounding gas via photoionization or stellar winds. The expelled gas sweeps up into a dense shell, which becomes gravitationally unstable and collapses to form a secondary subcluster at the edge of the feedback region. This was observed in model n3s6 of Akhmetali et al. (2026)<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup>.
  </li>
  <li>
    <strong>Dynamical (proximity merging):</strong> Two subclusters form in close proximity and begin to merge under their mutual gravitational attraction. After merging into a single system, dynamical interactions can cause the system to re-fragment into two or more subclusters. This results in oscillating Q values (merging → Q rises; separation → Q falls) and was observed in models n3s7 and n4s1.<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup>
  </li>
</ul>
<h2 id="observational-evidence">Observational Evidence</h2>
<p>
  Substructure in young star clusters is well-established observationally. Kuhn et al. (2014)<sup><a href="#ref2">2</a></sup> identified subclusters in Chandra observations of massive star-forming regions. Sun et al. (2022)<sup><a href="#ref4">4</a></sup> found hierarchical subcluster structure in the W40 and Serpens South region. Bastian et al. (2009)<sup><a href="#ref5">5</a></sup> traced the spatial evolution of stellar structures in the LMC. Some clusters retain substructure well beyond 100 Myr (e.g., NGC 1513, NGC 1641<sup><a href="#ref6">6</a></sup>).
</p>
<h2 id="effect-on-fractality">Effect on Cluster Fractality</h2>
<p>
  The presence and dynamics of subclusters directly control the evolution of the <a href="/pages/q-parameter-fractality" class="wikilink">Q parameter</a>:
</p>
<ul>
  <li>When subclusters are present and spatially separated, Q is low (fractal).</li>
  <li>During merging events, the stellar distribution momentarily concentrates, raising Q.</li>
  <li>Re-separation lowers Q again, producing oscillations.</li>
  <li>In simulations where subcluster activity persists (via feedback or repeated merging), fractality can be maintained beyond 4 t<sub>ff</sub>.<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h2 id="stochasticity">Stochasticity</h2>
<p>
  Feedback-triggered subcluster formation is not universal. Of 13 simulations with the same initial conditions but different random seeds, only 3 developed persistent subclusters.<sup><a href="#ref3">3</a></sup> The outcome depends critically on whether a massive star forms and when and where it forms relative to dense gas concentrations — an inherently stochastic process driven by the IMF sampling.
</p>
