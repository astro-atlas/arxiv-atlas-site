<p>
  <strong>Molecular clouds</strong> are the densest, coldest phase of the <a href="/pages/interstellar-medium" class="wikilink">interstellar medium</a>, composed primarily of molecular hydrogen (H₂) with traces of CO, N₂H⁺, NH₃, and other species. They are the exclusive sites of <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a> in galaxies — all known stars form within molecular clouds.
</p>
<h2 id="section-0">Structure</h2>
<p>
  Molecular clouds are hierarchical: giant molecular clouds (GMCs, 10⁴–10⁶ M☉, 10–100 pc) contain clumps (~10²–10³ M☉, ~1 pc) which contain cores (~1–10 M☉, ~0.1 pc). This hierarchy is organized along <a href="/pages/filamentary-star-formation" class="wikilink">filamentary structures</a> that pervade the ISM<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>.
</p>
<p>
  <a href="/pages/column-density" class="wikilink">Column density</a> is a primary observable, measured via <a href="/pages/dust" class="wikilink">dust</a> continuum emission or molecular line observations. High-column-density clouds (N(H₂) > 10²² cm⁻²) that appear dark against the mid-infrared background are classified as <a href="/pages/infrared-dark-clouds" class="wikilink">infrared dark clouds (IRDCs)</a><sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-1">Physics and Dynamics</h2>
<p>
  The internal dynamics of molecular clouds are governed by three forces: <a href="/pages/gravity" class="wikilink">self-gravity</a>, <a href="/pages/turbulence" class="wikilink">supersonic turbulence</a>, and <a href="/pages/magnetic-field" class="wikilink">magnetic fields</a>. Clouds are observed to have non-thermal velocity dispersions far exceeding thermal values, indicating turbulent support<sup><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. Magnetic fields thread the clouds and can be mapped via <a href="/pages/dust-polarimetry" class="wikilink">dust polarimetry</a>.
</p>
<p>
  Cloud masses are estimated from molecular line emission (e.g., C¹⁸O LTE analysis) or dust continuum. In G351.77-0.53, C¹⁸O(2–1) observations yield a total mass of ~4150 M☉ within a 7.8 pc filament<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="section-2">Key Surveys</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>ATLASGAL</strong> — 870 μm survey cataloging ~8000 dense Galactic clumps<sup><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup></li>
  <li><strong>Herschel Gould Belt Survey</strong> — mapped nearby molecular clouds at 70–500 μm</li>
  <li><strong>ALMA-IMF</strong> — high-resolution study of massive cloud fragmentation<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup></li>
</ul>
