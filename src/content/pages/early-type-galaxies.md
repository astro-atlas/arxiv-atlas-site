<p>
  <strong>Early-type galaxies (ETGs)</strong> &mdash; ellipticals and lenticulars &mdash; are characterized by smooth, featureless morphologies, old stellar populations, red colors, and little or no ongoing <a href="/pages/star-formation" class="wikilink">star formation</a>. They dominate the <a href="/pages/red-sequence-green-valley-blue-cloud" class="wikilink">red sequence</a> and are found preferentially in dense environments such as <a href="/pages/galaxy-groups" class="wikilink">galaxy groups</a> and clusters<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a><a href="#ref2">[2]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="properties">Properties</h2>
<p>
  ETGs are pressure-supported systems dominated by random stellar motions rather than ordered rotation. They have high stellar mass surface densities, old mass-weighted ages (&gt;5 Gyr typically), and high stellar metallicities reflecting extended early enrichment<sup><a href="#ref2">[2]</a><a href="#ref3">[3]</a></sup>. Their weak <a href="/pages/emission-line-diagnostics" class="wikilink">emission lines</a>, when present, are powered by hot low-mass evolved stars (HOLMES) rather than young massive stars, earning them the designation "retired galaxies"<sup><a href="#ref4">[4]</a><a href="#ref5">[5]</a></sup>.
</p>
<h2 id="formation">Formation Pathways</h2>
<p>
  ETGs are thought to form through a combination of <a href="/pages/galaxy-mergers" class="wikilink">mergers</a> (which destroy disk structure and build spheroidal morphology) and <a href="/pages/quenching-star-formation" class="wikilink">quenching</a> (which halts star formation)<sup><a href="#ref1">[1]</a></sup>. M51b (NGC 5195), the companion to M51a, exemplifies an ETG within an interacting pair: despite ongoing interaction with a star-forming spiral, M51b shows uniformly old populations and no significant star formation &mdash; a fully retired galaxy<sup><a href="#ref6">[6]</a></sup>.
</p>
