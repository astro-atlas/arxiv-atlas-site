## ABSTRACT

### Key Claims
1. **DF9 lacks dark matter**: DF9's stellar velocity dispersion is 6.4+4.0/-4.3 km/s, consistent with the 8.3+0.9/-1.4 km/s expected from its 1.4×10^8 M☉ stellar mass alone
2. **Dark matter not required**: The dispersion is far below the 27±3 km/s expected if DF9 fell on the stellar mass-halo mass relation
3. **Trail galaxies formed together**: Results are consistent with the "bullet dwarf" scenario prediction that trail galaxies should lack dark matter like DF2 and DF4
4. **Unique formation channel**: The trail of low mass galaxies in NGC 1052 field formed together through a unique galaxy formation mechanism

## SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION

### Key Claims
1. **Standard paradigm of galaxy formation**: Galaxies form within dark matter halos, starting as gas that cools inside halos and forms stars; low mass dwarf galaxies are most dark matter dominated (>100× more massive DM halos than stellar mass) due to feedback mechanisms
2. **DF2 and DF4 are anomalous**: These two galaxies have total masses consistent with stellar masses alone, extremely luminous globular clusters, and are spatially extended ultra-diffuse galaxies - properties that set them apart from all previously known galaxies
3. **Trail of galaxies is unique**: DF2 and DF4 are part of a tight linear alignment of ~12 faint galaxies with velocities linearly increasing based on position along trail, suggesting common origin
4. **Bullet dwarf scenario explains observations**: High-speed collision scenario explains DF2/DF4's luminous globular clusters, large sizes, monochromatic GC colors, and predicts other trail galaxies should also lack dark matter
5. **DF9 is ideal test case**: DF9 is the closest analog to DF2/DF4 with similar luminosity, size, and bright cluster population, making it feasible to measure velocity dispersion with KCWI

### Section 2.2: Data Reduction

#### Key Claims
1. **KCWI Pipeline with custom modifications**: Used KCWI DRP for initial reduction but employed custom cosmic ray detection adapted from CRCombine package
2. **Cosmic ray removal method**: Identified cosmic rays by taking difference between exposure pairs and masking pixels exceeding 99-percentile threshold in noise-normalized difference image
3. **Sky subtraction technique**: Used neighboring four sky frames rescaled based on weighting so combined sky frame matches strength of sky lines in 1D on-target spectra at wavelengths unaffected by galaxy spectrum
4. **Frame combination procedure**: Converted vacuum to air wavelengths, applied constant wavelength shift for common grid, spatially aligned frames by fitting 2D models of DF9 and star clusters, masked high-sigma outliers
5. **1D spectrum extraction**: Fit 2D models of DF9 and star clusters to collapsed 3D frame, performed weighted sum multiplying flux by fitted galaxy model, excluding pixels <15% of peak flux and contaminated by star clusters

### Section 2.3: Model Fitting & MCMC Parameter Estimation

#### Key Claims
1. **Template fitting approach**: Fit 1D spectrum using synthetic stellar population spectra generated with MIST isochrones, using 12 templates with metallicities [-1.5, -1.25, -1.0] and ages [7, 8, 9, 10] Gyr
2. **Instrumental resolution measurement**: Measured wavelength-dependent resolution by fitting Gaussians to sky lines, finding σ_inst = 0.375Å (13.0 km/s) with 0.007Å (0.2 km/s) standard deviation
3. **MCMC fitting procedure**: Used emcee algorithm to constrain posterior distributions for velocity dispersion σ and redshift z, fitting 1st order polynomial to continuum for both template and spectrum
4. **Systemic velocity measurement**: Fit velocity v_DF9 = 1661±2 km/s, above prior LRIS measurement of 1644±4 km/s and closer to prior KCWI result of 1680±40 km/s
5. **Velocity dispersion from fitting**: Average dispersion σ_fit = 8.7+3.4/-3.7 km/s combining all template fits and accounting for instrumental resolution uncertainty

## SECTION 3: RESULTS

### Section 3.1: Velocity Dispersion

#### Key Claims
1. **Stellar motion corrections required**: For low-dispersion systems, must account for internal stellar rotation/macroturbulence and binary star orbital motion, which don't affect resolved population measurements but do affect integrated light
2. **Stellar broadening effect**: Rotation and macroturbulence broadening estimated as σ_broadening = 5.4±2 km/s based on DF4 analysis with similar age/metallicity
3. **Binary orbital motion effect**: Binary confusion noise estimated as σ_binaries = α × σ_b where α is binary fraction (60% solar neighborhood, ~10% in globular clusters, up to ~100% in low-density systems) and σ_b = 2.87 km/s; estimated σ_binaries = 2.3±0.6 km/s for DF9
4. **Gravitational velocity dispersion**: After subtracting stellar effects in quadrature, σ = 6.4+4.0/-4.3 km/s
5. **Consistency with DF2/DF4**: DF9's velocity dispersion consistent with DF2 (6.1+3.7/-3.1 km/s) and DF4 (7.7+2.0/-2.4 km/s) after updating for stellar motion effects

### Section 3.4: Constraints from Star Clusters

#### Key Claims
1. **Two star cluster velocities measured**: Prior work measured velocities of two star clusters (one likely globular cluster, one bright central cluster) from Keck/LRIS spectra
2. **Low-N consistency check**: With two tracers, uncertainty is high but can provide useful consistency check - large velocity differences would rule out models without dark matter
3. **Statistical argument**: If Δv > 20 km/s for both clusters relative to diffuse light, σ = 7 km/s would be ruled out as requiring 2 of 2 samples to be 3σ outliers
4. **Likelihood function construction**: Constructed likelihood incorporating cluster velocities v_i and diffuse light systemic velocity μ as true mean, applying Jeffreys prior correction for low-N bias
5. **Cluster constraint result**: LRIS cluster constraint gives 7+23/-6 km/s (68% confidence), consistent with low KCWI dispersion; both clusters have very similar velocities to diffuse light (1649±2, 1645+5/-6 vs 1644±4 km/s)

### Section 3.5: Comparison to Wider Population

#### Key Claims
1. **DF2/DF4/DF9 are outliers in r_e-σ-M_★ plane**: Galaxies with similar dispersions are several times smaller and have orders of magnitude lower stellar mass; galaxies with similar stellar masses have much larger dispersions
2. **Comparison sample**: Included dispersion-supported, non-rotating dwarfs from Local Group and Virgo Cluster satisfying v_rot/σ criteria
3. **Halo mass calculation procedure**: Calculated M_e from velocity dispersion, subtracted stellar mass, fit NFW halo mass with same cosmology/concentration as SMHM relation, adjusted for universal baryon fraction
4. **Universal baryon fraction constraint**: Most dwarfs have halo masses ~100× stellar mass; galaxies cannot fall below universal baryon fraction line unless dark matter affected by interactions
5. **DF2/DF4/DF9 fall below universal baryon fraction**: All three galaxies fall below this line at all uncertainties, requiring either extremely small dark matter halos (unphysical baryon fractions) or lack of dark matter

### Section 4.1: A Third Galaxy without Dark Matter

#### Key Claims
1. **No dark matter required**: For all three galaxies (DF2, DF4, DF9), dynamical mass from gravitational motion of stars is consistent with stellar mass from emitted light

### Section 4.2: Formation Theories

#### Key Claims
1. **Common origin certain**: The tight, kinematically-connected trail of galaxies almost certainly formed together through mechanism causing dark matter deficiency
2. **Bullet dwarf prediction confirmed**: DF9 lacking dark matter was specific and original prediction of bullet dwarf collision model

5. **Bullet dwarf explains all properties**: Only formation theory explaining monochromatic extremely luminous globular clusters, large sizes, trail alignment, kinematics, and dark matter deficiency

## APPENDIX B: The Low Distance Sensitivity of Results

### Key Claims
1. **Distance assumption**: Paper assumes DF9 at 20.6 Mpc based on DF2/DF4 positions and distances under linear trail relation
2. **Distance uncertainties**: DF2 and DF4 best distance estimates have Mpc-scale uncertainties, plus modest scatter in linear distance expectation from simulations
3. **Low sensitivity demonstrated**: Placing DF9 at 16.2 Mpc (3σ low) or 25.0 Mpc (3σ high) has little effect on results
4. **Scaling relation**: Expected stellar dispersion proportional to √(M/r) ∝ √d, so 21% distance difference affects expected dispersion by only 11%
5. **All elements recalculated**: For both DF2, DF4, and DF9, all elements including stellar mass enclosed, expected stellar dispersion, DM halo potential, and expected DM dispersion recalculated at adjusted distances

## Broader Context

### nfw-profile
The NFW profile page describes the standard dark matter halo density model. Keim et al. use NFW profiles to predict expected velocity dispersions for DF9 assuming a standard dark matter halo (27±3 km/s), which is strongly inconsistent with the observed 6.4 km/s — making this paper a striking counterexample to NFW-based mass predictions at the dwarf galaxy scale.

### baryon-fraction-in-galaxy-clusters
The baryon fraction page focuses on cluster-scale measurements where f_b ≈ 0.157. Keim et al. show that DF2, DF4, and DF9 all fall *below* the universal baryon fraction line, meaning their baryonic mass exceeds what would be expected if they had any dark matter halo — a fundamentally different regime from clusters that approximate cosmic baryon fractions.

### agn-feedback
AGN feedback is mentioned only peripherally as an alternative formation scenario (quasar-driven outflows). The paper does not engage deeply with AGN physics; the connection is that some theories propose AGN jets could expel gas to form dark-matter-free dwarfs, but the bullet dwarf scenario is strongly preferred here.

### ram-pressure-stripping
Ram-pressure stripping is mentioned as an alternative mechanism for removing gas/dark matter from galaxies. However, the paper argues the bullet dwarf collision scenario better explains the full suite of observations (trail alignment, luminous GCs, kinematics) compared to ram-pressure stripping alone.

### star-formation
Star formation is referenced in the standard galaxy formation paradigm (gas cooling in halos → star formation). DF9 and trail galaxies represent an extreme case where star formation occurred from gas separated from dark matter during a high-speed collision, outside the normal bottom-up framework.

### gravity
Gravity is the underlying physics for all dynamical mass measurements in this paper. The key result — that gravitational velocity dispersion matches stellar mass predictions — relies on Newtonian gravity within the half-light radius.

## Critical Analysis

### Key Claims and Findings

1. **DF9 lacks dark matter**: Stellar velocity dispersion σ = 6.4+4.0/-4.3 km/s is consistent with the 8.3 km/s expected from stellar mass alone, but far below the 27±3 km/s expected from a standard NFW dark matter halo following the SMHM relation.

2. **Three dark-matter-free galaxies now confirmed**: DF2, DF4, and DF9 all have dynamical masses consistent with their stellar masses, with all three falling below the universal baryon fraction — a result that cannot be explained by scatter in the SMHM relation.

3. **Bullet dwarf scenario validated**: The prediction that trail galaxies should lack dark matter, made *before* DF9 was measured, has been confirmed. This is a genuine prediction-then-verification sequence, strengthening the bullet dwarf collision hypothesis.

4. **Trail galaxies share common origin**: The linear alignment of ~12 galaxies with kinematically connected velocities, combined with dark matter deficiency in the three brightest members, provides strong evidence for formation through a single high-speed collision event.

5. **Systematic corrections to velocity dispersion are critical**: Stellar rotation/macroturbulence (5.4±2 km/s) and binary orbital motion (2.3±0.6 km/s) must be subtracted in quadrature from integrated-light measurements. These corrections are comparable to the dispersion itself, making them scientifically crucial.

### Supporting Papers/Concepts

- **Bullet dwarf scenario** (Silk 2019, Shin et al. 2020, Lee et al. 2021, Lee et al. 2024): Simulations that predicted the trail and dark matter deficiency before confirmation
- **NFW profile** (Navarro et al. 1997): Standard halo model used as comparison benchmark
- **SMHM relation** (Zahra et al. 2023, Danieli et al. 2023): Framework showing DF9 is a >5σ outlier
- **DF2/DF4 discoveries** (van Dokkum et al. 2018, 2019): Established the class of dark-matter-free galaxies

### Potential Disputes

**DISPUTE: Dark matter content of DF2**
Multiple independent measurements confirm DF2 lacks significant dark matter (van Dokkum et al. 2018; Wasserman et al. 2018; Danieli et al. 2019; Emsellem et al. 2019). However, some authors have questioned the distance to DF2, which would affect the mass estimate. The paper treats this as settled based on HST TRGB distances.

**DISPUTE: Survivability of dark-matter-free galaxies**
Ogiya (2022) questioned whether dark-matter-free galaxies could survive tidal disruption in the NGC 1052 group environment. Lee et al. (2024) responded with simulations showing survivability is possible under the specific tidal field conditions. This remains an active discussion.

## Key Takeaways

### Main Results

- **DF9's velocity dispersion (σ = 6.4+4.0/-4.3 km/s) confirms it lacks dark matter**, consistent with its stellar mass alone (8.3 km/s expected) and far below the 27±3 km/s expected from a standard dark matter halo [van Dokkum et al. 2018, Zahra et al. 2023].
- **Three galaxies on the NGC 1052 trail are now confirmed dark-matter-free** (DF2, DF4, DF9), all falling below the universal baryon fraction line — ruling out normal scatter in the stellar mass-halo mass relation [van Dokkum et al. 2019, Keim et al. 2022].
- **The bullet dwarf collision scenario made a falsifiable prediction that has been confirmed**: trail galaxies should lack dark matter, and DF9 validates this [Silk 2019, Shin et al. 2020, Lee et al. 2024].
- **Systematic corrections for stellar broadening and binary motion are essential** for low-dispersion systems, reducing the measured 8.7 km/s to 6.4 km/s after quadrature subtraction [Shen et al. 2023].
- **Star cluster velocities provide an independent consistency check**, with both DF9 clusters showing velocities very close to the diffuse light systemic velocity, consistent with a low-mass system [Keim et al. 2025].

### Implications

This paper strengthens the case that the NGC 1052 trail represents a fundamentally different galaxy formation channel — one where dark matter and baryons were physically separated by a high-speed collision. The confirmation of a third dark-matter-free galaxy transforms what was a two-object curiosity into a systematic pattern, making it much harder to dismiss as measurement error or statistical fluke. For galaxy evolution, this demonstrates that the assumed universality of the dark matter halo framework breaks down in specific extreme environments, with implications for how we interpret the faintest galaxies and the scatter in scaling relations.

### Literature Context

Keim et al. (2026) sits at the intersection of dark matter physics, dwarf galaxy studies, and galaxy formation theory. The paper directly builds on the discoveries of DF2 and DF4 (van Dokkum et al. 2018, 2019) and the bullet dwarf collision model (Silk 2019; Shin et al. 2020; Lee et al. 2021, 2024). It supports the growing recognition that ultra-diffuse galaxies span an enormous range of dark matter content — from the most dark-matter-dominated systems known to systems with essentially none. The work challenges the standard bottom-up galaxy formation paradigm by providing a concrete example of top-down formation from collision debris, and connects to broader questions about the nature of dark matter through the demonstrated physical separability of dark and baryonic matter.
