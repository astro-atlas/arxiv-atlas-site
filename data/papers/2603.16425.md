# Unveiling an Hourglass-Shaped Magnetic Field toward IRDC G351.77–0.53

**arXiv:** 2603.16425  
**Authors:** O. R. Jadhav, L. K. Dewangan, I. I. Zinchenko, Thushara G. S. Pillai, Patricio Sanhueza, A. K. Maity, Ram K. Yadav, Saurabh Sharma  
**Affiliations:** Physical Research Laboratory (India); IIT Gandhinagar; IAP RAS (Russia); MIT Haystack Observatory; University of Tokyo; NARIT (Thailand); ARIES (India)

---

## Abstract

Multi-wavelength study of the infrared dark cloud G351.77-0.53 using SOFIA/HAWC+ 214 μm far-infrared polarimetric observations reveals a striking hourglass-shaped magnetic (B-)field morphology toward the central massive star-forming clump c1. The plane-of-sky B-field is predominantly perpendicular to the filament, consistent with Planck large-scale observations, except near c1 where the hourglass geometry indicates B-field pinching. Using the Davis-Chandrasekhar-Fermi (DCF) method, the mean B-field strength across the filament is ~147 ± 60 μG, with values reaching ~0.8 mG near c1. Mass-to-flux ratio analysis indicates the filament is magnetically transcritical (λ ~ 0.9 ± 0.6). Energy budget analysis shows that magnetic, gravitational, and kinetic energies are comparable, indicating B-fields play an important role in governing cloud gas dynamics. The hourglass morphology likely arises from magnetically regulated gravitational collapse and/or converging sub-filaments aligned with the B-field.

## Section-by-Section Analysis

### 1. Introduction

**Summary:** The authors motivate a study of the infrared dark cloud (IRDC) G351.77-0.53 by reviewing the role of magnetic fields in filamentary star formation. Filaments are ubiquitous in the ISM and host dense star-forming cores. IRDCs are dense, cold filamentary clouds seen in absorption against the mid-infrared background, representing the earliest stages of massive star and cluster formation. Despite extensive prior work on G351, no polarimetric study of its B-field morphology had been conducted. The authors aim to characterize the B-field, estimate its strength using the DCF method, and assess the relative importance of magnetic, gravitational, and turbulent energy.

**Key factual claims:**
- Filaments are prevalent in the ISM and linked to both low- and high-mass star formation.
- IRDCs trace the earliest stages of massive star formation due to their high column densities and low temperatures.
- B-fields are generally perpendicular to high-column-density filaments (N(H₂) > 5×10²¹ cm⁻²) but can be parallel or random in low-density filaments.
- G351.77-0.53 hosts massive protocluster IRAS 17233-3606, with 6.7 GHz Class II CH₃OH masers and several outflow activities.
- Distance to G351: ~2.0 kpc based on Gaia DR3 parallax (adopted for this work).

### 3. Results

#### 3.1 Physical Environment toward the G351 IRDC

**Summary:** The G351 cloud is seen in absorption at 12 μm against the MIR background, with ATLASGAL clumps concentrated in the northern region. A total of 53 Class I YSOs were identified using [4.5]−[5.8] vs [3.6]−[4.5] color-color diagrams, confirming active star formation throughout the filament. SMGPS 1.3 GHz emission is concentrated only toward clumps c1 and c2, while absence of radio emission toward most of the filament implies an early evolutionary stage. A Spitzer 4.5/3.6 μm ratio map shows bright regions consistent with H₂ emission (4.69 μm) near Class I protostars, while darker areas trace PAH emission (3.3 μm).

**Key factual claims:**
- 53 Class I YSOs identified using Spitzer CCD method.
- SMGPS 1.3 GHz emission is confined to clumps c1 and c2.
- The 4.5/3.6 μm ratio traces H₂ emission at Class I protostar locations.
- Absence of radio continuum toward most of the filament indicates an early evolutionary stage.

#### 3.2 POS B-field Orientations from SOFIA/HAWC+ and Planck

**Summary:** B-field position angles are derived by rotating polarization vectors by 90°, retaining vectors with p/σ_p > 3, I/σ_I > 100, and p < 30%. SOFIA/HAWC+ 214 μm data reveals a clear hourglass morphology toward clump c1, while B-field lines remain predominantly perpendicular to the filament's major axis elsewhere. A Line Integral Convolution (LIC) map using the magnetar package confirms this morphology. Planck 353 GHz data show B-field vectors predominantly perpendicular to the G351 cloud, consistent with the HAWC+ large-scale trend.

**Key factual claims:**
- B-field position angle: θ = (1/2) arctan2(U, Q), with vectors rotated by 90°.
- Quality cuts: p/σ_p > 3, I/σ_I > 100, p < 30%.
- Hourglass B-field morphology detected toward c1; perpendicular to filament elsewhere.
- Planck B-field is predominantly perpendicular to the G351 filament, consistent with HAWC+.

#### 3.4 Estimation of Magnetic Field Strength

**Summary:** The plane-of-sky B-field strength (BPOS) is estimated using the Davis-Chandrasekhar-Fermi (DCF) method with a correction factor Q = 0.5. The structure function method (Hildebrand et al. 2009; Houde et al. 2009) is used to estimate the polarization angle dispersion δθ = 8.3° ± 1.5° for the full cloud, with a sliding box method yielding 12.5° ± 5.3°. The mean non-thermal velocity dispersion from C¹⁸O(2–1) is ΔVnt = 1.47 ± 0.41 km/s. The mean BPOS from the structure function method is ~147 ± 60 μG, and from the pixel map is ~170 ± 127 μG. BPOS reaches ~0.8 mG toward c1. The mass-to-flux ratio λ_obs ~ 0.9 ± 0.6 indicates transcritical magnetization, with supercritical values (λ > 1) near c1.

**Key factual claims:**
- DCF formula used: BPOS ≈ 9.3 √n(H₂) × ΔVnt/δθ (μG) with Q = 0.5.
- Best-fit structure function parameters: b = 11.8° ± 1.5°, m = 0.53° ± 0.01°.
- Angular dispersion δθ = 8.3° ± 1.5° (structure function); 12.5° ± 5.3° (sliding box).
- Mean BPOS: ~147 ± 60 μG (SF method); ~170 ± 127 μG (pixel map).
- Peak BPOS: ~0.8 mG near c1.
- Mass-to-flux ratio: λ_obs ~ 0.9 ± 0.6 (transcritical); λ > 1 near c1.
- Total B-field: Btot = 1.3 × BPOS (from Crutcher 2004).
- Beam/LOS integration effects (Houde et al. 2009) can reduce effective angular dispersion and overestimate BPOS.

#### 3.5 Energy Balance

**Summary:** The virial theorem is applied to quantify the relative contributions of gravity (EG), turbulence/kinetic energy (EK), and magnetic energy (EB) for the G351 cloud treated as a cylinder. EG = −GM²/L ~ −1.88 × 10⁴⁷ ergs, EK = MσV² ~ 1.79 × 10⁴⁷ ergs, EB = B²V/8π ~ 3.20 × 10⁴⁷ ergs. Within the large uncertainties (factors of 2–4), all three energies are comparable, confirming that B-fields are dynamically important in the G351 cloud.

**Key factual claims:**
- EG ≈ 1.88 ± 1.88 × 10⁴⁷ ergs; EK ≈ 1.79 ± 1.04 × 10⁴⁷ ergs; EB ≈ 3.20 ± 2.24 × 10⁴⁷ ergs.
- B-fields provide the dominant energy contribution, followed by turbulence and gravity.
- Derived energies could be underestimated by factors of up to 4 (EG), 2 (EK), 2 (EB) based on mass uncertainties.
- Cylindrical geometry assumed for gravitational and magnetic energy calculations.

**Citation map:**

### 4. Discussion

#### 4.1 Hourglass-shaped B-field due to Magnetically Regulated Collapse

**Summary:** The authors interpret the hourglass B-field morphology as arising from magnetically regulated gravitational collapse. Large-scale Planck observations show field lines perpendicular to the filament, consistent with other filamentary cloud studies. The DCF-estimated BPOS ~ 147 μG, with ~0.8 mG peaks near c1, supports dynamically important B-fields. Nakamura & Li (2008) simulations show that when turbulence fails to support against gravitational settling along field lines, ambipolar diffusion allows dense cores to become self-gravitating and collapse, bending initially perpendicular B-field lines into an hourglass morphology. This is consistent with the G351 mass-to-flux ratio transition from transcritical on large scales to supercritical near c1. Gómez et al. (2018) further show that gas flows along filaments bend perpendicular B-fields into U-shapes, which become hourglass shapes with accretion from both sides.

**Key factual claims:**
- Large-scale Planck field is consistent with B-fields perpendicular to the filament (as in many other clouds).
- Mass-to-flux ratio transitions from transcritical (~0.9) on cloud scales to supercritical (>1) near c1.
- No longitudinal velocity gradient detected in C¹⁸O, likely due to small projection along LOS.

**Citation map:**
- [Planck_2016] Planck Collaboration 2016 DOI:10.1051/0004-6361/201525896: B-fields predominantly perpendicular to filaments, consistent with G351 Planck observations.
- [Soler_2017] Soler 2017 DOI:10.1051/0004-6361/201731049: Transition of B-field orientation from perpendicular to parallel at N(H₂) ~ 5×10²¹ cm⁻².

#### 4.2 Hourglass-shaped B-field due to Converging Sub-filaments

**Summary:** An alternative interpretation attributes the hourglass morphology to converging sub-filaments aligned with the B-field. Leurini et al. (2019) identified multiple sub-filaments feeding into the G351 central filament, consistent with a hub-filament system (HFS). B-field lines align parallel to low-density sub-filaments (striations) but are perpendicular to high-density regions, with the transition at N(H₂) ~ 5 × 10²¹ cm⁻². In the G351 cloud, HAWC+ B-field vectors align with sub-filaments in the Spitzer 8.0 μm image. The non-perpendicular orientation of sub-filaments to the main filament causes the field to transition from striation-aligned to filament-perpendicular, naturally producing an hourglass-like geometry. This resembles Pillai et al. (2020) in Serpens South, where B-field lines follow inflowing filaments to the main hub.

**Key factual claims:**
- Sub-filaments in G351 are not perpendicular to the main filament, distinguishing this case from simple hub-filament geometry.
- B-field aligns with sub-filaments; transition from parallel (low-density) to perpendicular (high-density) produces apparent hourglass shape.
- A similar pattern is observed in Musca (Cox et al. 2016) and Serpens South (Pillai et al. 2020).
- Both gravitational collapse and sub-filament accretion may jointly produce the observed morphology.

#### 4.3 Comparison with Other Filamentary Clouds and Important Caveats

**Summary:** G351 shows remarkable similarity to OMC-1 (Orion A) and DR21, where B-fields are also perpendicular to the filament on large scales but form hourglasses in the dense interior. In OMC-1, JCMT and SOFIA studies show magnetic energy dominates the energy budget, with gravity becoming relatively more important in dense regions — identical to the behavior observed in G351. The DR21 filament (JCMT/POL-2 and SCUBA-2) also shows perpendicular B-fields on pc-scales with hourglass morphology toward its most massive core. However, hourglass-shaped B-fields in IRDCs specifically are rare, highlighting the importance of this G351 result. Significant DCF method uncertainties are discussed: Q factor ranges from 0.2–1 depending on conditions; projection effects, LOS averaging, and cylindrical geometry assumptions all contribute to uncertainties potentially exceeding a factor of 2.

**Key factual claims:**
- DCF correction factor Q can range from 0.2 to 1 depending on turbulence regime, cloud geometry, and projection.
- In strongly magnetized systems, Q approaches unity; in weakly magnetized/self-gravitating systems, Q ~ 0.3–0.4.
- Kinetic energy contributions from stellar feedback and intrinsic turbulence are difficult to disentangle.
- G351 lies close to the critical mass-to-flux threshold within combined uncertainties.

**Citation map:**

## 5. Summary and Conclusions

**Summary:** The paper presents a multi-wavelength study of the G351 IRDC using SOFIA/HAWC+ 214 μm polarimetry. Six major findings are reported: (1) 53 Class I YSOs confirm active star formation along the filament; (2) B-field is predominantly perpendicular to the filament; (3) mean BPOS ~ 147 ± 60 μG with λ ~ 1 (transcritical); (4) all three energy components (magnetic, kinetic, gravitational) are comparable in the cloud; (5) BPOS reaches ~0.8 mG near c1, with hourglass morphology; (6) the hourglass arises from gravitational collapse, sub-filament inflow, or both. The overall conclusion is that B-fields are dynamically important in regulating massive star formation in G351.

## Critical Analysis

### Strengths
1. **Multi-wavelength coherence:** The paper combines six distinct datasets (Spitzer, unWISE, SOFIA/HAWC+, ATLASGAL, Planck, APEX C¹⁸O, MeerKAT) in a self-consistent analysis. This cross-validation of different physical tracers strengthens the main conclusions about B-field morphology and cloud energetics.

2. **Quantitative energy budget with uncertainty characterization:** The authors explicitly quantify magnetic, kinetic, and gravitational energies and provide careful uncertainty estimates including systematic effects from mass estimation differences (factor of ~2 discrepancy with Ryabukhina & Zinchenko 2021). The acknowledgment that Herschel band saturation limits TexT_ex spatial variation is intellectually honest.

3. **Dual-mechanism interpretation with clear observational predictions:** Rather than claiming a single explanation for the hourglass morphology, the authors present both (a) gravitational pinching and (b) sub-filament alignment as viable hypotheses, and explicitly state what future observations (high-resolution polarimetry + spectral lines) are needed to distinguish them. This is scientifically rigorous.

### Weaknesses
1. **DCF method limitations inadequately corrected:** The correction factor Q = 0.5 is chosen as a single value, but the authors acknowledge in Section 4.3 that Q can range from 0.2 to 1 depending on physical conditions. For a cloud transitioning from transcritical to supercritical, the appropriate Q is likely location-dependent. The final B-field strength estimates (~147–170 μG) carry at least a factor-of-2 systematic uncertainty from this choice alone, and the beam/LOS averaging effects noted after Eq. 8 could further reduce the effective angular dispersion without a correction applied.

2. **LTE mass estimate lacks spatial temperature variation:** The authors adopt a single Tex = 20 K throughout the cloud due to Herschel band saturation near c1, while acknowledging this leads to ~50% mass underestimate compared to Ryabukhina & Zinchenko (2021)'s pixel-by-pixel temperature map approach. The hotter c1 region likely has significantly higher Tex, meaning the mass (and therefore EG and EB) could be substantially underestimated precisely where the hourglass is detected — undermining the energy balance interpretation locally.

3. **No direct velocity structure evidence for infall/collapse at c1:** The discussion of magnetically regulated collapse (Section 4.1) invokes infall signatures but acknowledges no longitudinal velocity gradient is detected in C¹⁸O, attributed to projection effects. Without kinematic evidence of collapse or accretion specifically toward c1, the gravitational collapse interpretation rests primarily on the B-field morphology itself, creating some circularity.

### Open Questions
- What is the 3D geometry of the B-field in G351? The POS-only measurement from dust polarimetry cannot distinguish whether the hourglass is due to gravitational pinching or sub-filament accretion without 3D starlight polarization constraints.
- How does the B-field structure change on core scales (<0.01 pc) compared to the clump scale (~0.1 pc) observed with HAWC+? ALMA polarimetry toward c1 is explicitly called for.
- Is G351 truly magnetically transcritical, or does it transition to supercritical when accounting for LOS averaging and beam dilution effects that systematically underestimate δθ (and therefore overestimate BPOS)?
- What role does stellar feedback from the active protocluster IRAS 17233-3606 play in the observed turbulence, and how does this contaminate the non-thermal velocity dispersion used in the DCF estimate?

### Follow-up Papers to Check
1. **Beuther et al. 2025** (DOI: 10.1051/0004-6361/202452754) — The paper directly cited for hierarchical accretion flow in G351; would reveal the small-scale gas kinematics and core properties needed to test the gravitational collapse scenario.
2. **Sandoval-Garrido et al. 2025** (DOI: 10.1051/0004-6361/202452589) — ALMA-IMF dense N₂H⁺ kinematics in G351.77; critical for understanding whether coherent infall motions exist toward c1 to test the magnetically regulated collapse hypothesis.
3. **Houde et al. 2009** (DOI: 10.1088/0004-637X/706/2/1504) — The paper that quantifies beam/LOS integration effects on DCF estimates; should be followed up to assess whether the Houde et al. correction would shift G351 from transcritical to clearly supercritical or clearly subcritical.
