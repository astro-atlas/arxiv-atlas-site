* **Title:** SN 2025adpq: A Type Ia supernova in a collisional ring formed during a major galaxy merger

* **Comments / status:** To be submitted to The Open Journal of Astrophysics
* **Abstract:** Galaxy mergers can both trigger star formation and rearrange where stars live, producing long-lived tidal structures and collisionally driven density waves (known as collisional rings) that can extend for tens of kpc from their host galaxy centers. Here we report the discovery of SN 2025adpq, a Type Ia supernova at z=0.1540, found within a collisional ring, which we call Pika's Halo, with circumference ~70 kpc that was produced by a major merger between two comparable mass galaxies (log(M_*/M_☉)≈10.5). The supernova lies along the ring at a projected offset of ~11.4 kpc from the nucleus of the primary galaxy (hereafter G1). Optical spectroscopy obtained with the Southern African Large Telescope (SALT) and Gemini South reveal signatures consistent with merger induced ongoing star formation, while prominent Calcium H and K absorption indicates a substantial old stellar population within the ring. Given the long delay times expected for most SN Ia progenitors, we argue that SN 2025adpq most likely arose from an old progenitor system that was displaced from G1 during the head-on encounter. The progenitor was likely stripped from its parent galaxy by the collisionally induced pressure wave and exploded far from its birthplace. This event highlights collisional rings as a pathway for producing large offset SNe Ia, and it motivates targeted searches for faint, dynamically displaced old populations in seemingly hostless SN Ia environments. We additionally identify other supernovae, including supernova siblings, in the low redshift sample of collisional ring galaxies, and find that SN 2025adpq is one of only a handful of classified supernova identified in the expanding ring of a collisional ring complex.

## Introduction

### Key claims
- Collisional ring galaxies (CRGs) form when collisionally driven density waves propagate through disk galaxies during near head-on encounters, producing expanding rings extending tens of kiloparsecs
- CRGs are rare (~1% of all rings) due to requirement of bullseye collision geometry, with strongest features arising in major mergers during brief merging phase (≲0.5 Gyr)
- Type Ia supernovae have progenitor delay times ≳0.5 Gyr, making them underrepresented in actively star-forming merger phases unless pre-existing stars are dynamically displaced
- SN 2025adpq is a Type Ia supernova at z=0.1540 discovered in collisional ring "Pika's Halo" (~70 kpc circumference) from major merger at ~11.4 kpc offset from primary galaxy
- Spectroscopy reveals both merger-driven ongoing star formation and old stellar population (Ca H/K absorption) in ring, suggesting SN originated from old progenitor system displaced during encounter

## Observations

### Key claims
- SN 2025adpq discovered 2025-11-14 with DECam at g-band magnitude 20.5 AB mag during gravitational wave follow-up (GW-MMADS survey) at apparent offset from nearby galaxy but on ring structure at z=0.1537±0.0002
- Seven epochs of DECam multifilter (griz) imaging obtained between 2025-11-14 and 2025-12-31 using GPU-enabled difference imaging pipeline (SFFT algorithm)
- SALT/RSS and Gemini/GMOS-S spectroscopy confirmed common redshift z~0.1537-0.1540 for transient, ring, and galaxies G1 and G2, rejecting lensing hypothesis
- Spectroscopy reveals narrow emission lines ([O II], [O III], Hβ, Hα) indicating ongoing star formation extending ~8.5" (~24 kpc) along ring, with velocity shifts ~120-150 km/s between regions
- Archival KiDS optical and VIKING near-infrared imaging provides best pre-explosion view of collisional ring structure (5σ limiting sensitivity: u=24.2, g=25.1, r=25.0, i=23.7 AB mag)

## Results and Analysis

### Key claims
- Confirmed collisional ring galaxy system from major merger with mass ratio L2/L1~0.8 (log(M*/M☉)≈10.5 for both galaxies), ring circumference ~70 kpc, SN offset ~11.4 kpc from G1, G1-G2 separation ~9.2 kpc projected (~10.2 kpc deprojected)
- Ring inclination ~26.5° inferred from elliptical shape (semi-major axis 10.5 kpc, semi-minor axis 9.4 kpc), coherent velocity structure with ~350 km/s maximum velocity shift along arc
- Spectroscopy shows old stellar population (deep Ca H and K absorption) coexisting with ongoing star formation (bright [O II], [O III], Hβ, Hα emission), SFR ~0.91±0.01 M☉/yr in ring, subsolar gas metallicity 12+log(O/H)~8.50±0.05 (~0.6 Z☉)
- SED modeling with prospector confirms both galaxies have log(M*/M☉)≈10.5, formed bulk of stars at early times (~11 Gyr ago), G1 has current SFR=0.41 M☉/yr vs G2 SFR=0.10 M☉/yr, both near-solar stellar metallicity
- SN 2025adpq classified as normal Type Ia (best match SN 2008C at 15.4 d post-peak), SALT2 lightcurve fit gives peak time 7.5±1.1 d before discovery, stretch x1=-0.81±0.31, color c=0.07±0.03

## Discussion

### Key claims
- Ring age estimated 70-200 Myr based on 10.5 kpc radius and expansion velocities 50-150 km/s, consistent with short CRG lifetime ≲0.5 Gyr from simulations and current G1-G2 separation implying similar relative velocity over ~100 Myr
- Velocity gradient ~350 km/s along ring arc spanning ~70° position angle plausible from combination of radial expansion, residual disk rotation, and projection effects at 26.5° inclination
- SN 2025adpq environment (11.4 kpc offset in star-forming ring) consistent with old Type Ia progenitor displaced from G1 by collisionally driven pressure wave, supported by Ca H/K absorption showing old population and G1 forming most stars ~11 Gyr ago
- Collisional rings provide pathway for large-offset Type Ia SNe by stripping long-lived progenitor systems from parent galaxies, allowing explosions far from birthplace without requiring young/short delay time progenitor channel
- SN 2025adpq adds to class of hostless/large-offset Type Ia SNe, motivating targeted searches for faint dynamically displaced old populations in seemingly hostless SN Ia environments

## Introduction

### Key claims
- Collisional ring galaxies result from near head-on encounters producing density waves that extend tens of kpc, offering unique laboratories for studying gravitational perturbations and stellar population displacement
- While historically identified at low z, modern surveys find ring morphologies at z>1-3, underscoring their importance for galaxy assembly studies
- CRGs are rare (~1% of rings) requiring bullseye collisions and differ from polar/resonance rings
- Strongest tidal features arise in major mergers during brief ≲0.5 Gyr merging phase with enhanced star formation
- Core-collapse SNe (lifetimes ≲0.1 Gyr) efficiently produced during mergers; Type Ia SNe (delay times ≳0.5 Gyr) underrepresented unless pre-existing stars displaced
- SN 2025adpq: Type Ia at z=0.1540 in collisional ring "Pika's Halo" from major merger (log(M*/M☉)≈10.5), offset 11.4 kpc from primary galaxy G1, likely from old displaced progenitor system

## Critical Analysis

### 1. Key Claims and Findings

1. **Collisional rings host dynamically displaced old stellar populations alongside newly triggered star formation.** The ring "Pika's Halo" shows deep Ca H/K absorption (old stars) coexisting with bright [O II], [O III], Hβ, Hα emission (ongoing SF at SFR ~0.91 M☉/yr). SED modeling confirms G1 formed the bulk of its stars ~11 Gyr ago (Leja et al. 2017; Park et al. 2024). This dual population is the paper's central evidence that the collisional pressure wave both triggered new star formation and swept out pre-existing disk stars.

2. **SN 2025adpq most likely originated from an old progenitor system displaced from G1 by the collisional encounter.** The SN is classified as a normal Type Ia (best match SN 2008C at 15.4 d post-peak; SALT2 stretch x1=−0.81±0.31, color c=0.07±0.03) at 11.4 kpc projected offset from G1. Given Type Ia delay times ≳0.5 Gyr (Totani et al. 2008; Maoz et al. 2012) and the ring age of only ~70–200 Myr (from expansion velocity 50–150 km/s over 10.5 kpc radius), the SN progenitor predates the ring and was likely stripped from G1 during the head-on collision.

3. **Collisional ring galaxies provide a new pathway for producing large-offset and apparently hostless Type Ia SNe.** The paper argues that dynamical displacement during galaxy collisions can relocate long-lived SN Ia progenitors to tens of kpc from their birthplace without requiring a young/short-delay-time progenitor channel. At higher redshifts, surface brightness dimming would render such low-SB ring structures undetectable, causing these SNe to be misclassified as hostless (Gal-Yam et al. 2003; Graham et al. 2015; Strolger et al. 2025).

4. **CRGs are "supernova factories" hosting multiple transients of various types.** A systematic crossmatch of the Madore et al. (2009) low-z CRG catalog with TNS reveals 15 transients within 30 kpc of 11 CRGs (8% of the sample), with 7 in rings and 8 not in rings. Three CRG systems host supernova siblings. NGC 4774 alone hosts 3 transients, all within its ring. SN 2025adpq is the 8th classified SN in a CRG ring and only the 2nd confirmed Type Ia (after SN 2025khv).

5. **The system is confirmed as a major merger producing a collisional ring, not a gravitational lens.** Spectroscopy at consistent z~0.1537–0.1540 for all components (SN, ring, G1, G2) rejects the lensing hypothesis. The luminosity ratio L2/L1~0.8 (Δmr~0.2 mag) confirms a major merger (Lambas et al. 2012). The ring geometry (semi-major axis 10.5 kpc, semi-minor axis 9.4 kpc, inclination ~26.5°) and coherent velocity structure (Δv~350 km/s across 70° arc) are consistent with an expanding collisional ring.

## Key Takeaways

### Main Results

- **SN 2025adpq is a normal Type Ia supernova at z=0.1540 discovered at 11.4 kpc offset from its host galaxy G1, located within a collisional ring ("Pika's Halo") produced by a major merger between two ~10^10.5 M☉ galaxies** (Hall et al. 2025; O'Connor et al. 2026).

- **Spectroscopy reveals a dual stellar population in the ring: deep Ca H/K absorption indicates old stars displaced from G1, while bright emission lines ([O II], Hα) show ongoing merger-triggered star formation at SFR ~0.91 M☉/yr with subsolar metallicity (~0.6 Z☉)** (Pettini & Pagel 2004; Kennicutt 1998).

- **The SN Ia most likely arose from an old progenitor (delay time ≳0.5 Gyr) stripped from G1 by the collisional pressure wave, given that the ring is only ~70–200 Myr old while G1 formed most of its stars ~11 Gyr ago** (Totani et al. 2008; Maoz et al. 2012; Leja et al. 2017).

- **Collisional rings provide a new pathway for producing large-offset and apparently hostless Type Ia SNe by dynamically displacing long-lived progenitor systems from their parent galaxies** (Gal-Yam et al. 2003; Graham et al. 2015; Strolger et al. 2025).

- **A systematic crossmatch of low-z CRG catalogs with TNS reveals CRGs as "supernova factories": 15 transients in 11 CRG systems, 3 hosting supernova siblings, with SN 2025adpq being only the 2nd classified Type Ia in a CRG ring** (Madore et al. 2009).

- **At higher redshifts, surface brightness dimming would render collisional rings undetectable, causing these offset SNe to be misclassified as hostless — a systematic bias that Rubin LSST will help resolve** (Ivezić et al. 2019).

### Implications

This paper establishes collisional ring galaxies as a previously unrecognized channel for producing large-offset Type Ia supernovae, with direct implications for galaxy evolution studies. The demonstration that galaxy-galaxy collisions can dynamically displace old stellar populations to tens of kpc from their birthplace challenges the assumption that SN Ia environments directly reflect their progenitor formation sites. For cosmological surveys using Type Ia SNe as standardizable candles, the systematic misclassification of CRG-offset SNe as "hostless" at higher redshifts could introduce biases in host-galaxy-dependent corrections. More broadly, the coexistence of old displaced stars and newly triggered star formation in collisional rings offers a unique laboratory for studying stellar population mixing, delay time distributions, and the interplay between gravitational dynamics and star formation during galaxy mergers.

### Other Works

This paper builds on the theoretical framework of collisional ring formation (Lynds & Toomre 1976; Appleton & Struck-Marcell 1996) and extends earlier observations of Type Ia SNe in tidal structures (Schweizer et al. 2008; Ferretti et al. 2017) to the specific context of collisionally-driven density waves. The proposed dynamical displacement mechanism connects to the broader "hostless SN Ia" problem (Gal-Yam et al. 2003; Sand et al. 2011; Graham et al. 2015; Qin et al. 2024), offering collisional rings as a complementary explanation alongside intracluster stars and ultra-diffuse dwarf hosts (Strolger et al. 2025). The finding that CRGs are supernova factories with enhanced transient rates aligns with studies showing elevated core-collapse SN rates in interacting galaxies (Habergham et al. 2010, 2012; Michałowski et al. 2020) and extends this to include redistributed Type Ia progenitors — an effect recently modeled by Ditrani et al. (2024). The paper highlights a gap in current surveys: at z>0.15, most CRG rings fall below detection thresholds, motivating next-generation surveys like Rubin LSST to resolve this population.
