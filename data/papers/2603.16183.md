# 2603.16183 — Evolution of fractality in centrally concentrated young clusters

## Metadata

- **Title**: Evolution of fractality in centrally concentrated young clusters
- **Authors**: Almat Akhmetali, Adilkhan Assilkhan, Mordecai-Mark Mac Low, Nurzhan Ussipov, Marat Zaidyn, Ernazar Abdikamalov, Alison Sills, Xiaoying Pang, Bekdaulet Shukirgaliyev
- **arXiv ID**: 2603.16183
- **DOI**: 10.48550/arXiv.2603.16183
- **Submitted**: 2026-03-17
- **Comments**: Submitted to A&A
- **Abstract**: We investigate the structural evolution of young star clusters forming within centrally concentrated molecular clouds. Our simulations use the Torch framework, which integrates the FLASH magnetohydrodynamics code with the AMUSE environment, enabling a self-consistent treatment of gas dynamics, star formation, stellar evolution, radiative transfer, and gravitational interactions. We quantify cluster structure using the Q parameter for fractality and compute fractal dimensions via two methods: box-counting and correlation dimension. Our results show that clusters generally inherit fractal substructure from their parental clouds, which is typically erased within ~2.5 t_ff through dynamical relaxation. Massive stars can induce the formation of secondary subclusters via feedback, with outcomes strongly dependent on stellar mass and formation timing. Interactions among subclusters, including mergers and dispersal, can extend fractal structure beyond 4 t_ff. We also find systematic correlations between the fractality parameter Q and the fractal dimension: fractality is positively correlated with both the correlation and box-counting dimensions, with the correlation dimension exhibiting a stronger correlation. These results demonstrate how stellar feedback and internal dynamics jointly shape the measurable fractal properties of embedded star clusters.

## Key Takeaways

### Main Results
- Young star clusters inherit fractal substructure from their parental molecular clouds, as measured by the Q parameter (Cartwright & Whitworth 2004).
- This fractal substructure is erased by dynamical relaxation at approximately 2.5 free-fall times (t_ff), independent of simulation resolution and initial fractality, consistent with Laverde-Villarreal et al. (2025) across an order-of-magnitude range in cloud mass.
- Massive star feedback can trigger secondary subcluster formation by expelling gas that sweeps up a dense shell; however, this outcome is not universal — it depends on stellar mass and timing.
- Subcluster interactions (merging, dispersal, re-fragmentation) can sustain measurable fractality beyond 4 t_ff, extending the fractal lifetime significantly in some environments.
- The Q parameter shows weak positive correlation with the box-counting fractal dimension and moderate positive correlation with the correlation dimension; the correlation dimension is more sensitive to local stellar clustering.

### Implications
These results provide a quantitative timeline for the transformation of embedded star clusters from fractal, hierarchical configurations into smooth, centrally concentrated systems. The ~2.5 t_ff timescale for fractal erasure appears robust across cloud masses and resolutions, suggesting that global free-fall dynamics — rather than local initial conditions or feedback details — govern the structural evolution. For galaxy evolution, this means that the observable morphology of young clusters is strongly time-dependent, and surveys of embedded clusters at different ages sample physically distinct evolutionary states. Massive star feedback introduces stochasticity into this picture, sometimes prolonging hierarchical structure well beyond the typical erasure timescale.

### Other Works
This paper builds directly on the simulation suite of Assilkhan et al. (2026, A&A 705, A79), who established the centrally concentrated star formation model, and extends it by adding fractal structure diagnostics. The ~2.5 t_ff erasure timescale is consistent with Laverde-Villarreal et al. (2025, ApJ 989, 22) despite a factor of ~10 difference in cloud mass, suggesting universality. In contrast, Ballone et al. (2020) found clusters remaining fractal after 2 t_ff in their more massive (10^4–10^5 M_sun) simulations, possibly reflecting different density profiles. Earlier SPH-based work by Schmeja & Klessen (2006) and Maschberger et al. (2010) reported similar early-low-Q behavior but lacked the full stellar feedback physics of Torch. The paper also reconciles a tension with Sanchez & Alfaro (2009), who found strong Q–f_dim correlations in artificial clusters, by showing that real simulation data yields weaker (but positive) correlations.

## Section Analysis

### Introduction
- Key claim: clusters inherit fractal structure from GMCs through gravoturbulent collapse (Vazquez-Semadeni+ 2017, Grudic+ 2018, Mac Low & Klessen 2004)
- Key claim: some clusters (ρ Oph) show central concentration as early as 1 Myr; others retain substructure >100 Myr (NGC 1513, NGC 1641; Sanchez+ 2009)
- Key claim: previous work (Schmeja & Klessen 2006, Maschberger+ 2010) used simpler physics; this paper adds full MHD + stellar feedback
- Cites Laverde-Villarreal+2025 as the nearest comparable work in timescale

### Methods
- Simulations from Assilkhan+2025: 13 runs (n3s1–10, n4s1, n5s1, n6s1)
- Initial gas mass: 2513 M_sun, radius 5.5 pc, T=20K, virial parameter α_vir=0.5, B_z=3×10^-6 G
- Torch framework: FLASH (MHD) + AMUSE (N-body + stellar evolution with SeBa, ph4 integrator)
- Q parameter (Cartwright & Whitworth 2004): Q = m_bar/s_bar; Q < 0.8 (2D) or < 0.7 (3D) → fractal
- Two fractal dimension methods: box-counting (Minkowski-Bouligand) and correlation dimension (Grassberger 1983)

### Results — Subcluster formation
- Three models (n3s6, n3s7, n4s1) show multi-subcluster formation
- Two mechanisms: (1) massive star + feedback → gas expulsion → shell → second subcluster; (2) two subclusters form in proximity, merge, then re-fragment

### Results — Evolution of fractality
- Most models: Q exceeds fractality threshold at ~2.5 t_ff, regardless of resolution
- Three models (n3s6, n3s7, n4s1): Q stays below threshold past 4 t_ff due to persistent subcluster activity
- At 1–2 t_ff: 3D distributions show fractal f_dim ~ 2 and 1.6; 2D projections consistent with radial profiles α ~ -2.5 to -2
- By 3–4 t_ff: both 2D and 3D converge toward radial profiles with α ~ -2.9

### Results — Evolution of fractal dimension
- n3s* models: correlation dimension 3D reaches plateau ~2.4; box-counting ~1.8–1.9; both 2D methods ~1.5–1.6
- High-resolution n5s1, n6s1: box-counting ~1.3–1.4; correlation dimension exceeds 2.5 in 3D (due to dense core sensitivity)
- f_dim and Q track each other well during subcluster merging/re-fragmentation events

### Results — Q vs f_dim correlations
- Spearman ρ for Q vs. box-counting: weak positive (stable across N_thr)
- Spearman ρ for Q vs. correlation dimension: moderate positive (stronger correlation)
- Correlation dimension more sensitive to local clustering than box-counting's global measure
- Tension with Sanchez & Alfaro (2009) who found strong correlation in artificial clusters

## Critical Analysis

### 1. Key Claims and Findings
1. **Fractal erasure at ~2.5 t_ff**: The most important result. Supported by 9 of 13 simulations across 4 resolution levels. The uniformity across resolutions strengthens this claim. However, the paper uses a single global density profile (Shukirgaliyev 2017 / Parmentier 2013), limiting generalizability.
2. **Feedback-triggered subclustering is stochastic**: Only 3/13 models show persistent fractality via subcluster formation. The dependence on when and where a massive star forms is a genuine physical result, but with small-number statistics (n=3 prolonged-fractal cases).
3. **~2.5 t_ff universality**: The agreement with Laverde-Villarreal+2025 (clouds 10× more massive) is striking and suggestive of a universal timescale. But the paper correctly notes that different density profiles and mean densities need to be explored.
4. **Q–f_dim correlation weakness**: The Spearman ρ values are moderate at best, contrasting with Sanchez & Alfaro (2009). The difference is likely due to the simulated clusters being real physical systems rather than artificial fractal constructions — a valid distinction.
5. **Correlation dimension sensitivity**: The finding that correlation dimension is more sensitive to central concentration than box-counting is physically well-motivated and practically useful for observers.

### 2. Papers/Concepts This Paper Supports
- Laverde-Villarreal+2025: corroborates their ~2.5 t_ff timescale
- Cartwright & Whitworth 2004 Q parameter framework: used and validated
- Mac Low & Klessen 2004, McKee & Ostriker 2007: gravoturbulent collapse → hierarchical structure
- Assilkhan+2025 (companion paper): builds on and extends their simulation suite

### 3. Papers/Concepts This Paper Challenges/Tensions
- Ballone+2020: found clusters fractal after 2 t_ff in massive (10^4–10^5 M_sun) clouds — possibly different initial conditions
- Sanchez & Alfaro 2009: strong Q–f_dim correlation in artificial clusters not reproduced in physical simulations
- Parker & Dale 2015, Gavagnin+2017: showed little feedback effect on Q — this paper shows feedback CAN matter, but stochastically
